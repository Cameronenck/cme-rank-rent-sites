What Drives Up the Cost

The floor coating itself is only part of the equation. Here's where jobs get more expensive, and what I tell every business owner before we sign a contract:

Surface prep is the biggest variable. If your concrete slab has been exposed to oil, hydraulic fluid, or chemical spills, we have to deal with that before any coating goes down. A badly contaminated floor might need multiple degreasing passes, grinding, and a moisture-barrier primer before we can apply epoxy. That adds time and material cost. On a clean, dry slab, prep is fast. On a 20-year-old shop floor that's been dripping oil for two decades, prep can double the labor cost.

Crack and joint repair. Commercial slabs move. Control joints open up. Cracks that look minor can telegraph through a coating if they're not filled properly. We typically fill cracks with semi-rigid epoxy filler, which flexes with the slab rather than cracking straight through the topcoat. This adds cost, but skipping it leads to premature failure.

Moisture. Peoria's clay-heavy soil and our freeze-thaw cycles mean concrete slabs in older buildings often have moisture vapor transmission issues. We test before we coat. If moisture is above acceptable levels, we apply a moisture-mitigation primer — that's an additional cost, but it's what prevents delamination six months after install.

Square footage. Larger jobs cost less per square foot because setup, mobilization, and material handling is spread across more area. A 500-square-foot auto bay costs more per foot than a 5,000-square-foot warehouse coating.

Commercial vs. Residential Epoxy — What's Different

I do both residential garages and commercial floors, and there are real differences in what we install and why. Commercial systems use thicker builds — often 20 to 30 mils or more — compared to a typical residential 2-car garage system that might be 10 to 15 mils. Commercial topcoats are typically harder, more abrasion-resistant, and formulated to handle the specific chemical exposure of your industry. A system I'd put in a Peoria restaurant kitchen is a completely different product from what I put in a retail showroom, even if they look similar.

If your business involves chemicals, heavy equipment, frequent washdowns, or food handling, tell me upfront. The right system for your specific use case saves you money long-term even if it costs more today. A topcoat that's not rated for the chemicals in your facility will fail in six months. The right one will last a decade.

How Long Does Commercial Epoxy Last?

In Peoria's climate — which includes cold winters, humid summers, and a lot of industrial use — a well-installed commercial epoxy system holds up for 10 to 20 years in moderate-traffic environments. Forklift-heavy operations or areas with constant chemical exposure will see more wear, and a topcoat refresh every 5 to 7 years is realistic. The base epoxy layers almost always outlast the topcoat, so often a recoat is all you need rather than a full replacement.

The coating systems we use are designed to be maintained, not replaced. We'll walk you through a cleaning protocol that keeps the floor looking sharp and extends its life. Most commercial epoxy floors I've installed around Peoria are still going strong years later with simple maintenance.

Is Commercial Epoxy Flooring Worth It?

Compared to alternatives — VCT tile, polished concrete, or painted floors — commercial epoxy delivers the lowest total cost of ownership for most Peoria businesses. It's one surface that handles chemical resistance, cleanability, load capacity, and appearance all at once. A well-coated floor reduces cleaning time, improves lighting (the reflective surface bounces light around the space), protects your concrete from damage, and makes a much better first impression on customers or inspectors.

If you're comparing epoxy to polyurea coatings, the main tradeoff is cost and cure time. Polyurea is faster to cure and slightly more flexible, but the premium pricing doesn't make sense for every application. For most commercial spaces in Peoria, a quality epoxy system with a polyurethane or polyaspartic topcoat hits the right balance.
