If your garage floor is starting to look beat up — flaking, pitting, or covered in hairline cracks — you're probably weighing your options. Garage floor resurfacing is a cost-effective middle ground between living with a damaged slab and committing to a full tear-out. But how much does it cost in Peoria, IL, and is it the right move for your floor?

This guide breaks down local pricing, explains when resurfacing makes sense versus full replacement, and tells you the truth about those box-store DIY kits. Resurfacing is not the same as a full epoxy install. A resurface job focuses on repairing the existing concrete — grinding down high spots, patching spalls and cracks, and applying a thin polymer or concrete overlay that restores the surface. A full epoxy installation starts with surface prep and then builds up chemical-bonded layers of epoxy for a seamless, high-performance floor. Knowing the difference saves you from paying for the wrong service.

When Does a Garage Floor Need Resurfacing vs. Replacement?

This is the question every Peoria homeowner asks. The short answer: most floors don't need to be replaced — they need to be properly prepped and coated. But there are limits.

Signs You Can Resurface

Surface spalling (the top layer of concrete flaking off like a bad sunburn) is almost always a resurfacing candidate. Same with delamination — when a prior coating or overlay peels away from the concrete. Hairline cracks that don't go all the way through the slab are cosmetic and can be filled before resurfacing.

Signs You May Need Replacement

Structural cracks are a different story. If cracks run edge-to-edge, are wider than ¼ inch, or show vertical displacement (one side higher than the other), that indicates movement in the sub-base. Resurfacing over a structurally compromised slab is a waste of money — the overlay will crack again within a season. A contractor doing honest work will tell you this up front. We do.

Other red flags: significant heaving from tree roots or frost, active water infiltration from below, or a floor that "gives" when walked on. These conditions require more than a coating.

DIY Resurfacing Kits vs. Professional in Peoria IL

Walk into any Menards or Home Depot in the Peoria area and you'll find resurfacing kits for $50–$150. They look like a deal. In practice, most of them fail within one to two Illinois winters — and here's why.

The problem isn't the product itself; it's the application conditions and surface prep. Proper concrete resurfacing requires mechanical grinding to open the surface and ensure adhesion. Box-store kits rely on acid etching or wire brushing, which rarely achieves the profile needed for a durable bond in our freeze-thaw climate. When water gets under a poorly bonded overlay and freezes, it expands and pops the whole thing off.

Professional contractors use diamond-grinding equipment, moisture meters, and professional-grade polymer overlays engineered for temperature swings. The materials alone are in a different class. A job done right the first time costs more upfront but doesn't have to be redone every two years.

If you've already tried a DIY kit and it failed, that's actually a very common situation we see in Peoria. The failed overlay needs to be ground off completely before any new system can be applied — which adds to the prep cost but is non-negotiable for a lasting result.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does garage floor resurfacing cost in Peoria, IL?

For a standard two-car garage, expect to pay $800–$1,800 for full concrete resurfacing by a professional. Minor crack repair runs $200–$400. If you want an epoxy system applied over the freshly resurfaced floor, the combined project typically runs $1,500–$3,500 depending on floor size and condition.

What causes garage floor spalling in Peoria?

Spalling is almost always caused by freeze-thaw cycles combined with deicing salts. Water gets into the porous top layer of concrete, freezes, and expands — breaking the surface apart. Peoria's winters are especially hard on concrete because we get repeated freeze-thaw events each season. Poor original concrete mix design or inadequate curing at the time of the pour can accelerate this damage.

Can you apply epoxy over a resurfaced concrete floor?

Yes — and it's actually a great combination. Once the concrete has been resurfaced and fully cured (typically 24–48 hours minimum), an epoxy system can be applied on top. The resurface layer provides a fresh, stable substrate, and the epoxy adds chemical resistance, a decorative finish, and a much easier-to-clean surface. We do this type of two-stage project regularly in Peoria.
