How to Maintain Your Epoxy Garage Floor in Peoria IL

Updated May 2026 • 7 min read • By Peoria Epoxy Flooring Experts

After we finish an epoxy job, the question I get asked most before we pack up is: "So what do I actually have to do to keep this looking good?" And my honest answer is — not much. That's one of the reasons epoxy is worth the investment. But "not much" doesn't mean nothing. A few simple habits will keep a Peoria garage floor looking sharp for 15–20 years. Neglect a couple of them and you'll be dealing with issues a lot sooner than you should.

Here's exactly what we tell our customers after every install.

Start Right: The First 72 Hours Matter

The first thing to understand is that your floor needs time to fully cure before you put it through its paces. When we wrap up a job, we tell customers to stay off the floor for 24 hours and keep vehicles off it for at least 72 hours — ideally a full week if it's a cold snap. Polyaspartic topcoats cure faster than straight epoxy, but temperature still matters. Our central Illinois winters mean colder garage temps slow the cure process. If you park on a floor that's still slightly soft, you risk tire impressions that are permanent.

Pro tip: If we install in November or December, we recommend waiting 5–7 full days before parking. Temps in the 30s and 40s slow cure time significantly. In summer, 72 hours is usually plenty.

Regular Cleaning: Keep It Simple

An epoxy floor does not need special equipment or expensive cleaners to stay clean. Here's what actually works:

  • Weekly sweep or dust mop. Sand, grit, and small rocks tracked in off the driveway are your floor's biggest enemy over time. They act like sandpaper underfoot and can dull the topcoat finish if you let them grind in. A quick sweep takes two minutes and prevents years of wear.
  • Monthly damp mop. Mix a small amount of Simple Green or any pH-neutral floor cleaner in a bucket of warm water. Mop the floor and rinse with clean water. That's it. No scrubbing, no elbow grease required.
  • Spot clean spills immediately. Oil, brake fluid, and antifreeze can stain if they sit for extended periods — especially on chip finishes where liquids can seep into the texture. Wipe them up when you notice them. A paper towel and a quick rinse is all it takes.

The cleaners I see cause the most problems are the ones that seem logical but aren't — bleach, ammonia-based cleaners, citrus degreasers, and steel wool pads. All of those will degrade the topcoat over time. If your floor has an oil stain that's been there a while, a diluted degreaser rated safe for sealed surfaces works fine. Just rinse thoroughly afterward.

Handling Peoria Winters

This is the one that catches a lot of homeowners off guard. Illinois winters are rough on garage floors. Road salt tracked in off the driveway is the main culprit — it's mildly corrosive and, if left sitting, can work at the edges of your coating over time, especially around expansion joints and floor drains.

What we recommend from November through March:

  • Rinse the floor down with a garden hose or bucket of water after a heavy salt event. You don't need to scrub — just flush the salt off before it dries and gets ground in.
  • Keep a floor mat at the garage entry to catch some of the wet and grit before it spreads.
  • If you're parking a vehicle that came through a heavy brine treatment, rinsing the floor the same day is worth it.

The good news: a polyaspartic topcoat handles salt and chemical exposure significantly better than bare concrete or a basic water-based epoxy. You're not dealing with a fragile surface — just one that lasts longer with a little attention during the winter months.

Watch for Edge Chips and Separation

The most common damage I see on floors that are a few years old starts at the edges — along walls, floor drains, and doorway transitions. This happens for a few reasons: foot traffic at the edges, water infiltration from outside, or moisture that found its way under the coating. If you notice a chip or a small section starting to lift, deal with it early. A minor edge repair takes twenty minutes and costs almost nothing. A delaminated 10-square-foot section that's been wet underneath for a year is a much bigger job.

Watch for this: If you see any bubbling, lifting, or a section that sounds hollow when you tap it, that usually means moisture got underneath. Don't wait on it. Call us at 309-487-2138 — catching it early makes the repair simple.

Protecting Against Heavy Use

Most residential garage floors don't take a huge beating. But if you're doing heavy mechanical work, using your garage as a workshop, or rolling heavy equipment in and out regularly, a couple of extra precautions help:

  • Use rubber or foam mats under jack stands and heavy equipment. Point loads from metal feet can chip the coating if they're dropped or dragged. A rubber mat distributes the weight.
  • Drop cloths for big jobs. If you're doing a brake job or an engine swap, lay down some cardboard or a drop cloth in your work area. Fluid spills during a job are harder to catch in real time.
  • Felt pads on metal shelving feet. Dragging metal shelving across epoxy will eventually scratch the surface. A few stick-on felt pads are a cheap fix.

Annual Check-In

Once a year — spring is a good time after the salt season — take a walk around your garage floor and look it over carefully. Check the edges along walls, the area around the drain, and any spots where water tends to pool. Look for dullness or haziness in sections that get the most sunlight. If the topcoat is doing its job, none of this should be alarming — but catching anything early means an easy fix rather than a major repair.

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should I clean my epoxy garage floor?

A quick sweep once a week and a full mop once a month is enough for most Peoria homeowners. If you drive in with road salt or mud during winter, rinse it off sooner. Salt sitting on epoxy overnight won't ruin it, but it doesn't help long-term either.

What cleaners can I use on an epoxy garage floor?

Stick to pH-neutral floor cleaners — Simple Green diluted in water is a go-to for us. Avoid bleach, ammonia, citrus-based cleaners, and anything that says "degreaser" unless it's specifically rated safe for epoxy. Harsh chemicals can dull the topcoat over time.

Will hot tire pickup damage my epoxy floor?

Hot tire pickup is a real problem with lower-quality water-based epoxy systems, which is exactly why we use 100% solids epoxy with a polyaspartic topcoat. Polyaspartic has a much higher heat resistance than standard epoxy. That said, letting a hot car sit with the tires in the same spot repeatedly over many years can eventually cause marking — parking mats are cheap insurance if you're concerned.

Can I use a pressure washer on my epoxy garage floor?

Yes — a standard garden hose or pressure washer on a low setting is perfectly fine for rinsing off winter grime, salt, or mud. Keep the pressure under 1,500 PSI and don't hold the nozzle 3 inches from the floor. A wide-angle tip from a normal distance cleans great without risking any edge lifting.

How do I repair small chips or scratches in my epoxy floor?

Small chips along edges or around floor drains are the most common damage we see. If you catch them early, a touch-up with matching epoxy filler is straightforward. Ignoring chips lets water get under the coating, which causes delamination. If you notice a chip, give us a call at 309-487-2138 — we can usually do small touch-ups quickly.

How long does a professional epoxy garage floor last with proper maintenance?

A properly installed 100% solids epoxy with a polyaspartic topcoat — installed by a professional who did the prep right — should last 15 to 20 years with normal maintenance. The floors that fail in 3–5 years almost always had prep problems, used water-based products, or skipped the topcoat.
