What the Installation Process Actually Looks Like

Here's what happens when we show up for a job in Peoria Heights:

Day one, first thing: We grind the entire slab with diamond tooling. This is the non-negotiable step that separates a job that lasts from one that peels in two years. Grinding opens the pores of the concrete so the epoxy has something to bite into. We also grind off any existing paint, sealer, or previous coating at this stage.

Crack and joint repair: Any structural cracks get filled with a rigid epoxy filler. We feather and profile them so they don't read through the finished surface. Control joints get treated depending on what the homeowner wants — some people want them filled, others prefer to leave them visible.

Primer coat: We roll on a penetrating epoxy primer that soaks into the open concrete. This is what locks the system to the slab.

Base coat and broadcast: If you're going with a chip system — which most Peoria Heights homeowners choose — we roll on the color base coat and immediately broadcast vinyl flake chips into it. The density of the broadcast determines the look: a full broadcast (100% coverage) gives you a terrazzo-style appearance and is the most durable because it protects the base coat underneath. Partial broadcasts show more of the base color and can be tuned to your preference.

Topcoat: The final layer is where we lock everything in. We use a polyaspartic topcoat that UV-stable, hard, and resistant to abrasion, chemicals, and the hot tire contact that kills cheaper systems. Polyaspartic cures faster than traditional epoxy topcoats — usually walkable in a few hours — which is a nice bonus for homeowners who want their garage back quickly.

Total time for a standard two-car garage: one day. You're walking on it the next morning. Vehicles go back in after three to five days.
