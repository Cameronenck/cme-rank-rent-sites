Epoxy Floor Repair in Peoria IL

Updated June 2026 • 8 min read • By Peoria Epoxy Flooring Experts

I get calls about epoxy floor repairs all the time. Someone notices a chip along the edge of their garage. Or water starts seeping in from outside and lifts a section of the coating. Or they spot what looks like a hairline crack spreading across the floor. And the first question is always the same: "Is it ruined? How much is this going to cost?"

The good news is that most epoxy damage is fixable. The key is catching it early and understanding what caused it in the first place. Here's what I see most often, why it happens, and how we fix it.

The Most Common Repairs We Do

Not all epoxy damage is created equal. Let me walk you through what I find most in a Peoria garage.

Edge Chips and Cracks

This is number one. A chip appears along a wall, around a floor drain, or at a doorway transition. Usually it happens from foot traffic at the edges, or sometimes from something getting dropped or dragged across the floor. If you catch a chip early, the concrete underneath is still dry and sealed. We can fill it with color-matched epoxy filler, sand it smooth, and you're done. Takes an hour, costs between $150–300 depending on size.

But here's the thing: if you leave a chip sitting for weeks or months, water and moisture can work their way under the coating around the damaged spot. Then you're not just dealing with a chip anymore. You're dealing with delamination.

Delamination and Peeling

Delamination is what it sounds like — the epoxy coating separates from the concrete slab underneath. You'll see it as a raised section or bubbling in the floor, and when you tap it, it sounds hollow. The causes are almost always one of three things: moisture got under the coating, the original prep before installation was rushed or incomplete, or the product used didn't have the right adhesion strength for the substrate.

We see this most often along edges and where water tends to pool. It's also the reason I always tell customers to deal with chips right away — a small chip ignored becomes a delamination repair, and that's a much bigger job.

Fixing delamination means we have to strip out the failed section, verify the concrete is actually dry (not just looks dry), re-prep properly, and apply new epoxy. That's why it costs more — $800 and up depending on square footage. But once it's done right, it should last as long as the rest of your floor.

Surface Scratches and Dull Spots

Heavy use — dragging tools, parking cars in the same spot for years, using your garage as a workshop — gradually dulls the topcoat. This isn't structural damage. It's cosmetic. But if you're particular about how your floor looks, a localized re-coat of the topcoat in that section brings the shine back. It's less intensive than a full repair and usually costs between $400–700 depending on the area size.

Why Epoxy Fails — And How to Prevent It

Understanding what causes damage helps you avoid needing repairs in the first place.

Moisture is the enemy. Epoxy is only as strong as its bond to the concrete. If moisture gets between them, that bond fails. In Peoria, this happens most on floors where the concrete has high moisture content before installation. It also happens over time when water is allowed to pool or when edges aren't sealed properly. The fix: make sure your installer does a proper moisture test on the concrete before starting. And don't ignore pooling water — address drainage issues outside the garage if water is finding its way in.

Poor prep before installation. I've seen too many epoxy failures traced back to the original contractor skipping steps. Not grinding the concrete to profile, not removing all sealers or coatings, not filling cracks beforehand — all of that shows up as failed adhesion within a few years. If your floor was installed a while ago and is having issues, this is often why. There's no way around it once it's down, but it's a reason to get a real contractor when you do your repair.

Wrong product for the job. Water-based epoxy is cheaper but way less durable. 100% solids epoxy with a polyaspartic topcoat is what we use for every repair because it holds up. If your original floor was cheap water-based product, chips and wear are going to happen faster. When we repair, we upgrade to quality materials so you don't have the same problem in five years.

Red flag: If your floor is still under warranty and starting to fail, your original installer should stand behind their work. Don't accept "this is normal wear" if the failure is clearly caused by prep or material issues. Hold them accountable or get a second opinion from another contractor.

When to Repair vs. Replace

Not every damaged spot means you need a full re-do. Here's how I think about it:

Repair the floor if: You have isolated damage (a chip, a small delaminated section, edge peeling). The rest of the floor is in good shape. The damage is from age or a specific incident, not a systemic prep or moisture problem.

Replace the floor if: You have widespread delamination across multiple areas. The floor is failing in multiple spots consistently. You know the original install was done poorly or with cheap materials. Moisture is a chronic problem and you've fixed the root cause but the epoxy is still failing.

If you've got damage and you're not sure which bucket you're in, call us. We'll walk your floor and give you an honest assessment of whether a repair is going to hold up or whether a full re-coat makes more sense long-term.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can you repair a small chip in an epoxy garage floor?

Absolutely. Small chips along edges, around floor drains, or from dropped tools are the most common repairs we do. If we catch them within the first few months, a spot repair with matching epoxy compound takes an hour and costs between $150–300. The longer a chip sits exposed, the more likely water gets underneath and causes delamination — that's when it becomes a much bigger job.

How much does epoxy floor repair cost in Peoria?

It depends on the damage. Small chip repairs run $150–400. Edge re-coating (10–15 linear feet) is usually $300–600. Larger delamination repairs where we have to strip, prep, and re-coat sections start at $800 and go up from there. For a full assessment and quote, call us at 309-487-2138 — we'll tell you exactly what you're looking at.

What causes epoxy floor delamination and can it be fixed?

Delamination happens when moisture gets underneath the coating and the epoxy loses adhesion to the concrete slab. Common causes are water seeping in from outside, poor prep before the original install, or long-term salt exposure on edges. Yes, it can be fixed — we strip the delaminated section, verify the concrete is dry, re-prep, and apply new epoxy. It's more work than a chip repair, but it's a permanent fix if done right.

Can I DIY a small epoxy floor repair?

For tiny surface scratches, a color-matched epoxy touch-up kit can work. But anything deeper than that — a real chip where you can see the concrete underneath — needs professional attention. The issue isn't just filling the hole; it's making sure moisture can't get under the coating afterward. A DIY repair that looks fine for six months can separate and fail after a year. That's why we recommend calling a pro.

How long do epoxy floor repairs last?

A professional repair that addresses the root cause of the damage — fixing the moisture issue, re-prepping the surface, using the right materials — should last as long as the rest of the floor. That's 15–20 years for a properly installed system. If we repair a chip but don't fix the underlying moisture problem, you might see the same spot fail again in a couple years.

For small repairs and assessments, we try to fit emergency calls into our schedule same-day or next-day.
