Epoxy Flooring Installation in Peoria IL — Complete Process & Timeline

Published: June 11, 2026 Reading time: 6 min

When we installed our first residential epoxy floor back in 2015, we didn't realize how much the process itself would become one of our biggest selling points. Today, after completing over 300 projects across Peoria and the surrounding areas, I want to walk you through exactly what happens during an epoxy flooring installation — step by step. This is the real process we use, not the glossed-over version.

The Pre-Installation Phase: Getting It Right the First Time

Before our crew shows up with equipment, we spend time understanding your concrete. This isn't optional. I've seen too many contractors skip this, and the results are always disappointing.

Here's what we do:

  • Moisture testing — We use calcium chloride and relative humidity testing to ensure your concrete isn't holding too much moisture. Epoxy won't bond to damp concrete, period.
  • Surface assessment — We check for cracks, spalling, oil stains, and previous coatings. Each issue requires a different prep strategy.
  • Timeline planning — We review your schedule and make sure you understand that your floor will be out of commission for 5-7 days minimum while curing.

Most people think installation is 1-2 days. That's only the active work. The real timeline includes surface prep, multiple cure periods, and proper waiting time between coats. Rushing this is how you end up with floors that peel or fail.

Day 1 & 2: Surface Preparation

This is where 60% of the success happens, and nobody wants to talk about it.

We grind the concrete surface to open the pores and remove the top layer. For most Peoria garages and basements, we use 150-grit diamond grinding to expose clean, bare concrete. If there's old paint or sealer, we go more aggressive — sometimes to 80-grit to strip it completely.

After grinding, we vacuum thoroughly and then mop with an etching solution. This removes all dust and further opens the concrete's porosity so the epoxy base coat can really grip. A lot of installers skip this step "to save time." We don't. It's the difference between a floor that lasts 5 years and one that lasts 15.

Finally, we seal any cracks with epoxy filler and let everything cure for 24 hours before we apply the first coat.

Day 3: Primer & Base Coat Application

On day 3, we apply a moisture-barrier primer, then two coats of your chosen epoxy finish. We use a roller for large areas and a brush for edges to ensure even coverage.

The base coat is where color goes in. Whether you want solid gray, metallic effects, or flaked designs, this is where it happens. Two coats at 8-12 mils dry film thickness per coat. That thickness is critical — too thin and you won't get durability; too thick and you risk runs and uneven curing.

Curing time: 24 hours before we can walk on it (light foot traffic only), 48-72 hours before it's ready for normal use.

Optional Day 4: Top Coat & Sealant

For premium finishes, we apply a polyurethane or polyaspartic top coat. This adds another layer of protection against UV damage, chemical spills, and wear. Most commercial spaces and high-traffic garages benefit from this.

The top coat is applied after the base epoxy has fully cured (usually 48 hours after the base coat). This adds 2-3 more days to the timeline, but the durability increase is significant.

Curing & Site Closure

After installation, the floor needs 5-7 days of curing before it's truly ready for heavy use. Here's what that means:

  • Days 1-3: No foot traffic except light steps
  • Days 3-5: Light foot traffic is okay, but no vehicles or rolling equipment
  • Day 7+: Full use — parking, equipment, wheelchairs, whatever your application requires

Temperature and humidity affect this timeline. If it's cold or very humid, add 1-2 days. In Peoria's climate, we typically see optimal conditions in spring and fall.

Why the Timeline Matters

I know waiting 5-7 days is frustrating when you want your floor done "this weekend." But rushing the cure is how you end up with a floor that fails in 2 years instead of lasting 10. We'd rather lose a job than rush it. Your floor's durability is our reputation.

What to Expect During Installation

Our crew shows up early, sets up containment, and works systematically. You'll hear grinding and see dust on day 1-2 (that's normal). Days 3-4 are quieter — just rolling and brushing. The smell is noticeable but not overwhelming if the space is ventilated.

We'll ask you not to use the space during work, but we coordinate around your schedule. Most of our Peoria clients take 5-7 days off from using the garage or closed off the space and worked around it.

Common Questions About Installation Timeline

Can you rush the installation timeline? ▼
Not without sacrificing quality. The science of epoxy curing is non-negotiable. If someone promises to have your floor ready in 3 days start-to-finish, they're either skipping steps or using inferior products. We're committed to doing it right.
What happens if it rains during installation? ▼
Rain won't harm exterior prep work, but it does delay timeline because we need dry conditions to apply epoxy coatings. Interior work is done in climate-controlled spaces, so weather isn't a factor there.
How do you prepare concrete that's already damaged? ▼
Cracks up to 1/4" wide get filled with epoxy filler. Larger cracks or spalling areas may need concrete repair or patching before epoxy application. We assess this during the pre-install visit.
Can I enter the space during curing? ▼
Light foot traffic is okay after 48-72 hours, but we recommend staying off the floor as much as possible for the full 5-7 day cure period. No rolling equipment, no vehicles, no heavy loads during the first week.
How do outdoor temperature and humidity affect installation? ▼
Epoxy cures best in moderate temps (60-85 degrees) and moderate humidity (40-60% RH). Cold or very humid conditions slow curing significantly. In Peoria, spring and fall are ideal. Summer heat isn't a problem; winter cold is our main challenge.
