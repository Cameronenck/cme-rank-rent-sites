Why Lititz Homes Get Dirty Faster Than Most

Lititz sits on the edge of farm country with a fair amount of shade coverage from mature trees. That matters for cleaning because shade creates the perfect environment for algae and moss to grow. You see it on driveways—that dark green or black streaking isn't just dirt, it's living algae that actually spreads over time.

The water table here is also higher than some surrounding areas, which means more moisture retention on surfaces. Pavers, concrete, and wood surfaces all hold water longer, feeding the algae and mold growth. This is why we see a lot of Lititz homeowners with patios and driveways that look dirtier than they would in sunnier areas.

If your property is on the eastern side of Lititz or near tree coverage, you'll probably need cleaning more often than folks in open sun—but more on that later.

The Right Pressure for Each Surface

This is where a lot of people run into problems. I've seen homeowners rent a pressure washer from the hardware store, crank it to 4,000 PSI, and blast their vinyl siding or deck. That's expensive. High pressure drives water into walls, can crack siding, and splinters wood.

Here's how we adjust based on the surface:

Driveways & Concrete

2,500–3,500 PSI. Concrete is tough. We use the higher end for algae-covered drives, lower for newer or delicate finishes. Most Lititz driveways can handle full power.

Patios & Pavers

1,500–2,000 PSI plus biodegradable soap. High pressure can pop pavers or drive water under them. We focus on the chemistry—the soap does most of the work, water pressure just rinses it off.

Siding (Vinyl or Fiber Cement)

Under 1,000 PSI, often called soft wash. We use biodegradable cleaners and let them sit for a few minutes to break down the algae. The water pressure is low enough that it never forces water behind the siding.

Wood Decks

Under 1,500 PSI on the surface, with special nozzles to avoid splintering the grain. We clean with the wood grain, not against it. On newer wood we drop it to 800–1,000 PSI.

Timing: When to Schedule Power Washing in Lititz

Lititz homeowners often ask: when's the best time? Here's what we recommend based on what we see:

  • Spring (April–May) — After winter, algae and mold have built up. This is the busiest time for us. If you only do it once a year, do it in spring.

You show up with the rental unit, spend 30 minutes figuring out how to start it, and then you either use too much pressure and damage something, or you use too little and waste two hours getting half-decent results. Plus you're responsible for any damage—and high-pressure mishaps on siding or decks are expensive to fix.

If it's just a small pad of concrete, sure, DIY makes sense. For anything bigger than that, the time and risk usually aren't worth it.

What To Expect When We Show Up

So you've called and we're coming out. Here's how the job goes:

  1. We arrive with our equipment and do a walkthrough. If there are plants you're worried about or something fragile nearby, we protect it with sheeting or move it.
  2. We set up our water source (either from your outdoor faucet or our tank if we need high-volume water) and run safety checks on equipment.
  3. We start with the highest-pressure areas first (concrete driveways) and work toward the softer surfaces (siding, wood). This way everything gets even drying.
  4. For surfaces that need soap (patios, siding), we apply the cleaner, let it sit for a few minutes to break down algae and mold, then rinse thoroughly.
  5. We do a final walkthrough with you to make sure you're happy with the results. If we missed a spot, we'll hit it again.
  6. We clean up all our equipment and leave your yard the way we found it.

Most single driveways take 45 minutes to an hour. A full house wash takes 3–4 hours. If it's a big property or heavy buildup, we'll let you know going in.

Maintenance: Keep It Clean Longer

After we clean your driveway or patio, it'll look great. But algae starts growing back the same day—you just can't see it yet. Here's how to slow that down:

  • Keep leaves off patios and driveways — Leaves hold moisture and feed algae growth. Rake them regularly.
  • Trim low branches — Less shade = slower algae growth. If you have trees overhanging your driveway, trimming branches helps.
  • Improve drainage — If water pools on your patio or driveway, standing water promotes algae. Better drainage means a cleaner surface longer.
  • Seal concrete — After cleaning, sealing concrete (not pressure washing—this is a separate product) slows algae regrowth and makes the next cleaning easier. We can do this or recommend someone who does.
  • Power wash annually — In Lititz, with the shade and moisture, most properties benefit from annual cleaning. Keeps algae from getting thick.

Power Washing vs. Pressure Washing—What's the Difference?

We use the terms interchangeably, but technically there's a small difference. Power washing uses hot water under pressure, which breaks down oils and grease faster. Pressure washing uses cold water and relies more on mechanical scrubbing and flow. For driveways and patios in Lititz, cold pressure washing is usually fine. But if you have oil stains or stubborn buildup, hot water (power washing) works better.

Most of our Lititz work is cold pressure washing because algae and mold aren't oil-based. Hot water would be more expensive and doesn't add much value. But if you have an oil-stained driveway or grease spot that won't come off, tell us—we can heat it up.
