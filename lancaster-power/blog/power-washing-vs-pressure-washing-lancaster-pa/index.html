Power Washing vs Pressure Washing Lancaster PA — What's the Difference?

Published May 2026 • 6 min read • By Lancaster Power Washing Pros

I get this question pretty often — usually right before someone books: "Do I need power washing or pressure washing?" Most people use the terms interchangeably, and honestly, so do a lot of guys in this business. But there is a real technical difference, and knowing it helps you understand what your property actually needs before you call anyone.

Here's the straight answer, from someone who's been running equipment across Lancaster County for years.

The Actual Technical Difference

The difference comes down to one thing: water temperature.

  • Pressure washing uses cold, high-pressure water — typically 1,500 to 4,000 PSI depending on the surface. Great for concrete, brick, pavers, and anything that needs straight mechanical force to clean.
  • Power washing uses heated water at high pressure. The heat — usually around 200°F — helps dissolve grease, oil, gum, and heavy buildup that cold water alone won't cut through as effectively.

In practice, most residential jobs in Lancaster PA use cold pressure washing. Hot water machines are bulkier, burn more fuel, and are better suited for commercial work — restaurant loading docks, fleet vehicles, industrial equipment. For a house wash or driveway cleaning, cold high-pressure water plus the right cleaning solution does the job just as well.

So when you see a contractor advertising "power washing," they're almost certainly using cold pressure washing equipment unless they specifically tell you otherwise. That's not a scam — it's just how the industry talks.

Where Hot Water Actually Matters

If your driveway has motor oil stains, hydraulic fluid, or heavy cooking grease near a back entrance, hot water power washing is genuinely worth asking for. The heat breaks down the hydrocarbon molecules faster than cleaning solution alone. For standard mold, mildew, dirt, and algae — the stuff most Lancaster homes deal with — cold pressure washing with the right biodegradable pre-treatment handles it cleanly.

The Method That Gets Left Out of This Conversation: Soft Washing

Here's what I want every Lancaster homeowner to understand. The power washing vs. pressure washing debate is almost a distraction, because for a lot of surfaces, the right answer is neither — it's soft washing.

Soft washing uses low pressure (under 500 PSI, sometimes as low as 60) combined with a biodegradable cleaning solution to kill algae, mold, mildew, and bacteria at the root level. It doesn't just blast the surface clean — it kills what's causing the discoloration. That means results last significantly longer. A properly soft-washed roof stays clean for 3 to 5 years. A pressure-washed roof (which you should never do) strips granules off the shingles and voids your manufacturer warranty.

Soft washing is the correct method for:

  • Asphalt shingle roofs
  • Vinyl siding and aluminum siding
  • Painted wood surfaces
  • EIFS / stucco exteriors
  • Cedar shake or wood shingle siding

If a company quotes you for "pressure washing your roof" at high PSI, walk away. That's a sign they either don't know what they're doing or they're using outdated equipment and methods. We use soft wash on every roof job we run in Lancaster County — period.

That combination breeds green algae and black mold on north-facing siding, roof staining on older homes, and years of grit and road spray on driveways along the main routes.

  • House washing: Soft wash on the siding, medium pressure on brick foundations and concrete block. Pre-treat with surfactant and biocide, rinse clean. The whole thing for an average Lancaster rancher or cape cod takes 2–3 hours.

How to Talk to a Contractor About This

When you're getting quotes, here's exactly what to ask:

  1. "What pressure do you use on vinyl siding?" — The right answer is under 500 PSI, or soft wash. Anything over that is a red flag.
  2. "Do you use a pre-treatment solution, or just water?" — A professional always pre-treats. Skipping this means the clean won't last.
  3. "How do you handle roof cleaning?" — Soft wash only, low pressure, cleaning solution. If they say "pressure wash," hang up.
  4. "Are you insured?" — Get a certificate of insurance. Don't take a verbal answer.

Those four questions will filter out 90% of the guys you don't want touching your house.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is power washing or pressure washing better for a driveway in Lancaster PA?

Both work well on concrete driveways. Power washing (heated water) gives a slight edge on oil stains and embedded grease because heat breaks down hydrocarbons faster. Cold pressure washing handles regular dirt, algae, and mildew just fine. Either way, the most important thing is proper PSI — 2,500 to 3,500 on concrete — and a surface cleaner attachment for even results.

Can I pressure wash vinyl siding myself?

You technically can, but it's easy to do it wrong. The biggest mistake homeowners make is using too much pressure or angling the spray upward, which forces water behind the panels and into the wall cavity. Vinyl siding should be soft washed — low pressure plus a cleaning solution — not hit with 2,500 PSI from a rented unit. If you do it yourself, keep pressure under 500 PSI and always spray downward.

What's the difference between soft washing and pressure washing?

Soft washing uses low-pressure water (under 500 PSI) combined with biodegradable cleaning solutions to kill and remove algae, mold, mildew, and bacteria. Pressure washing uses high-pressure water (1,500–4,000 PSI) to physically blast away dirt and buildup. Soft washing is correct for roofs, siding, and painted wood. Pressure washing is correct for concrete driveways, sidewalks, and brick patios.
