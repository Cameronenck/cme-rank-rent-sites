Soft Wash House Washing for Stone and Stucco Homes

A large percentage of the homes I clean in the Strasburg area have stone or stucco exteriors, and these surfaces demand a fundamentally different approach than the vinyl or aluminum siding you find on newer builds throughout Lancaster County. High-pressure washing on stucco is a genuine problem: stucco is a cement-based plaster finish, and aggressive pressure forces water behind the surface layer, can crack or chip the finish coat, and creates moisture intrusion pathways that lead to bigger issues over time. Stone masonry has mortar joints that erode under high pressure — you're removing the material that holds the structure together, not just cleaning the surface.

The correct approach for both surfaces is soft washing: low-pressure application of a biodegradable cleaning solution that lifts algae, mildew, organic pollen staining, and atmospheric grime from the surface without mechanical abrasion or high-pressure water penetration. The solution does the work. The pressure is low enough that there's no risk to mortar joints, stucco finish coats, or the surrounding landscaping. For the historic brick homes in Strasburg Borough itself, we use the same low-pressure methodology — brick can handle slightly more pressure than stucco, but mortar joints on older properties are often softer lime mortar rather than modern Portland, and that material doesn't tolerate aggressive washing.

The organic staining profile here is also distinctive. Farm-adjacent properties throughout the township deal with elevated pollen loads, airborne debris from field operations, and persistent algae and moss growth fed by proximity to the Conestoga Creek corridor. North-facing walls and any surface that stays shaded are particularly prone to heavy green and black biological buildup. Soft washing with a properly formulated cleaning solution kills the growth at the cellular level and removes the staining — not just the visible surface discoloration, but the organism causing it — which means results last significantly longer than high-pressure washing alone.

Driveway, Brick Walkway & Patio Cleaning

Strasburg properties come with a lot of brick and stone hardscape — walkways, patios, retaining walls, and decorative borders that are part of the historic aesthetic of the borough and the working farmstead character of the township. Brick walkways in particular accumulate moss and lichen growth rapidly, especially in shaded spots or low areas that stay damp. Moss growing between brick joints compromises the joint material over time and creates a slip hazard on walkway surfaces.

We clean brick and stone hardscape with rotary surface cleaners and hand wands at controlled pressure, working from the surface outward to avoid driving water and debris into mortar joints. For heavily moss-covered brick, we apply a pre-treatment solution that begins breaking down the growth before any pressure is applied, which produces a more thorough clean without requiring aggressive PSI that would damage the material. Concrete driveways and parking areas get the same surface cleaner treatment that produces even coverage across the full width without the stripe pattern from a single wand pass. Oil staining on driveway aprons gets a commercial degreaser pre-treatment before the wash.

Heritage fencing — wood board-and-batten, split rail, and decorative picket styles common on Strasburg properties — can be cleaned carefully at low pressure to remove mildew, algae, and surface weathering. If you're planning to restain or repaint, cleaning comes first. Stain applied over mildew and oxidized wood fiber bonds poorly and fails early. We can prep the surface correctly so whatever finish you apply afterward lasts as long as it should.

Deck and Fence Washing

Decks on Strasburg properties deal with heavy pollen loading from the surrounding agricultural landscape and persistent organic staining from shaded or low-drainage situations. A deck adjacent to a mature tree line or shaded by an outbuilding can go from clean to visibly stained and slippery in a single season. We match the pressure to the material — pressure-treated pine and cedar at lower PSI to avoid raising the grain, composite decking handled more carefully still, since high-pressure washing damages composite surfaces and voids manufacturer warranties on most products.

If the deck has visible mold or mildew growth, we pre-treat before washing. The pre-treatment penetrates the growth before the wash rinse displaces it, which produces a more thorough result. For decks you plan to seal or restain, we can clean to a level of surface prep that's appropriate for coating — not just surface-clean, but removing the oxidized wood fiber and any biological contamination that would prevent adhesion.

Roof Soft Washing — Gloeocapsa Magma Treatment

If your Strasburg home has dark streaks running down the roof slope, that's Gloeocapsa magma — a cyanobacterium that feeds on the limestone filler in asphalt shingles and spreads by spore. It's present on roofs throughout Lancaster County, but it establishes particularly fast in Strasburg because of the summer humidity in the Conestoga Creek watershed. Roofs that face north, sit under shade, or stay damp in the morning hours are prime candidates. If you've noticed the streaking getting more pronounced over the past few summers, the growth is active and spreading.

Soft washing is the only correct approach for asphalt roofs. We apply a low-pressure solution of sodium hypochlorite and surfactant that kills the Gloeocapsa magma organism at the cellular level. The dead material then washes away gradually with normal rainfall over the following weeks. What you get is a clean roof that doesn't rebloom immediately because the growth is actually killed, not just cosmetically removed. High-pressure washing on a roof knocks off the visible streaking but strips shingle granules, doesn't kill the underlying organism, and voids most asphalt shingle warranties. We don't use high pressure on any roof work — period.

Beyond aesthetics, regular roof cleaning extends shingle life. Gloeocapsa magma and the moss growth that often follows it retain moisture against the shingle surface, accelerating the granule loss and cracking cycle that shortens roof lifespan. Cleaning before the growth establishes heavily is maintenance, not just cosmetics.
