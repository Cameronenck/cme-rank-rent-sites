House Washing in Quarryville

The most common job I do in Quarryville is house washing on properties with vinyl siding that's gone gray-green on the north face and along the foundation line. Green algae, mildew streaks, and the general gray film that develops from pollen and atmospheric dust are all there — and down in southern Lancaster County, they tend to be worse than what I see in the northern part of the county because the moisture lingers longer between dry spells.

Soft washing is the right approach for vinyl. I don't use high pressure on siding — it drives water behind panels, cracks caulk around windows and doors, and doesn't actually treat what's causing the problem. The surface stays clean noticeably longer because the organism is dead rather than just temporarily removed.

For the older brick homes in the downtown — and there are some good-looking ones along State Street and the side streets off it — I'm careful about pressure levels and chemistry. Roof Soft Washing in Quarryville

The dark streaking on asphalt shingle roofs throughout Quarryville and the surrounding southern Lancaster County communities is Gloeocapsa magma — a cyanobacterium that feeds on the limestone filler manufacturers use in shingle composition. It grows in the humid conditions that the Octoraro watershed provides, and once it establishes on a roof, it spreads year over year without treatment. From the ground it can look like weathering or dirt, but it's a living organism that's actively consuming shingle material.

Soft washing is the only correct approach for roof cleaning. A low-pressure application of a specialized chemical solution kills the organism and the residue rinses off over the following weeks of rain. High-pressure roof washing — which some companies still offer — blows shingle granules off the surface, damages the waterproofing layer, and voids most manufacturer warranties. I don't pressure wash roofs. Low pressure, proper chemistry, and patience are what produces a clean roof that stays clean.
