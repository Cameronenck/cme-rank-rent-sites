What Makes Denver Different — Local Factors

Denver's location near the creek, combined with the rolling farmland and mature tree canopy around many properties, creates specific conditions that affect how often homes need cleaning:

Moisture from the Cocalico watershed: The creek and groundwater in the area mean higher humidity, even in winter. That humidity accelerates algae growth — especially on north-facing siding and roofs. A home that sits in deep shade from old oak or maple trees will accumulate the dark algae streaks (Gloeocapsa magma) faster than a sunnier property.

Agricultural dust and pollen: Denver is surrounded by working farmland. In spring and summer, pollen and field dust settle on exterior surfaces. Some of it washes off with rain, but much of it sticks to siding and becomes a thin film that looks like dirt and dulls your home's appearance. A power wash removes that buildup quickly.

Older brick and mortar in Denver borough: Many of Denver's homes were built in the 1950s through 1980s. Older brick and mortar are beautiful but require care. High pressure can damage the mortar joints and accelerate erosion. I always assess brick homes carefully and use soft wash techniques when needed.

Pressure Washing vs. Soft Washing — Which Do You Actually Need?

This is the most important distinction. "Pressure washing" and "soft washing" are two very different techniques, and many contractors use pressure where they shouldn't.

Pressure washing is appropriate for concrete and asphalt surfaces: driveways, sidewalks, patios, pool decks. High pressure — typically 3,000 to 4,000 PSI — removes dirt, algae, and stains effectively. It's fast and looks impressive. But on delicate surfaces, it causes damage.

Soft washing uses lower pressure (500–1,500 PSI) combined with eco-friendly cleaning solutions. It's the right choice for:

  • Vinyl siding (pressure can force water behind the siding into wall cavities)
  • Roofs (high pressure damages asphalt shingles and voids manufacturer warranties)
  • Painted wood (pressure strips paint and erodes wood fibers)
  • Older brick and mortar
  • Stucco and delicate stone

On your free estimate, I'll tell you exactly which approach your home needs. Most Denver-area homes get a combination: pressure for concrete and asphalt, soft wash for siding and roof.

The Black Streaks on Your Roof — What Are They?

If you've lived in Denver long enough, you've noticed the dark, sometimes mottled streaking on asphalt shingles. That's Gloeocapsa magma, a cyanobacterium that colonizes the limestone filler in shingle granules. It thrives in moist, shaded conditions — perfect for north-facing roofs in central Lancaster County.

Left untreated, it spreads and can eventually reduce a roof's lifespan. The good news is that soft washing kills it at the cell level. We apply a specialized low-pressure treatment, the organism dies, and the streaking gradually washes away over the following weeks of rain. No shingle damage, no warranty void.

Important: Pressure washing a roof damages shingles. If a contractor offers to pressure wash your roof, that's a red flag. We use soft wash only.

How Often Should Denver Homes Be Cleaned?

Most Denver-area homes benefit from a full exterior cleaning every one to two years. Here's how to decide:

Annual service makes sense if your home is:

  • Surrounded by mature shade trees (algae grows faster in shade)
  • Close to the creek or in a low-lying area (more moisture)
  • North-facing or mostly shaded (algae thrives in the shade)

Every two years is typical if your home is:

  • On higher ground with good sun exposure
  • Away from large trees
  • Recently cleaned (the second cleaning is often easier)
