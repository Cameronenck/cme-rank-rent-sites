What Actually Affects the Price

Roof cleaning isn't just size times a rate. A handful of factors genuinely move the price up or down, and I want you to understand them before you call anyone.

Roof Square Footage and Pitch

Square footage is the starting point, but pitch matters just as much. A low-slope ranch is faster and safer to work on than a steep 10/12 pitch colonial. Steeper roofs require more time to set up safely, slower movement across the surface, and sometimes additional equipment. That time cost is reflected in the price — but the difference is usually $50 to $100, not hundreds of dollars.

What's Growing Up There

Black algae streaks — the Gloeocapsa Magma most Lancaster homeowners see running down their north-facing shingles — respond well to a single soft wash treatment with minimal extra effort. Moss is a different story. Moss has a root structure that grips into shingle granules, and removing it takes a pre-treatment, dwell time, sometimes a gentle brush pass, and a thorough rinse. Heavy moss adds $75 to $150 to the job. Lichen — the flat gray-green crusty growth — is the toughest, sometimes requiring a second treatment visit a few weeks later once it starts releasing.

Roof Accessibility

How easy it is to get equipment to your roof affects the quote. A house with a flat driveway approach right up to the foundation is simple. A house set back on a hill, bordered by mature landscaping, or with no accessible outdoor water source takes longer to set up. We'll always flag this before we start so there are no surprises.

Last Time It Was Cleaned

A roof that was cleaned two years ago and has light algae coming back is a fast job. A roof that hasn't been touched in eight years with thick black streaking, moss beds along the north slope, and lichen at the ridge — that's a heavy-duty treatment that costs more and takes longer. Staying on a regular cleaning schedule (every two to three years) keeps costs lower over time compared to letting it go until it's a serious problem.

Soft Wash — The Right Way to Clean a Roof

I want to be clear about this because I still run into homeowners who were quoted for pressure washing their roof by another contractor: you should not high-pressure wash asphalt shingles. The granules on asphalt shingles are what protect the underlying mat from UV degradation. Blast them with high pressure and you're stripping years off your roof's life — and potentially voiding your shingle manufacturer's warranty.

The correct method is soft washing. We use a low-pressure delivery system — usually 100 PSI or less at the nozzle — combined with a cleaning solution based on sodium hypochlorite (diluted to the right concentration for roof surfaces). The solution does the work: it kills algae, mold, moss, and lichen at the biological level. The pressure is just enough to rinse it away. Results last two to four years for most roofs. High-pressure washing might look cleaner the day of, but it physically damages the surface and the algae grows back faster from the roughened texture.

If a contractor quotes you "pressure washing" for your asphalt shingle roof, that's a red flag. Ask specifically if they use soft wash technique. The answer will tell you a lot.

What the Black Streaks Actually Are

I get this question constantly on jobs, so I'll answer it here. Those dark gray or black streaks running down your shingles — especially on the north-facing side — are a cyanobacteria called Gloeocapsa Magma. It spreads via airborne spores, feeds on the limestone filler used in modern asphalt shingles, and thrives in the humid Lancaster County summers. It's not a structural problem in the short term, but it does hold moisture against your shingles, and over five to ten years of unchecked growth it accelerates granule loss and reduces the life expectancy of your roof.

The good news: it responds well to soft wash treatment. After a proper cleaning, your shingles should look close to original color within a day or two as the solution continues to work and the dead algae washes away with rain.

Is It Worth It?

Every time I quote a roof cleaning, someone asks some version of "is it actually worth doing?" The short answer is yes — if your roof has visible algae, moss, or lichen growth. Here's why.

Asphalt shingles in this part of Pennsylvania are built to last 25 to 30 years. Persistent algae and moss growth, especially if left for five or more years, can cut that lifespan noticeably by holding moisture and accelerating granule wear. A $400 cleaning every two to three years is cheap insurance compared to an early roof replacement that runs $10,000 to $20,000 for a typical Lancaster County home. Your insurance company and your roof manufacturer both have opinions on this — most shingle warranties require regular maintenance including cleaning.

Beyond the longevity argument, there's the curb appeal reality. Black streaked shingles read as neglected to anyone driving past your house. A clean roof makes the whole property look better-maintained.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does roof cleaning cost in Lancaster PA?

Most residential roof cleanings in Lancaster PA range from $300 to $700 depending on roof size, pitch, and how bad the algae or moss buildup is. A standard single-story ranch with a simple low-pitch roof usually runs $300–$450. A two-story colonial with more square footage and a steeper pitch typically falls in the $450–$650 range. Heavy moss or lichen growth adds cost due to extra treatment time.

Should you pressure wash or soft wash a roof?

Soft washing is the correct method for asphalt shingle roofs — period. High-pressure washing blasts away the protective granules on shingles, which shortens the life of your roof and can void your shingle warranty. Soft washing uses low pressure combined with a biodegradable cleaning solution that kills algae, mold, and moss at the root. The results last longer and the roof isn't damaged in the process.

What causes the black streaks on roofs in Lancaster PA?

Those dark streaks are Gloeocapsa Magma — a type of algae that feeds on the limestone filler in asphalt shingles. It spreads from spore to spore across the roof surface and shows up as dark gray or black streaking, usually starting on north-facing slopes or shaded sections. The algae itself doesn't cause immediate structural damage, but it holds moisture against the shingles and shortens their lifespan over time. A proper soft wash removes it completely.

How long does roof cleaning last?

A professional soft wash typically keeps a roof clean for 2–4 years. Homes in Lancaster County with heavy tree canopy overhead or significant shade on north-facing slopes may see algae return closer to the 2-year mark. Open, sun-exposed roofs can stay clean for 3–4 years. Some contractors apply a post-treatment zinc or copper-based inhibitor that extends the clean period — we can discuss that option when we quote your job.

Is roof cleaning safe for my landscaping?

Yes, when done correctly. Before we apply any cleaning solution, we wet down all plants, shrubs, and grass around the perimeter of the house. The sodium hypochlorite solution we use is diluted to the appropriate concentration for roof cleaning — effective at killing algae but safe for established landscaping when properly rinsed. We do a final rinse pass on all beds and ground cover when the job is complete.
