Tree Removal vs Tree Trimming in Lancaster PA — Which One Do You Actually Need?

By Lancaster Tree Removal Pros  ·  May 10, 2026  ·  6 min read

I get this question almost every week: "Can we just trim it back instead of taking it out?" Sometimes the answer is yes. Sometimes the honest answer is no — the tree is too far gone and trimming it is throwing money at a problem that won't go away. After 15-plus years of working trees throughout Lancaster County, I've learned to read the difference pretty quickly.

If you're trying to figure out whether your tree needs to come down or just needs a good trim, this guide will walk you through exactly how I think about it — no sales pitch, just the straight breakdown.

The Core Difference

Tree trimming — also called pruning — is about improving a living tree. You're shaping the canopy, removing dead or crossing branches, and improving structure so the tree stays healthy and safe for years to come. A well-trimmed tree is a better tree.

Tree removal is what happens when the tree can't be saved or when keeping it creates an unacceptable risk to your property or the people around it. We take the whole tree down, section by section, and haul it away. Done right, there's nothing left but a stump — and we can grind that same day if you want.

The key question isn't just which costs less. It's which actually solves the problem.

When Trimming Is the Right Call

A healthy tree with some overgrowth, dead branches, or a shape that's gotten out of hand is a great candidate for trimming. Here's what I look for:

  • The trunk is solid. No significant cracks, cavities, or soft spots. If the trunk is structurally sound, the tree has a future worth investing in.
  • Less than 30–40% of the canopy is dead or diseased. A tree can recover from losing a third of its canopy. It cannot recover from losing most of it.
  • The root system is intact. If you haven't had construction or soil disruption near the base in the last few years, roots are probably fine.
  • The tree is just overgrown. Long branches reaching toward the roof, rubbing against the house, or blocking light — those are trimming jobs, not removal jobs.
  • Crossing or rubbing branches. When branches rub together, they create wounds that let disease in. A trim every few years prevents that from becoming a bigger issue.

In Lancaster County, I'd say about 60% of the calls I go out on turn out to be trimming jobs. People assume they need the whole tree out and are relieved to hear it's just a cleanup. That's a good outcome for everyone.

When Removal Is the Only Real Answer

There are situations where no amount of trimming saves a tree. In those cases, removing it sooner rather than later is almost always cheaper and safer than waiting for it to fail on its own.

  • More than 50% of the canopy is gone. At that point, the tree doesn't have enough leaf surface to sustain itself. It's going to decline no matter what.
  • The trunk has significant rot or a large cavity. A hollow or rotting trunk can't support the tree's weight, especially in wind. These trees fail without warning.
  • The tree is leaning toward a structure. A lean that's developed recently — meaning it wasn't always like that — often signals root failure. That tree needs to come down before it comes down on its own terms.
  • Major storm damage. If more than half the crown was lost in a storm, the tree is compromised. It's also now more exposed to wind loading, which accelerates the problem.
  • Disease or pest damage is severe. Emerald ash borer has taken a lot of ash trees in Lancaster County over the past decade. Once a tree is 40–50% dead from EAB, removal is the right move.
  • The tree is dead. Dead trees don't get better. And as wood dries out and becomes brittle, removal gets harder and more expensive. Remove dead trees sooner rather than later.

The Trap: Trimming a Tree That Should Come Down

Here's what I see go wrong sometimes: a homeowner gets a quote for removal and thinks, "What if we just trim it instead to save money?" I understand the impulse. But if the tree has root problems, significant trunk rot, or is already mostly dead, trimming it doesn't change the outcome. It just delays it — and when the tree finally fails, it usually does so during a storm, which is the worst possible scenario for your roof, your fence, or your neighbor's property.

I've seen plenty of cases where a homeowner paid $400 to trim a tree that should have come out, then paid $1,200 to remove it 18 months later after it cracked in a nor'easter. The honest contractor who recommends removal when removal is needed is saving you money in the long run, even if the upfront number is higher.

If you're not sure, get a second opinion. That's fine. Just make sure whoever you're asking is licensed and has real experience with tree health assessment — not just someone with a chainsaw and a truck.

How Often Should You Trim Your Trees?

For most healthy trees in Lancaster County, a trim every 3–5 years is plenty. Fast-growing species — silver maple, willow, poplars — might need attention every 2–3 years. Fruit trees, if you want them productive, usually get annual pruning in late winter before they bud out.

The goal is to stay ahead of it. Small dead branches are easy and cheap to remove. When those branches have been hanging for five years and grown to 6 inches in diameter, the job gets bigger. Regular maintenance is almost always cheaper than deferred maintenance.
