Tree Species Issues in the Quarryville Area

Black walnut. Black walnut is common on older southern Lancaster County properties, and it presents specific removal challenges. The wood is extremely dense and heavy — significantly heavier than most hardwoods of comparable size — which means sectioning and rigging have to account for loads that would surprise you if you're used to working with oaks or maples. Black walnut is also valuable lumber; if you have a large, straight-grained walnut that needs to come down, it's worth asking whether a local sawyer wants to purchase the log before we chip it. That doesn't change what we charge for the removal, but it can offset cost for you.

Dead and declining ash trees. Emerald ash borer has been through southern Lancaster County the same as everywhere else in the region. Most ash trees that weren't treated years ago are already dead or in advanced decline. I want to be direct about this: a dead ash tree dries out fast, and dead ash wood is unpredictable in a way that living wood isn't. Limbs snap without visible warning cracks. Bark sloughs off, which affects where and how you can rig. If you have a dead ash near a structure, a lane, or anywhere people walk or work, it needs to come down now — not after the next wind event.

Oaks with basal decay. Mature oaks with basal decay — soft wood or fungal growth at the root flare or base of the trunk — are among the most hazardous trees I work with. A 70-foot oak can look healthy 40 feet up and be catastrophically compromised at ground level. Ganoderma bracket fungi, or a soft hollow at the base, or significant excavation damage around the root zone from grading years ago are all red flags. If you have a large oak with any of those signs, get it looked at before the next major wind event.

Wild cherry and locust along fence lines. On farm properties throughout Fulton and Providence townships, I regularly see wild black cherry and black locust that has grown up along fence lines over the years, sometimes to 40 or 50 feet, leaning over lanes and outbuildings. These aren't ornamental trees and they don't get treated like ornamental trees — but when they're positioned over something you care about, they need to come down properly. We handle fence-line tree work throughout southern Lancaster County.

Stump Grinding in Quarryville

After a removal, most homeowners want the stump gone. I get it — a stump in a yard or along a property edge looks unfinished, it's a mowing obstacle, and on farm properties it's an outright hazard. Stump grinding takes the stump down to 8–12 inches below grade and grinds out most of the lateral root mass. The resulting chips fill the hole; you can top-dress with topsoil and seed the area within a few weeks.

On properties with heavy limestone near the surface, stump grinding takes more time and more passes than in softer soils. I'll quote it accurately based on the stump's diameter and what I can see of the root collar. Bundling stump grinding with the removal is almost always cheaper than scheduling it as a separate return visit — the equipment is already on site and the crew is already there.
