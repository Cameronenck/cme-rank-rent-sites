Lot Clearing Cost in Lancaster PA: 2026 Pricing from a Local Pro

By Lancaster Tree Removal Pros  ·  May 15, 2026  ·  7 min read

Every couple of weeks I get a call from someone who just bought a raw lot in Lancaster County and wants to know what it's going to cost to clear it. Sometimes it's a half-acre they picked up off Route 30 to build a pole barn. Sometimes it's a wooded parcel near Manheim or Elizabethtown they want to turn into a homesite. And sometimes it's just an overgrown corner of an existing property that's gone wild for 20 years.

I've been doing tree work and lot clearing in Lancaster County for over 15 years. The cost question is never a one-line answer, but I can give you a realistic framework so you know what to expect before you start calling for quotes.

Lot Clearing Cost in Lancaster PA: What You're Actually Looking At

Here's a straight look at what lot clearing typically costs in the Lancaster area in 2026. These are real numbers from jobs we've done, not generic national averages that have no bearing on what a Lancaster County crew actually charges.

Lot Size & Condition Typical Cost Range Notes
Quarter-acre, light brush and small trees $1,500 – $3,000 Mostly saplings and overgrowth, minimal stumps
Quarter-acre, moderate density (mixed trees) $3,000 – $5,000 Mix of medium-size maples, oaks, pines with stumps
Half-acre, lightly wooded $3,500 – $6,000 Open understory, trees spread out
Half-acre, dense woodland $6,000 – $10,000 Tight canopy, heavy debris, full stump grinding
Full acre, lightly wooded $5,000 – $9,000 Grassy with tree lines and scattered hardwoods
Full acre, fully wooded $9,000 – $15,000+ Mature timber, full clearing, grading included

These ranges assume full debris removal and basic stump grinding. If you want the lot graded and seeded after clearing, that adds cost. If you want timber value credited back to you for any marketable logs, that can sometimes reduce cost. I'll get into both below.

What Drives the Price Up or Down

Tree Size and Density

This is the biggest cost driver. A lot covered in 6-inch saplings clears fast. A lot with 30-inch oaks and silver maples is a completely different job. Mature hardwoods require larger equipment, more crew time, and produce significantly more debris to process. When I walk a lot to quote it, I'm counting large trees and noting species before I put a number on the job.

Stump Disposal

Stumps are a major cost variable. Your options are: grind them (most common for residential and light commercial lots), pull them with an excavator (faster but rougher on the grade, common for new construction sites), or leave them to rot (only works if the area won't be built on or landscaped). Grinding every stump on a densely wooded half-acre adds $1,500–$3,500 to a job depending on stump count and size. Pulling with an excavator is often faster on a job that's getting graded anyway.

Debris Hauling vs. Chipping On-Site

Small brush and limbs can be run through a chipper and spread as mulch or hauled. Large-diameter wood is either left for the property owner as firewood, processed and sold, or hauled. If you want everything completely off the property, that's more than if you're okay with a firewood pile left in the corner. Be clear about this when you're getting quotes — "lot clearing" can mean different things depending on who you're asking.

Equipment Access

If I can get a skid steer and chipper truck to the lot easily, the job moves faster and costs less. If the lot is down a narrow lane, blocked by a low bridge, or has soft ground after rain that limits heavy equipment, we have to factor that in. I've cleared lots in Lancaster County where we had to bring everything in on foot because a tracked machine would have destroyed a neighbor's driveway. That adds time.

Slope and Grade

Flat lots are straightforward. Lots with significant slope slow down chainsaw work and complicate machine access. A steep lot in the hills west of Lancaster city is going to cost more than a flat lot near Manheim or Mountville. Not dramatically more, but it factors in.

Permits for Lot Clearing in Lancaster County

This is something a lot of people don't think about until they're already scheduled. In Lancaster County, permit requirements vary by municipality. Some townships are fine with residential lot clearing with no paperwork at all. Others require a land disturbance permit or a grading permit before you break ground. A few have local tree ordinances that restrict removal on lots above a certain size or within certain zoning districts.

My advice: call your township or borough before we schedule anything. Just give them a quick call, describe the job, and ask if any permits are required. It takes 10 minutes and can save you from a stop-work order and a fine. We run into this occasionally, and it always creates delays that could have been avoided. If you're not sure who to call, reach out to us at 717-297-1693 and we'll help you figure out which municipality covers your parcel.

Can You Sell the Timber?

Sometimes. If your lot has mature oak, walnut, or cherry timber in good condition, a timber buyer might be interested. In Lancaster County, there are a handful of sawyers and timber buyers who will assess a stand and offer you a value for the logs. That value can sometimes be applied as a credit against your clearing cost — meaning the job ends up cheaper because we're removing something worth money.

The catch: not every stand of trees has marketable timber. Crowded lots with lots of species competition produce logs with sweep, lean, and defects that mills don't want. Short sections between fences and property lines often aren't worth hauling. If you think your lot might have value, I'll tell you honestly whether it's worth pursuing or whether it's just going to slow the job down chasing a number that isn't there.

What Clearing Includes (and What It Doesn't)

When you get a lot clearing quote, make sure you know exactly what's included. Here's how I break it down on every job:

  • Included in standard clearing: Felling all trees and brush within the designated area, chipping or hauling all debris, stump grinding to 6–10 inches below grade, rough raking of the clearing area.
  • Often excluded from base price: Fine grading, topsoil spreading, seeding or hydroseed, fence removal, invasive vine removal (which can add significant time), disposal of non-wood material (old fence posts, wire, buried debris).
  • Usually available as add-ons: Deeper stump grinding for slab or foundation areas, log splitting and stacking, topsoil grading, removal of existing structures like old sheds or outbuildings.

Get the scope in writing. I've seen homeowners get surprised when they realized the quote they accepted didn't include stump grinding or debris hauling. A good contractor will walk through the scope clearly before anything is signed.

How Lot Clearing Compares to Standard Tree Removal

Lot clearing and standard tree removal in Lancaster PA are related services but priced differently. When you're removing a single tree from a yard, we price by that tree — its size, location, and complexity. With lot clearing, we're pricing by the acre and by the overall density of what needs to come out. The per-tree cost on a lot clearing job is usually lower than individual tree removal because we're moving efficiently across a large area with no need to protect landscaping or work carefully around structures.

If you have a handful of trees on a residential property — say, four or five trees that need to come down — that's still individual tree removal, not lot clearing. Lot clearing typically starts when you're talking about a half-acre or more of trees that all need to go, or an overgrown parcel where the entire vegetative layer is being removed. If you're unsure which service you need, just call and describe what you've got. We'll tell you the most efficient way to approach it.

Choosing a Lot Clearing Contractor in Lancaster County

A few things to check before you hire anyone for lot clearing work in Lancaster County:

  • Licensed and insured. Lot clearing involves heavy equipment and falling trees. Ask for a certificate of liability insurance before anyone starts work. Minimum $1M coverage is standard.
  • Written scope of work. Know exactly what's included and what's not. Verbal agreements on $5,000+ jobs lead to disputes.
  • Equipment they own vs. sub out. Some tree crews don't own a skid steer or chipper and have to sub those out, which adds cost and coordination headaches. Ask what equipment they're bringing to the job.
  • References from similar jobs. Clearing a wooded lot is different from trimming trees in a neighborhood. Ask if they've done lot clearing jobs of similar size in Lancaster County specifically.
  • Debris disposal plan. Ask where the wood and brush is going. Some outfits that seem cheap are cheap because they dump illegally. That's your property on the line, not theirs.
