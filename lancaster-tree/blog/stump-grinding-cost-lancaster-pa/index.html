Stump Grinding Cost in Lancaster PA: What to Expect in 2026

By Lancaster Tree Removal Pros  ·  May 13, 2026  ·  6 min read

Every week I get a call from someone who had a tree taken down — sometimes by us, sometimes by another crew — and they're staring at a stump in their yard wondering what it's going to cost to get rid of it. It's one of the most common questions I hear, and the honest answer is: usually a lot less than people expect.

I've been doing tree work in Lancaster County for over 15 years. Stump grinding is one of those services that's pretty straightforward once you understand what drives the price. Let me break it down the way I'd explain it to a neighbor.

Here's what you can realistically expect to pay for stump grinding around Lancaster County in 2026:

I price by stump diameter at ground level. Some companies charge by the inch — say, $3–$5 per inch of diameter — which works out to a similar range. Either way, the diameter of the stump is the main number driving the quote.

What Affects the Price Beyond Size

Species and Wood Hardness

Not all stumps grind the same. A 20-inch black walnut stump will take significantly longer than a 20-inch silver maple. Walnut, oak, and hickory are dense hardwoods — the grinder works harder and slower. Softer species like pine, poplar, and silver maple go fast. When I'm pricing a job, I'm mentally adjusting for wood density, not just diameter.

Access to the Stump

Stump grinders are machines — they need to get to the stump. If the stump is surrounded by a fence, buried in a corner of a tight backyard, or near a retaining wall, we have to factor in setup time or potentially use a smaller machine. Tight access adds time; time adds cost. If there's a gate or a fence panel that needs to come off temporarily, mention that when you call so we can account for it.

Stump Age and Condition

Old stumps that have been sitting for years are often easier to grind — the wood has started to soften. Fresh stumps from recently cut trees are at peak density. If you had a tree taken down two years ago and just now getting around to the stump, it may actually grind faster than you'd expect.

Surface Roots

Some trees — silver maples and cottonwoods are the worst offenders around here — spread surface roots several feet from the base. Standard grinding covers the main stump and root flare. If you've got large surface roots creeping across your lawn that you want ground down, that's extra work and typically priced separately. Worth asking about upfront so there are no surprises.

Do You Actually Need to Grind the Stump?

This is something I get asked a lot. The short answer: yes, in almost every situation.

Here's what happens when you leave a stump in the ground. First, it sends up new shoots — some species are relentless about this, especially oaks and sweetgums. You'll be cutting those suckers back all summer. Second, rotting wood attracts carpenter ants, termites, and wood-boring beetles. I've been called to jobs where a neglected stump had become a full-on insect colony 10 feet from someone's foundation. Third, the stump is a tripping hazard, a lawn mower hazard, and a headache every time you try to plant or seed around it.

The $100–$200 to grind it is almost always worth it. The only time I'd say skip it is if the stump is in a far corner of the property where it's out of the way and you don't care about the area at all.

What Happens After Grinding

After we grind a stump, you're left with a pile of wood chips and a depression in the ground. We typically fill that back in with the chips. Here's what to do from there:

  • For grass: Remove excess chips, mix in topsoil, and seed or sod. Most stumps are ready to grass over within a week or two.
  • For replanting a new tree: Wait at least a few months and use fresh topsoil mixed into the hole. The old root system decomposing underground can tie up nitrogen, which isn't ideal for a new tree right away.
  • For a garden bed: The chips actually make decent mulch. Rake them into place and plant around them.

One thing to know: grinding does not remove the entire root system. The lateral roots spread out underground and will decompose naturally over several years. That's fine for most purposes. If you're pouring concrete or building a structure on that spot, talk to us first — there may be more excavation needed.

How to Get the Best Price on Stump Grinding Near Lancaster PA

  • Combine with tree removal. If you're scheduling a tree removal anyway, add the grind to the same job. You'll save on mobilization and almost always get a better overall price.
  • Do multiple stumps at once. If you've got three stumps on the property, doing them all in one visit is significantly cheaper per stump than three separate trips.
  • Measure before you call. Knowing the approximate diameter of your stump lets me give you a ballpark number over the phone without needing to drive out for an estimate. Grab a tape measure and measure across the top of the stump at ground level.
  • Book off-peak. Winter and early spring are slower. We're more flexible on scheduling and sometimes on price during the off-season.
