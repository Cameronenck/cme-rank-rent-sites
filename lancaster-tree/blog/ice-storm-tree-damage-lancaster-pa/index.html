Ice Storm Tree Damage in Lancaster PA — What to Do & How Much It Costs

Published June 15, 2026 | Lancaster Tree Removal Pros

I've been doing tree work in Lancaster County for over a decade, and every winter brings the same scene: ice storms tear through our area, snap trees like toothpicks, and leave homeowners scrambling to figure out what to do next.

If you're reading this because an ice storm just dropped a branch on your roof or snapped one of your trees in half, you need to know a few things right now. First: stay away from the damage. Second: call a licensed tree removal company today. Third: your homeowners insurance might cover it.

Let me walk you through what ice storms do to trees, what it costs to fix them, and exactly when you should call us.

Why Ice Storms Hit Lancaster Trees So Hard

Lancaster gets the perfect storm for ice damage every winter. We're in that zone where December through February brings rain, freezing temperatures, and freezing rain — not snow, but actual ice accumulation on branches.

Here's what happens: A half-inch of ice weighs about a pound per square foot of branch surface. A mature oak with a 60-foot crown might accumulate 100+ pounds of ice in a single storm. Branches aren't engineered for that kind of load. They snap.

Unlike wind damage, which bends and sways a whole tree, ice damage starts at the branch tips and works inward. The smallest twigs snap first, then thicker branches, then major limbs. In bad storms, we see entire trees that have been "cored" — snapped off at the trunk.

Even healthy trees fail under ice. I've seen 80-year-old oaks split down the middle after a bad storm. It's not about whether the tree was weak. It's about physics.

What to Do Immediately After an Ice Storm

When the ice melts and you see the damage, your first instinct might be to grab a chainsaw and start cutting branches. Don't.

Here's the right order:

  1. Keep people and pets away. Broken branches don't fall straight down. They hang at odd angles, under tension, and can snap without warning. A 50-pound branch can seriously injure someone. A tree that's 40% snapped can still fall all the way.
  2. Document with photos. Take pictures of the damage from multiple angles. This matters for insurance claims later.
  3. Call a tree removal company the same day or next morning. Not tomorrow afternoon. Not next week. Today or tomorrow. The longer broken trees stand, the more liability they create for you.
  4. Don't attempt removal yourself. You'll read online about DIY tree removal. Ice-damaged trees are unpredictable — the break points are weak, branches can fall without warning, and it's easy to get caught under something heavy.

We get calls all winter from people who tried to fix storm damage themselves and got hurt. Not worth it.

Does Homeowners Insurance Cover Ice Storm Damage?

This is the question everyone asks, and the answer is: sometimes.

Covered: If the tree damages your house, garage, deck, or another structure, most homeowners policies cover removal up to a stated limit (often $500–$2,000). You pay your deductible, insurance pays the rest.

Not covered: If the tree just falls in your yard and doesn't hit anything, you pay. No removal coverage unless your policy has specific tree damage language.

I recommend calling your insurance agent the day of the storm. Have photos ready. They'll tell you what's covered in your specific policy. Some companies are faster than others. Some require you to get estimates from licensed contractors — which we're happy to provide.

Should You Wait to Remove a Partially Damaged Tree?

No. Not for any reason.

A partially broken tree is a hazard. The damage path is unpredictable. The next wind event — even minor wind — can snap more branches or topple the whole thing. We've removed ice-damaged trees that looked stable for weeks and then suddenly failed in 20-mph wind.

Liability matters too. If a branch falls on a neighbor's car, a guest's head, or a power line, you're potentially liable. Your homeowners insurance might not cover it if the tree was left standing in a visibly damaged state.

Remove it within 48 hours of the storm. Don't wait for a nice day. Don't wait to see if it survives. The cost of removal is far lower than the risk.

Can Ice-Damaged Trees Be Saved?

Sometimes. If only small branches are broken, we can prune the damage and the tree survives. If the main trunk is cracked or the tree is 60%+ broken, it's a goner. We make that assessment during the free estimate.

If we can save it, we do. If we can't, we remove it safely and you move on.

How to Prevent Ice Damage (Next Winter)

You can't prevent ice storms, but you can reduce damage:

  • Remove weak or dead branches before winter. Call for a pruning in October or November. Healthier trees resist ice better.
  • Remove trees that overhang your house. If you have a large tree hanging over your roof and you're not attached to it, remove it before ice season.
  • Thin dense trees. Trees with thicker canopies accumulate more ice. Strategic pruning reduces weight load.
  • Avoid planting in areas with trees. Space new plantings away from existing trees to reduce cascading damage.

These are preventative measures. In a truly bad ice storm, even perfect trees fail. But proper maintenance helps.
