Common Tree Problems We See in Ephrata

Bradford Pears Splitting Apart

If I had to name one tree that causes more emergency calls in Ephrata than any other, it's the Bradford pear. They were planted everywhere in Lancaster County developments through the 80s and 90s, and now they're hitting 25–35 years old. Bradford pears have a structural flaw — the branching pattern creates tight, almost vertical forks that eventually split under their own weight, especially in ice storms or heavy spring snow. When half of a Bradford pear comes down, it usually doesn't give much warning. If you've got one over your driveway or near your house and it's been more than 20 years, have someone look at it.

Ash Trees and the Emerald Ash Borer

The emerald ash borer has wiped out ash trees all across Lancaster County, and Ephrata has been hit like everywhere else. A dead ash is a problem: the wood goes brittle fast, and within a couple of years it becomes unpredictable to work with. We've removed a lot of ash trees in Ephrata that were still standing but had already lost structural integrity. If you've got an ash that's looking thin — sparse canopy, lots of dead branches, bark splitting in vertical strips — it's probably been EAB-killed and needs to come out before it becomes an emergency.

Silver Maples Over Driveways and Rooflines

Silver maples grow fast, which is why people plant them — but they also get massive, they drop branches constantly, and their root systems are aggressive enough to crack sidewalks and get into sewer lines. Older properties in Ephrata Borough often have silver maples that were planted as "shade trees" 40 years ago and are now 70–80 feet tall with canopies hanging over everything. The calculus on when to take one down is different for every homeowner, but once you're seeing consistent limb drops, that's the signal.

Do You Need a Permit for Tree Removal in Ephrata PA?

For most private residential properties in Ephrata Borough, you don't need a permit to remove a tree on your own land. That said, there are exceptions worth knowing: trees in the public right-of-way (between the sidewalk and the street) technically belong to the borough, not you, and removing them requires going through the borough's public works department. Trees on properties within historic districts may also have additional restrictions.

If you're not sure which category your tree falls into, ask us when we come out for the estimate. We've been doing this in Lancaster County long enough to know what needs approvals and what doesn't. We'll flag it if anything looks like it could be an issue before we start.
