Silver Maple Hazards in Millersville

Silver maples are the dominant problem tree on student rental properties and postwar residential streets throughout Millersville Borough. They were planted heavily in this type of neighborhood through the 1960s and 1970s because they grow fast — you get a full canopy in less than a decade. The problem shows up 30 or 40 years later when you're looking at a 50- or 60-foot tree with a tight co-dominant stem structure, a root system heaving the driveway, and a crown that's overextended well past the roofline.

Silver maples on rental properties are a particular liability issue. They develop V-shaped unions — two or more major trunks splitting from the same point — that become structural failure points under wind or ice. I've seen these split clean down the middle after a thunderstorm, taking out fences, damaging roofs, and landing across neighbor properties. If you own rental property in Millersville and have large silver maples on the lot, get them evaluated before a storm forces your hand. The cost of proactive removal is a fraction of what you'll pay after a tree comes down on the house.

When a silver maple can be saved with selective crown reduction or cabling, I'll say so. But when the structural compromise is past the point of correction, removal is the right call and I'll give you a straight answer about that too.

EAB and Dead Ash Tree Removal in Millersville

Emerald ash borer has been working through Lancaster County for years and Millersville has not been spared. The Millersville University campus had a significant ash tree population — mature specimens that contributed to the neighborhood canopy on surrounding streets — and most of those trees that weren't treated early are dead or severely declining. Dead ash trees in and around residential properties on West Cottage Avenue, Manor Avenue, and surrounding borough streets represent active hazards.

Here's why dead ash tree removal can't wait: once an ash tree dies, it dries out fast. Within one to two growing seasons the bark starts sloughing off, the wood becomes brittle, and the tree enters a deterioration curve that makes removal progressively harder and more expensive. Limbs from dead ash trees drop without the gradual warning signs you get from other species — there's no predictable pattern of decline before failure. If you have a dead ash near a walkway, driveway, or structure in Millersville, it needs to come down now.

We've removed dead ash trees throughout Millersville Borough and Manor Township in all stages of decline. Even in advanced deterioration, there are safe techniques for sectioning and rigging a dead ash down. But earlier is always better — both for cost and for safety.
