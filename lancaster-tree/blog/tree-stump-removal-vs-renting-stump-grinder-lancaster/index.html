Tree Stump Removal vs. Renting a Stump Grinder in Lancaster PA

Updated June 2026 • 8 min read • By Lancaster Tree Removal Pros

You've had a tree removed. The stump is ugly, it's in the way, and you're wondering: should I rent a stump grinder and grind it myself, or hire somebody to do it? The rental looks cheap online. But like a lot of DIY projects that involve heavy equipment, the real cost — in money, time, and safety — is more complicated than it first appears.

I've been removing stumps in Lancaster County for fifteen years. I've seen people rent grinders, hurt themselves, damage their property, and end up paying me anyway to fix it. Let me walk you through the actual costs and the real risks so you can make an informed decision.

The Rental Cost Illusion

A stump grinder rental looks affordable. Most equipment rental shops in Lancaster charge $75–$150 per day, sometimes less for eight-hour half days. Your first instinct is: that's way cheaper than hiring a pro.

But that's only the equipment rental. You also have to factor in:

  • Delivery and pickup. A self-propelled grinder weighs 1,000+ pounds. Most rental shops charge $100–$200 to deliver it and pick it up. Some let you haul it yourself if you have a trailer rated for the weight.
  • Fuel. Stump grinders are gas-powered and thirsty. A full day of grinding can burn through $15–$30 in fuel depending on the machine.
  • Operator's time. This is the big one. If you've never operated a stump grinder, expect a learning curve. What a professional finishes in 45 minutes might take you 2–3 hours. Every extra hour is more fuel, more wear on the equipment, and more frustration.
  • Your physical effort and risk. This isn't factored into cost, but it matters.
  • Damage costs. Grinders can tear up a yard, hit rocks, damage the area around the stump. If something goes wrong, you're paying for repairs.

Let's work through a realistic example. Small stump, six inches in diameter, in your yard.

Delivery costs the same whether it's a small stump or a large one. Suddenly professional removal at $400–$600 looks reasonable.

Time and Difficulty

A stump grinder is not intuitive to operate. You're standing on a moving machine with one hand on the throttle and the other on a hydraulic lever that moves a spinning carbide wheel. The wheel is rotating at several thousand RPM and can tear through a stump, but it can also kick back violently if you hit a rock or nail in the wood.

Here's what a typical first-timer encounters:

  • Getting the machine positioned. You need to back the grinder up to the stump so the wheel is centered. First time doing this? That's 10–15 minutes of careful maneuvering and probably a few adjustments.
  • Learning the controls. Throttle, wheel depth, forward and reverse. Each one has a learning curve. Go too deep too fast and you'll stall the machine or hit the deck. Grind too shallow and you'll spend forever making passes.
  • Making passes. A small stump needs 5–8 passes, side to side, raising the wheel slightly each time. Get the pattern wrong and you'll have a stubborn chunk in the middle that takes forever to finish.
  • Cleanup and adjustments. After each pass you chip out the ground-up stump and roots, reposition the machine, and try again. A small stump can take 45 minutes to an hour. A medium stump: 1.5–2 hours. A large stump: 3–4 hours if you're comfortable with it.

A professional crew does this dozens of times a week. We know the speed, the angle, the technique. We have backup equipment if something goes wrong. You get one shot with a rental. If you tear something up or spend six hours on a job you thought would take two, that cost advantage disappears fast.

Safety — The Real Cost

Stump grinders are dangerous. Not "handle carefully" dangerous. Legitimately violent equipment dangerous.

The spinning wheel has serious kinetic energy. If you lose your balance or slip, or if something catches and the machine lurches, your hand or arm can be drawn into that wheel. People lose fingers, get deep cuts, break bones. The machine can kick sideways suddenly — torque is real — and throw you off balance.

Flying debris is another hazard. Rocks in the stump get ejected at high speed. A piece of shrapnel can take out your eye. I wear protective glasses and a face shield every time. You probably won't. First accident, you learn.

The machine itself can tip, especially on sloped ground or if you're operating it tired. If you tip a 1,000+ pound machine toward yourself, you're getting hurt. No helmet is going to save you.

Most people who get hurt on rental stump grinders have never used one before. The learning curve, the moment you get overconfident, the instant you lose focus — that's when things go wrong. Professional crews have insurance, emergency protocols, and the experience to avoid most of those situations.

I've had to call 911 because someone rented a grinder, had an accident, and ended up in the ER. The hospital bill is $5,000+. That totally erases any savings from a $260 rental.

What Happens to the Roots?

A common misconception: grinding a stump removes the whole tree root system. It doesn't.

A stump grinder grinds the visible stump down to 8–12 inches below the surface (or sometimes less if you want to plant something in that spot). The root ball, the major roots spreading out underground, stays put. Over time it rots. Hardwoods like oak take 5–10 years to decompose. Softwoods might take 2–4 years. You can plant over a ground stump and most plants do fine.

If you want complete root removal, that's excavation work. You hire a bobcat operator to dig out the root ball. That's expensive and leaves a big hole. Almost nobody does it unless the stump is in the middle of a future foundation or parking lot. For normal property use, grinding is the standard.

When DIY Makes Sense

I'm not saying never rent a grinder. There are situations where it's reasonable:

  • You have multiple small stumps. If you've got five or six small, soft-wood stumps (ash, cottonwood, pine), DIY might be worth it. You're spending the same delivery fee but getting a lot of work done in one rental period.
  • You have rental experience. If you've used heavy equipment before, operated a bobcat or a loader, you'll be more comfortable with a grinder. The learning curve is shorter.
  • You like the project work. Some people enjoy operating equipment and the challenge of a DIY job. If that's you, the cost is worth the experience.
  • You're okay with imperfect results. If you don't mind the stump not being perfectly ground down, or if you take an extra day and don't care, rental can work.

For a single medium or large stump, especially if you've never done it before, professional removal makes more sense financially and definitely makes sense safety-wise.
