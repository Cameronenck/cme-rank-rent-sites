Tree Health Assessment in Lancaster, PA

Mature trees on Lancaster County properties are worth protecting — financially and structurally. A 100-year-old oak on an Ephrata farmstead, a double row of maples lining a Lititz property, a black walnut on a Manheim lot — these are assets that take a century to replace. A tree health assessment identifies problems early enough that intervention is still possible, and tells you honestly when a tree has passed the point where investment makes sense.

Lancaster Tree Pros provides professional tree health assessments for homeowners, commercial property managers, HOAs, and municipalities throughout Lancaster County. We evaluate structure, disease, pest pressure, root condition, and site factors, then give you a clear report with options — not a sales pitch for services you don't need.

What a Tree Health Assessment Covers

  • Crown condition — live canopy percentage, leaf size and color, deadwood volume
  • Structural defects — cracks, included bark, weak branch unions, co-dominant stems
  • Root zone condition — soil compaction, grade changes, construction damage, root decay
  • Pest and disease indicators — emerald ash borer, oak wilt, anthracnose, hypoxylon canker, vertical cracks indicating internal decay
  • Site factors — soil type, drainage, impervious surfaces affecting root health
  • Hazard rating — likelihood of failure and consequence given the target area
  • Written report with findings and recommendations

Common Problems We Find in Lancaster County Trees

Emerald Ash Borer (EAB): Ash trees throughout Lancaster County have been hit hard. EAB signs include D-shaped exit holes, S-shaped galleries under loosened bark, and crown die-back starting at the top. Ash trees within 15 miles of a confirmed EAB find are at risk. Treatment is effective if the tree has over 50% live crown remaining; below that threshold, removal is typically the right call.

Oak Wilt: Less common than EAB but spreading in Pennsylvania. Red oaks are particularly susceptible. Rapid wilting starting in midsummer, often moving from branch tips inward. Treatment options are limited — assessment is most valuable for identifying which neighboring trees are at risk.

Construction damage: New development in Manheim Township, Mount Joy, and Elizabethtown routinely damages root systems of trees that weren't removed. Grade changes, soil compaction from equipment, and root cutting within the drip line cause decline that shows up 2–5 years later. Early intervention with decompaction and amended irrigation can extend a stressed tree's life significantly.

Structural defects: Included bark in major branch unions, co-dominant stems without adequate taper, and old wound compartmentalization that's failing — all increase the risk of sudden branch failure. These show up in trees that look completely fine from the street.

When to Get an Assessment

Proactively: before any construction or renovation within 50 feet of a tree you want to keep. After any major storm event. If a tree has been in visible decline for a season or more. If you're buying or selling a property with significant trees and want to know their condition. If a tree has shown sudden changes — rapid leaf drop, bark cracking, unusual lean.

We'll also tell you if a pre-sale assessment isn't worth the cost for the trees in question — not every tree warrants the investment. The goal is a clear picture, not billable hours.
