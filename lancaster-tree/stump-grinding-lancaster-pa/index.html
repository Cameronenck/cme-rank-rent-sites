Stump Grinding in Lancaster, PA

A stump left in the ground is a problem that doesn't go away on its own. It sprouts suckers, attracts wood-boring insects, creates a trip hazard, and takes up space you could be using. In Lancaster County — where old farmsteads, large lots, and decades-old trees are everywhere — we grind stumps left by other companies, stumps from self-felled trees, and stumps from our own removal jobs.

Stump grinding is fast, effective, and far less disruptive than full stump excavation. The grinder reduces the stump and surface roots to wood chips, which you can use as mulch or have us haul away. The ground settles over a few months, and the space is yours to replant or reseed.

What Stump Grinding Includes

  • Grinding the stump 6–12 inches below grade (enough for grass and most plantings to grow over)
  • Grinding exposed surface roots within 3–4 feet of the stump
  • Backfilling the hole with the generated wood chips
  • Option to haul chips away or spread them as mulch — your call
  • Raking the area level before we leave

The Stump Grinding Process

We use a walk-behind or self-propelled stump grinder depending on stump size and access. Before we start, we check for underground utilities — call 811 is standard practice on all stump jobs. Most residential stumps in Lancaster, Lititz, and Manheim take 20–45 minutes per stump. A big old oak or silver maple stump might run an hour or more depending on root spread.

We cut the stump to as close to ground level as possible with a chainsaw before grinding — this saves grinder time and extends blade life. Grinding goes 6 to 12 inches below the soil surface, which is enough depth to cover cleanly, grow grass over, or plant shrubs. If you're replanting a tree in the same spot, tell us — we'll grind deeper and advise on how long to let the chips decompose before new planting.

After Stump Grinding: What to Expect

The hole left behind fills in over 6–12 months as chips decompose and the soil settles. If you want to reseed quickly, rake out the majority of chips, add topsoil, and seed. For planting shrubs or perennials, let the area settle and decompose for a full season first if possible — fresh chips are nitrogen-depleting and will compete with new plantings.

Surface roots not captured by grinding will decompose on their own over a few years. They won't sprout once the stump is ground below the root flare.

Multi-Stump Discounts

If you have three or more stumps, the per-stump price drops. Moving equipment to the site is a fixed cost — additional stumps on the same property cost much less than the first. If you're cleaning up an old fence line in Ephrata, clearing stumps after a developer took trees, or removing the last remnants of an old orchard in Columbia Township, give us a count and we'll price it as a batch job.
