The Most Common Furnace Repairs I See in Ohio

Igniter replacement is the single most common repair I make. Hot surface igniters are ceramic and brittle — they fail from age, power surges, and the constant heat cycling that Ohio's long heating season demands. When the igniter goes, your furnace tries to light, fails, and shuts down on safety lockout. You'll get short bursts of the blower fan but no heat. Total cost including a service call: $150–$300 depending on the furnace brand and igniter type.

Flame sensor cleaning or replacement is the second call I make most often. The flame sensor is a small metal rod that confirms the burner actually lit. Over time it gets coated with oxidation and can't verify the flame, so the furnace shuts itself down as a safety measure. Sometimes a tech can clean it for less than $100. A full replacement runs $150–$200. If you're getting short heat cycles — furnace lights, runs 30 seconds, shuts off — a dirty flame sensor is the first thing to check.

Draft inducer motor failures are more expensive but common on 15+ year-old Ohio furnaces. The inducer motor pulls combustion gases out through the flue before the burners light. When it fails, the pressure switch can't confirm proper draft and the furnace won't ignite. You'll often hear a grinding or humming sound from the furnace cabinet before it stops working completely. Draft inducer replacements run $350–$700, with parts being the bulk of the cost.

Blower motor issues affect airflow rather than ignition. The blower pushes conditioned air through your duct system. A failing blower runs loud, runs slowly, or doesn't run at all — the furnace fires up but the heat stays trapped in the heat exchanger and triggers the high-limit switch. Blower motor replacement runs $400–$900 depending on motor type (PSC vs ECM) and access difficulty.

Control boards are the brain of the furnace. When a board fails, you can get a wide range of symptoms — intermittent operation, no spark, fans running without heat, or a completely dead system. Control boards for common brands like Carrier, Trane, Lennox, and Rheem typically run $250–$500 for the part, plus 1–2 hours of labor. Total cost: $400–$850.

Heat Exchanger: The Repair That Changes the Conversation

A cracked heat exchanger is in a different category. This is where hot combustion gases (including carbon monoxide) are separated from the air your family breathes. A crack in the heat exchanger is a carbon monoxide leak risk — and a good tech will red-tag the furnace and tell you it can't run until it's fixed or replaced.

Heat exchanger replacement costs $900–$2,200 or more depending on the furnace and whether the manufacturer still makes that part. At that price point, you need to do the math on your system's age and efficiency. A $1,500 heat exchanger repair on a 22-year-old, 80% AFUE furnace is almost never the right call. A new 96% AFUE furnace installed ranges from $2,800 to $4,500 in most Ohio markets — and it starts saving you money on gas bills from day one.

Safety first: If a tech tells you your heat exchanger is cracked, don't run the furnace. Carbon monoxide is odorless and colorless. Get a second opinion if you want — but don't operate a furnace with a confirmed or suspected cracked heat exchanger while you're deciding what to do.

Repair vs. Replace: The 50% Rule

Here's the straightforward way I explain it to homeowners: if the repair cost exceeds 50% of what a new, installed furnace would cost you, replacement is usually the better financial decision — especially if the furnace is over 15 years old.

A new 96% AFUE two-stage furnace installed in a typical Ohio home runs $2,800–$4,200. Fifty percent of that is $1,400–$2,100. So if you're looking at a heat exchanger or blower motor repair that hits that range on a 20-year-old furnace, replacement wins. You get a manufacturer warranty, full efficiency, and another 20+ years of reliable heat.

If the furnace is 8 years old and needs a $300 igniter, obviously you repair it. Age and total repair cost together are what matter.

What Ohio Winters Do to Furnaces

Ohio runs its furnaces hard. Columbus averages 27 heating days at or below freezing per year, and Cleveland is worse. Our furnaces cycle constantly from October through April. That's 6+ months of regular heat-and-cool cycling on every electrical connection, every burner, every motor bearing.

The specific failure patterns I see most in Ohio versus warmer states: earlier igniter burnout from long seasons, more condensate drain issues on high-efficiency furnaces (our cold winters produce a lot of condensation), and faster capacitor degradation from wide temperature swings. It's worth having a licensed tech look at your furnace in October — before the first cold snap — rather than waiting for a January breakdown when every service truck in the state is booked solid.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does furnace repair cost in Ohio?
Most Ohio homeowners pay between $150 and $700 for furnace repairs in 2026, depending on the part and labor involved. Simple fixes like a dirty flame sensor or a failed igniter run $100–$300 total. Mid-range repairs like a pressure switch, draft inducer, or gas valve run $250–$650. Major repairs like a blower motor or control board can reach $500–$1,000. Heat exchanger replacement often exceeds $1,200 and frequently tips the scales toward full replacement instead.
What is the diagnostic fee for furnace repair in Ohio?
Expect to pay $85–$130 for a furnace diagnostic call in Ohio in 2026. Most reputable contractors apply the diagnostic fee toward your repair if you proceed on the same visit. Be cautious of contractors who charge the diagnostic fee on top of all repairs — that's not standard practice in the Ohio market. Emergency or after-hours diagnostic calls typically run $150–$200 or higher.
What are the most common furnace repairs in Ohio and what do they cost?
The most common furnace repairs in Ohio: igniter replacement ($150–$300), flame sensor cleaning or replacement ($80–$200), pressure switch ($120–$280), draft inducer motor ($350–$700), blower motor ($400–$900), control board ($400–$850), gas valve ($300–$600), and heat exchanger ($900–$2,200 — often replacement territory). Parts and labor vary by furnace brand, access difficulty, and your Ohio market.
When should I repair vs replace my furnace in Ohio?
Use the 50% rule: if the repair cost exceeds 50% of what a new furnace would cost installed, replacement is usually the smarter investment — especially if the system is more than 15 years old. A $700 blower motor repair on a 12-year-old furnace is borderline. That same repair on a 22-year-old 80% AFUE furnace should almost certainly be skipped in favor of a new 96% AFUE unit and the energy savings that follow.
Why is my furnace blowing cold air in Ohio?
Cold air from a furnace usually means a failed igniter or dirty flame sensor (furnace tries to light, fails, fan keeps running), overheating from a dirty filter causing the limit switch to cut the burners, a cracked heat exchanger triggering the safety shutoff, or a faulty control board. Start by replacing your air filter. If that doesn't fix it, you need a licensed tech to diagnose the root cause.
How long does furnace repair take in Ohio?
Common repairs — igniter, flame sensor, pressure switch — are completed in 1–2 hours when the tech has the part on the truck. Blower motors, control boards, and draft inducers sometimes require a parts run. Heat exchanger replacements are typically scheduled return visits. Emergency repairs in peak winter are usually same-day through most Ohio HVAC companies.
Does homeowner's insurance cover furnace repair in Ohio?
Standard Ohio homeowners insurance doesn't cover furnace repair from normal wear and tear. Coverage may apply if damage resulted from a covered peril — fire, lightning, or similar event. Home warranty plans typically do cover furnace repairs subject to deductibles and exclusions. Read your warranty contract carefully: many exclude pre-existing conditions and cap covered repair costs.
Can I repair my furnace myself in Ohio?
Homeowners can safely swap an air filter, clear a blocked condensate drain on a high-efficiency unit, and reset a tripped power switch. Beyond that, gas furnace work — burners, heat exchanger, gas valve, electrical components — should be handled by a licensed Ohio HVAC technician. Carbon monoxide leaks from a cracked heat exchanger and gas leaks from improper valve work are real, serious risks. Don't try to save $150 on labor and put your family at risk.
