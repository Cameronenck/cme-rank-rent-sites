Summer AC Cooling vs Furnace Use in Ohio: Which System to Run

Published June 21, 2026 | Updated Today | By Ohio HVAC Pros

One of the most common questions we get from Ohio homeowners is: "Should I be running my furnace in summer?" The answer is simple and absolute: No. Never run your furnace in summer.

In this guide, we'll explain why furnaces and air conditioners are separate systems designed for different seasons, how mixing them damages efficiency and costs you money, and how to properly transition between cooling and heating as Ohio's seasons change.

Why You Should Never Run a Furnace in Summer

A furnace is a heating device. It creates heat by burning natural gas, propane, oil, or using electric resistance coils. That heat is then pushed through your home's ductwork to warm rooms during winter. Running a furnace during summer makes zero sense and causes three major problems:

1. It Cannot Cool Your Home

A furnace generates heat. It has no cooling capacity whatsoever. If you set your thermostat to 'cool' and your furnace is on, the furnace will sit idle because it has no refrigerant, no compressor, and no mechanism to remove heat from your home. Your air conditioner handles all cooling. The furnace simply cannot do this job.

If you're testing your heating system in June and accidentally left it in 'heat' mode, your furnace will blow warm air into your home while your AC tries to cool it. You'll see your electricity bill spike because both systems are fighting each other, wasting thousands of dollars.

2. Energy Waste and Standby Power Loss

Even when idle, furnaces consume energy. Natural gas furnaces keep a pilot light burning all year (unless you manually turn off the gas valve). Electric furnaces and propane models also have control systems drawing minimal power 24/7. During Ohio's hot summers, when you don't need heat, this is pure waste.

A furnace left running during summer can cost $200-500 extra over three months in unnecessary fuel and standby power. In Columbus, Cleveland, Cincinnati, or Toledo, where summer temperatures regularly exceed 85 degrees, there is no scenario where a furnace helps you cool your home.

3. System Interference and Thermostat Confusion

If your thermostat is set to 'auto' mode instead of 'cool' only, your furnace and AC can both activate, depending on temperature swings. Early mornings might trigger the furnace to run heat. Afternoons trigger the AC to run cooling. This creates thermal stress on your ductwork and equipment, shortening component lifespans and increasing repair costs.

Thermostats in 'auto' mode are useful during spring and fall shoulder seasons, but in deep summer (June-August), switch to 'cool' only.

How Your AC System Works (And Why It's Designed for Summer)

Your air conditioning system is purpose-built for summer cooling. Here's the basic process:

The cooling cycle: An AC system uses a compressor to pump refrigerant through an outdoor condenser coil (where heat is released outside) and an indoor evaporator coil (where indoor air is cooled). A fan distributes that cold air through your ducts to cool rooms. The entire process is designed to pull heat out of your home and dump it outside, which is exactly what you need in summer.

Modern AC units in Ohio are sized and configured to handle our specific climate: temperatures in the 85-92 degree range, humidity levels between 50-75%, and cooling demands that peak in July and August. If your AC system is properly maintained and your home is well-insulated, it will handle Ohio's summer cooling needs efficiently without any help from a furnace.

When to Shut Down Your Furnace for Summer

Here's the timeline for safely shutting down your furnace as Ohio transitions into summer:

May 1 - May 15

As overnight temperatures consistently stay above 55 degrees (which typically happens in mid-May), switch your thermostat from 'heat' or 'auto' to 'cool' mode. Your furnace will go into standby. If your system is manually controlled (not a smart thermostat), turn off the furnace at the breaker as well to completely stop energy drain.

May 15 - June 1

Have an HVAC technician perform a spring maintenance check on your air conditioner before peak summer. They'll clean outdoor condenser coils, check refrigerant levels, inspect electrical connections, test the thermostat, and replace air filters. This prevents summer breakdowns when it's hot, humid, and repairs take longer to schedule.

June 1 - September 15

Furnaces remain off. Your AC is your only active HVAC system. Keep your thermostat in 'cool' mode. Replace air filters every 2-3 weeks during peak cooling season (filters clog faster during summer humidity). Monitor your energy bills—if cooling costs spike unexpectedly, have a technician check for refrigerant leaks or compressor issues.

September 15 - October 1

As overnight temperatures start dropping below 65 degrees, have an HVAC contractor perform a fall furnace maintenance check. They'll inspect the heat exchanger for cracks, clean burners, check gas lines (for safety), verify the ignition system, and replace filters before winter. After sitting idle all summer, your furnace needs verification that it will start safely and run correctly.

October 1 Onward

Switch your thermostat back to 'heat' or 'auto' mode. Your furnace is now your primary heating system. Keep the AC on standby in case you get an unusually warm fall day that needs cooling.

The Cost of Running Both Systems Simultaneously

If your furnace and air conditioner run at the same time (which happens on thermostats set to 'auto' with faulty wiring), expect these costs:

For a 2,000 sq ft Ohio home (June through August):

  • AC cooling cost alone: $900-1,500 for the season
  • Furnace running simultaneously: +$300-500 extra
  • Combined system waste: $1,200-2,000 total (vs. $900-1,500 with AC only)

That's an extra $300-500 of unnecessary expense over three months, just because the furnace shouldn't be running in summer.

Pro Tip: Smart Thermostats Prevent This

Smart thermostats like Ecobee and Nest automatically switch between heating and cooling based on outdoor temperature. Set your preferences once (cool when it's above 70 degrees, heat when it's below 65 degrees), and the system handles mode selection for you. No more manually switching thermostat modes each season.

Common Mistakes Ohio Homeowners Make in Summer

Mistake 1: Leaving Thermostat in 'Auto' All Year

This causes furnace and AC to fight each other during spring and fall. Switch to 'cool' only in summer, 'heat' only in winter.

Mistake 2: Not Changing Air Filters Before Summer

A dirty filter from spring forces your AC to work harder, cutting efficiency by 15-30%. Replace filters in late May before peak cooling season.

Mistake 3: Setting Thermostat Too Low

Setting your thermostat to 68 degrees when it's 90 degrees outside forces your AC to run continuously. Set it to 72-75 degrees and save 15% on cooling costs. Add ceiling fans for comfort without lowering temperature.

Mistake 4: Ignoring AC Maintenance in Spring

An AC system that hasn't been serviced in 2+ years runs at 60-70% efficiency by summer. Dirt, mold, and refrigerant leaks accumulate. A spring tune-up costs $150-300 and saves you $500+ in wasted cooling all summer.

What to Do If Your Furnace Won't Stay Off in Summer

If your furnace keeps running even though you've set the thermostat to 'cool' mode, you have one of these problems:

  • Thermostat malfunction: The thermostat is sending heat calls to your furnace even in cool mode. Replace the thermostat battery, recalibrate the temperature sensor, or replace the entire unit.
  • Wiring error: The thermostat wires are crossed, sending conflicting signals. A technician can trace and fix the wiring in 30-60 minutes.
  • Furnace relay stuck: The relay that controls furnace ignition is stuck closed, so the furnace activates regardless of thermostat setting. This requires furnace service.
  • Dual-fuel system misconfig: Some homes have heat pumps with backup furnace heating. If the system isn't programmed correctly, both run in summer. Call your HVAC contractor to verify settings.

Action: Call an Ohio HVAC contractor (Columbus, Cleveland, Cincinnati, Toledo, Akron areas) to diagnose. Don't leave a furnace running in summer while you troubleshoot.

Final Takeaway: AC in Summer, Furnace in Winter

The rule is straightforward:

  • May through September: AC only. Thermostat on 'cool' mode.
  • October through April: Furnace only. Thermostat on 'heat' mode.
  • April and October: Use 'auto' mode only during shoulder seasons when temperatures swing daily.
  • Smart thermostat: Set once, then forget—it handles mode switching automatically.

Running your furnace in summer is like running your car's heater in July with the AC on: inefficient, wasteful, and completely unnecessary. Your air conditioner is specifically designed to handle Ohio's humid, hot summers. Trust it to do its job, maintain it in spring, and set your thermostat to 'cool' only when temperatures rise.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can you use a furnace to cool your home in summer?

No. A furnace is designed to heat air and deliver it through ductwork. It cannot cool air. Running a furnace in summer with the thermostat set to cool mode will waste energy and fail to cool your home. Furnaces generate heat from burning natural gas or electric resistance. They have no cooling capacity. If your air conditioner is broken and you're trying to stay cool using a furnace, you need immediate AC repair, not a furnace run.

Why should I turn off my furnace in summer?

A furnace should be off during summer (typically May through September in Ohio) for three reasons: (1) It wastes energy standing by and consuming pilot light fuel or electric standby power. (2) Running both AC and furnace together confuses your system and prevents proper cooling. (3) Furnace filters and components degrade faster in non-operational states. Most modern HVAC systems are set to 'cool' mode in summer and 'heat' mode in winter automatically. Your thermostat should never call for heat when outside temperatures exceed 70 degrees.

What is the difference between a furnace and an air conditioner?

A furnace heats air and an air conditioner cools it. Furnaces burn fuel (natural gas, propane, oil) or use electric resistance to create heat, then force heated air through ducts into your home. Air conditioners reverse this: they use a compressor and refrigerant to extract heat from indoor air and pump it outside, leaving cooler air behind. Many homes share the same ductwork for both systems (split systems), but they operate independently. In Ohio's humid summers with temperatures reaching 85-92 degrees, you need AC. In winter when it drops to 15-35 degrees, you need the furnace.

Does my furnace run when my AC is on?

No. Your furnace should NOT run when your AC is on. If your thermostat is set to 'cool' mode and your AC unit is working, your furnace remains inactive. If you notice both running simultaneously, you have a thermostat problem or a system malfunction. Check your thermostat settings: the mode should be 'cool' (not 'heat' or 'auto'). If both are running, switch to 'cool only' or turn the furnace off at the breaker. Running both wastes thousands of dollars annually. Call an HVAC contractor to diagnose why your furnace isn't staying off.

When should I turn my furnace back on in fall?

Furnaces should restart in early fall, typically September 15 - October 1 in Ohio, depending on overnight temperatures. If your home dips below 65 degrees at night, switch your thermostat from 'cool' to 'auto' or 'heat'. Many homeowners set the furnace to kick in when outdoor temps drop below 60 degrees. Before turning on your furnace for the first time after summer, have an HVAC technician perform a fall maintenance check: inspect the heat exchanger, clean burners, check gas lines, verify ignition, and replace air filters. A furnace that sat idle all summer needs verification that it will start and run safely.

How much does it cost to run AC all summer in Ohio?

Running AC continuously during Ohio's hot summer months (June-August) costs between 800-2,000 for the season, depending on your home size, AC age, outdoor temperatures, and electricity rates. A modern efficient AC unit in a well-insulated 2,000 sq ft home costs about 40-60 per month to run. Older or undersized units cost 100+ monthly. Reducing thermostat settings by 5 degrees increases costs by roughly 15%. If you maintain your AC regularly, use a smart thermostat, and keep filters clean, you'll stay on the lower end of that range. The alternative furnace heating is not applicable in summer, so AC is your only cooling option.
