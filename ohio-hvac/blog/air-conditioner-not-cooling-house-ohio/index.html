Air Conditioner Not Cooling House? An Ohio HVAC Pro Explains the Fix

Published 2026-05-30 · Ohio HVAC Pros

Home › Blog › Air Conditioner Not Cooling House? An Ohio HVAC Pro Explains the Fix

It's the hottest day of the year and your AC is running — but the house isn't getting any cooler. I've been doing HVAC work in Ohio for years, and this is the call I get more than any other once June hits. The good news is that most of the reasons an air conditioner runs without cooling are diagnosable, and several of them you can address yourself before calling a technician. Here's exactly how I work through this problem, in the order I'd check it on a service call.

Start Here: The Quick Checks

Before anything else, I always verify the obvious stuff — not because homeowners are careless, but because these are the things that get overlooked when you're hot and frustrated.

Thermostat settings. Make sure the thermostat is set to COOL (not just FAN), the set temperature is actually below the current room temperature, and the fan is set to AUTO rather than ON. When the fan runs on ON, it circulates air constantly — including warm air when the compressor isn't running — which makes it feel like the system isn't cooling even when it's working normally.

Circuit breaker. The condenser (outdoor unit) and air handler (indoor unit) are typically on separate breakers. A tripped breaker on the outdoor unit means the fan and compressor aren't running, but the air handler fan might still blow — pushing unconditioned air through the house. Check both breakers and reset any that are tripped. If a breaker trips again immediately, stop and call a technician — there's an underlying electrical problem that needs diagnosis.

Air filter. Pull it out and hold it up to a light source. If you can't see light through it, it needs to be replaced. This is the single most common cause of "AC running but not cooling" calls I go on in Ohio. A choked filter starves the evaporator coil of airflow, causes it to ice over, and stops cooling entirely. I tell every customer: check your filter monthly from May through September in Ohio.

The Frozen Evaporator Coil Problem

If you check the refrigerant line going into your indoor unit and see ice — or if you notice ice on the outdoor unit — your evaporator coil is frozen. This happens when airflow is too low (dirty filter, closed vents, blocked return) or when refrigerant levels are low. A frozen coil can't transfer heat, so the system blows warm air even though everything is "running."

The fix for a frozen coil is to turn the system off (leave the fan on AUTO or switch to fan-only) and let it thaw for 2–4 hours. While it's thawing, replace the filter and open any closed supply or return vents. After it thaws, restart the system. If it freezes again within a few hours, refrigerant is likely the culprit and you need a technician.

Dirty Condenser Coils

The outdoor condenser unit rejects heat from your home to the outside air. When the coil fins get packed with cottonwood fluff, grass clippings, or airborne debris — which is common in Ohio from late spring through summer — heat rejection suffers. The system works harder, runs longer, and still can't keep up on hot days.

Turn off power to the unit at the disconnect box or breaker. With a garden hose, gently rinse the condenser coil fins from the inside out to push debris out of the fins. Don't use a pressure washer — it bends the fins and makes the problem worse. Also make sure nothing is blocking airflow around the unit: vegetation, fencing, or stored items within 2 feet of the condenser all hurt performance. This simple cleaning is something I recommend every Ohio homeowner do once in May before the cooling season starts.

Low Refrigerant

Refrigerant isn't consumed by the system — it's a closed loop. If levels are low, there's a leak somewhere. Signs of low refrigerant include warm air from the vents, ice on the refrigerant lines, a hissing or bubbling sound near the indoor unit, and an electricity bill that's climbing without explanation.

This is a job for a licensed technician. EPA regulations require that refrigerant be handled by certified professionals, and adding refrigerant without finding and fixing the leak is just money down the drain — the refrigerant will leak out again. A proper repair involves pressure-testing the system, locating the leak, making the repair, and then recharging to manufacturer specifications. For homes with older R-22 refrigerant, be aware that R-22 has been phased out and is now expensive — a refrigerant leak on an older R-22 system often tips the math toward full system replacement.

Failing Capacitor

This is one of the most common service calls I go on in Ohio during heat waves. Capacitors are the small cylindrical components that give the compressor and fan motors the electrical jolt they need to start up. When a capacitor starts to fail, the motor struggles to start, the system draws more current, and in many cases the compressor won't start at all — leaving just the indoor fan running and blowing warm air.

Replacing a capacitor is typically a $150–$300 repair including labor. It's quick and it's usually a reliable fix. If you can hear the outdoor unit "humming" but the fan isn't spinning, or the system shuts off quickly after starting, a capacitor is a strong suspect. Don't try to replace it yourself — capacitors hold a potentially dangerous electrical charge even when the system is off.

Undersized System or Heat Load Changes

Sometimes the AC isn't broken — it just can't keep up with the load you're asking it to handle. If you've added square footage, replaced windows, removed insulation, or are dealing with a multi-day Ohio heat wave where outdoor temps are pushing 95°F or higher, even a properly functioning system may struggle. A correctly sized Ohio system is typically designed to maintain a 20°F differential between indoor and outdoor temperature. When it's 97°F outside, you're getting 77°F inside — not 70°F.

If your system genuinely can't keep up on average Ohio summer days (not during extreme heat waves), it may be undersized, have duct leakage losses, or have lost efficiency from age. A load calculation by a licensed contractor will tell you if the system is the right size for the home or if something else is going on.

When to Call a Pro

Run through the checklist above: thermostat, breakers, filter, outdoor unit clearance and cleanliness. If none of those resolve the problem — or if you see ice, hear unusual sounds, or the system trips breakers — it's time to call a licensed Ohio HVAC technician. In Ohio, HVAC contractors are required to hold a state contractor's license, and any technician handling refrigerants must hold EPA Section 608 certification. Ask for both before letting anyone touch your system.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is my air conditioner running but not cooling the house in Ohio?
The most common causes are a dirty air filter restricting airflow, a refrigerant leak causing the system to lose cooling capacity, a frozen evaporator coil, or a failing compressor. A dirty condenser coil — clogged with cottonwood, grass clippings, or debris — is especially common in Ohio summers. Start with a fresh filter and a visual check of the outdoor unit before calling a technician.
How do I know if my AC is low on refrigerant?
Signs include warm air from the vents even when the system is running, ice forming on the refrigerant lines or evaporator coil, a hissing or bubbling sound near the indoor unit, and higher-than-normal electric bills. Refrigerant doesn't get consumed — if levels are low, there's a leak. A licensed Ohio HVAC technician needs to find and fix the leak before recharging the system.
Can a dirty air filter cause an AC not to cool?
Absolutely. A clogged filter restricts the airflow that the evaporator coil needs to absorb heat from your home. Without enough airflow, the coil gets too cold and freezes over, which completely stops cooling. In Ohio, where systems run hard from May through September, I recommend checking your filter monthly and replacing it at least every 60 to 90 days during peak season.
What should I check before calling an HVAC technician in Ohio?
Check the thermostat settings (make sure it's set to COOL and the fan to AUTO), replace the air filter if it's been more than 60 days, verify the circuit breaker hasn't tripped, and check that the outdoor unit isn't covered in debris or cottonwood. Clear at least 2 feet of space around the condenser. If the system still isn't cooling after these checks, call a licensed Ohio HVAC technician.
How much does AC repair cost in Ohio?
AC repair costs in Ohio range from $150 to $900+ depending on the problem. A refrigerant recharge runs $200–$500 depending on the refrigerant type (R-410A vs. R-22) and the amount needed. Replacing a capacitor is typically $150–$300. A compressor replacement is the most expensive common repair at $800–$2,500, and at that price point, replacement of the full system often makes more financial sense.
