Furnace Installation Cost in Ohio:
What You'll Actually Pay in 2026

By Ohio HVAC Pros
May 29, 2026
10 min read

Furnace replacement is one of the bigger home repair decisions Ohio homeowners face, and it rarely comes at a convenient time. Whether your existing unit is limping toward the end of its useful life or you're planning a proactive replacement before winter hits, knowing what this project actually costs — and why quotes vary as much as they do — will help you make a confident decision without overpaying or getting undersold on equipment that won't keep up with an Ohio winter.

Furnace Installation Costs in Ohio: 2026 Breakdown

In my experience across Central and Northern Ohio, a complete furnace installation — equipment, labor, venting, and permit — runs $3,200 to $6,500 for most residential jobs. The spread comes down to efficiency tier, equipment brand, home complexity, and what code upgrades the existing system needs. Here's how it typically shakes out:

Furnace Type Typical Installed Cost Best For
80% AFUE single-stage gas furnace $3,200–$4,200 Budget-conscious replacement, rental properties, mild cold exposure
96% AFUE two-stage gas furnace $4,200–$5,400 Most Ohio homeowners — balances efficiency, comfort, and cost
96–98% AFUE variable-speed gas furnace $5,000–$6,500 Larger homes, homeowners who want maximum comfort and lowest gas bills
Electric furnace (resistance heat) $2,400–$4,000 Homes without gas service — higher operating cost vs. gas
Code upgrades and gas line work (if needed) $300–$1,200 additional Older homes with undersized gas lines or non-compliant flue setups

Ohio code note: High-efficiency 90%+ AFUE furnaces use PVC pipe for intake and exhaust rather than a traditional metal flue. If your home has an older chimney setup, this often means routing new PVC through an exterior wall — a straightforward but additional cost item that not every low-bid quote includes. Confirm what venting work is covered before signing any contract.

What Drives the Price Up or Down

Two quotes on the same job can legitimately differ by $1,000 or more. Here's what actually changes the number:

Equipment Tier

The furnace itself is usually 50–60% of the total job cost. A no-frills 80% AFUE single-stage unit from a mid-tier brand runs the contractor $800–$1,200 wholesale. A premium variable-speed unit with smart thermostat compatibility and a 10-year parts warranty runs $2,000–$2,800 at the distributor. That difference flows straight through to the install quote.

Home Size and Duct Condition

A properly sized furnace requires a Manual J load calculation. Ohio homes have wide variance — a 1940s brick colonial in Cleveland Heights with original storm windows loses heat very differently than a 2010s-built ranch in Dublin with spray foam insulation. If the existing ductwork is undersized, leaky, or poorly balanced, additional duct work adds cost but pays back in comfort and efficiency. Ductwork repair costs in Ohio typically run $300–$900 for targeted fixes, more for major reconfigurations.

Furnace Location and Access

Basement furnaces in open mechanical rooms are the easiest installs — straightforward access, plenty of working space. Furnaces in crawl spaces, tight utility closets, or attic installations take significantly more labor time. Add 15–25% to the labor component for anything outside of standard basement access.

Permits

Ohio municipalities require a mechanical permit for furnace replacement, and most counties require a subsequent inspection. Some contractors roll this into their quote; others line-item it. The permit itself runs $75–$200 depending on your jurisdiction. Never hire a contractor who tells you a permit isn't required — it's legally required in virtually every Ohio municipality, and doing the work without one puts your homeowner's insurance coverage at risk.

Efficiency Ratings and Ohio's Climate

Ohio's winters are real. Columbus sits around 6,000 heating degree days per year, Cleveland and the northern tier push closer to 6,500–7,000 due to lake effect cold. That's a meaningful amount of runtime, and efficiency differences compound quickly over a heating season.

The math is straightforward: an 80% AFUE furnace loses 20 cents of every dollar of gas in exhaust heat. A 96% AFUE unit loses 4 cents. On a $1,200/year gas bill where heat is the majority of consumption, that's roughly $190/year in savings — meaning the efficiency upgrade pays back in 5–8 years on the price difference between tiers, with 15–20 years of additional benefit after that.

My recommendation for most Ohio homeowners: go 96% or higher. The two-stage operation also improves comfort dramatically — lower-stage running on mild Ohio fall and spring days keeps temperatures more consistent and reduces the blast-cycle feeling of single-stage units. If you're planning to stay in the home 10+ years, variable-speed is worth pricing out too.

Signs Your Ohio Furnace Needs Replacement (Not Just Repair)

I get calls every January from homeowners who spent $600 on repairs in October on a furnace that died in a cold snap anyway. Knowing when repair stops making financial sense matters:

  • Age over 18–20 years — gas furnaces in Ohio typically last 18–25 years with regular maintenance. Past 18, parts availability shrinks and failure probability climbs. If it's 20+ years old and needs a heat exchanger, compressor, or control board, you're putting expensive money into equipment that's near end of life.
  • Heat exchanger cracks — a cracked heat exchanger is a carbon monoxide risk. This is not a repair-or-replace decision; a cracked exchanger means replace, full stop. Any reputable Ohio contractor will flag this immediately on inspection.
  • Repair cost exceeds 50% of replacement value — if a repair bill is heading toward $1,500–$2,000 on a furnace that would cost $3,500 to replace, the replacement math is straightforward.
  • Persistent uneven heating — if certain rooms in your Ohio home have been cold for years despite multiple service calls, the furnace may be undersized or the duct system may be fundamentally compromised. A new, properly sized system often fixes what years of band-aid repairs couldn't.
  • Rising gas bills without explanation — if your heating costs have gone up noticeably over the past 2–3 winters and your usage patterns haven't changed, declining efficiency is a likely culprit. A combustion analysis will confirm it.

Rebates and Tax Credits for Ohio Homeowners

The federal 25C energy efficiency tax credit covers up to $600 for a qualifying gas furnace at 95% AFUE or higher, installed in your primary residence. This is a dollar-for-dollar credit against your federal tax bill, not a deduction. Keep the manufacturer's certification statement and your paid invoice — you'll need both when you file.

On the utility side, Columbia Gas of Ohio and AEP Ohio both run rebate programs for high-efficiency heating equipment. Amounts change annually, but $50–$150 per qualifying unit is typical. Your contractor should know what's currently active — if they don't, ask them to check before finalizing the equipment selection.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does furnace installation cost in Ohio?
Most Ohio homeowners pay $3,200 to $6,500 for a complete furnace installation in 2026, including equipment, labor, venting, and permit. A basic 80% AFUE gas furnace with a straightforward swap runs $3,200–$4,200. A high-efficiency 96–98% AFUE variable-speed unit in a more complex installation typically runs $5,000–$6,500. Homes needing gas line upgrades or significant duct work can push higher.
What size furnace do I need for my Ohio home?
Proper sizing requires a Manual J load calculation — not just a square footage estimate. Ohio's cold winters mean undersizing causes real problems. A 1,500 sq ft well-insulated Columbus home might need 60,000–80,000 BTU; an older, poorly insulated home the same size might need 100,000 BTU. Any contractor quoting without asking detailed questions about insulation, windows, ceiling height, and home age is guessing — and guessing on furnace size leads to discomfort, higher bills, and shortened equipment life.
How long does furnace installation take?
A straightforward like-for-like furnace swap in a standard basement installation takes 4 to 6 hours for an experienced crew. Jobs involving flue relining, gas line work, duct modifications, or difficult access locations (crawl space, attic, tight closet) take a full day — 6 to 9 hours. Same-day heat is achievable in most cases.
What's included in a furnace installation in Ohio?
A complete installation covers: equipment delivery and staging, old furnace removal and disposal, new unit installation and commissioning, gas line connection and pressure test, flue venting or new PVC exhaust pipe (for high-efficiency units), thermostat hookup or replacement, duct connection and plenum sealing, startup performance test, and permit filing. Ask contractors to confirm permit costs are included — some quote them separately.
When is the best time to replace a furnace in Ohio?
Late summer or early fall — August through October — is the sweet spot. Contractor availability is better, equipment lead times are shorter, and you're not replacing a broken furnace in a January cold snap when demand spikes and crews are stretched. If your furnace is over 15 years old and had any trouble last winter, proactive summer replacement is almost always the smarter financial call than an emergency swap in the cold.
Gas vs. electric furnace: which is better for Ohio?
Natural gas is the dominant and more cost-effective option for most of Ohio, given the state's gas infrastructure and typical electricity rates. Gas delivers heat at lower cost per BTU than electric resistance. The exception worth considering is a cold-climate heat pump, which can outperform both options at moderate Ohio temperatures — though most Ohio homes still benefit from a gas backup for the coldest weeks of the year. If you're on a propane system and considering a switch, run the numbers with a contractor who can pull current propane vs. utility gas rates for your area.
Are there rebates or tax credits for furnace replacement in Ohio?
Yes. The federal 25C tax credit offers up to $600 for a qualifying high-efficiency gas furnace (95% AFUE or higher) installed in your primary residence — that's a direct credit against your tax liability, not a deduction. Ohio utilities including Columbia Gas of Ohio and AEP Ohio also run rebate programs for high-efficiency equipment, typically $50–$150. Ask your contractor to identify current utility rebates before finalizing equipment selection, and save your invoice and the manufacturer's certification statement for tax filing.
