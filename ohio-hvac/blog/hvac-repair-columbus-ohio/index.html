Most Common HVAC Problems in Columbus

Failed capacitor or contactor. This is probably the single most common AC repair call I run in Columbus during summer. Capacitors are cylindrical components inside the condenser unit that help start and run the compressor and fan motors. They degrade over time and fail more frequently in extreme heat. A bad capacitor means the AC won't start or won't run efficiently. It's a relatively inexpensive fix — $150–$350 fully repaired — and usually a same-day job.

Low refrigerant / slow leak. If your AC is blowing but not cooling well, low refrigerant is high on the suspect list. Refrigerant doesn't "use up" like fuel — if the charge is low, there's a leak somewhere. Patching the leak and recharging the system fixes it, but if the leak is in the evaporator coil, you're looking at a bigger repair. I always find and fix the leak first; just recharging without addressing the source is money wasted.

Frozen evaporator coil. Columbus homes with older ductwork, undersized returns, or dirty air filters are prone to evaporator coil freezing. When airflow is restricted, the coil gets too cold and frost builds up — which then blocks airflow further and can damage the compressor if left running. The fix is usually straightforward: thaw the coil, fix the airflow issue (replace the filter, open closed vents, check duct obstructions), and verify refrigerant levels. If the filter is the culprit, this one's free to prevent — change it every 1–3 months.

Furnace igniter failure. Columbus winters are cold enough that a dead furnace in January is a genuine emergency. Hot surface igniters are the most common ignition type on modern gas furnaces, and they wear out — typically after 3–7 years depending on how hard the furnace runs. The igniter is a relatively inexpensive part, but it needs to be the right one for your furnace model. Most igniter replacements in Columbus run $150–$300 and can be done same day.

Thermostat and wiring issues. Intermittent problems, a system that won't respond to temperature changes, or equipment that short-cycles (turns on and off in rapid succession) are often traced back to thermostat calibration, dead batteries, or wiring issues. Smart thermostat retrofits have introduced some new failure modes too — incorrect wiring for the C-wire (common wire) is something I see regularly on DIY installs. Always verify wiring compatibility before installing a smart thermostat on an older system.

Columbus-Specific Factors That Affect HVAC Wear

Franklin County's climate puts specific demands on HVAC equipment. Summers are humid — Columbus averages around 71% relative humidity in July — which means your AC's dehumidification function matters almost as much as cooling. Systems that are oversized for the home cool fast but don't run long enough to pull adequate moisture out of the air. The result is a house that feels clammy even when the temperature reads fine. Proper sizing at installation prevents this; an oversized replacement doesn't fix it.

Columbus also sits in a region with meaningful heating seasons. Average January lows in the mid-20s mean furnaces work hard from November through March. Systems that don't get annual maintenance going into fall show more mid-season failures — dirty heat exchangers, worn belts and bearings, and neglected filters that restrict airflow. A pre-season tune-up in October runs $80–$150 and consistently prevents the $400+ repairs that come from deferred maintenance.

Repair vs. Replace: How to Think About It in Columbus

Here's the framework I use with Columbus homeowners. If a system is under 10 years old and the repair is under 35% of what replacement would cost, repair almost always wins. If the system is 14–16 years old and you're looking at a major component failure — compressor, heat exchanger, evaporator coil — the math usually points toward replacement. You're spending serious money on equipment that's past its median lifespan, without any improvement in efficiency or reliability.

The 614 service area has a lot of homes built in the 1990s and early 2000s, which means a significant number of HVAC systems in Columbus are approaching or past replacement age right now. If your system is on its second or third major repair, it's telling you something. A new system installed with proper sizing today will run more efficiently, qualify for available tax credits, and run on current refrigerants — not the phased-out R-22 or transitional R-410A.
