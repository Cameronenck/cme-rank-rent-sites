Boiler Installation Cost in Ohio: 2026 Pricing Guide

Published 2026-05-31 · Ohio HVAC Pros

Home › Blog › Boiler Installation Cost in Ohio: 2026 Pricing Guide

I've installed and replaced boilers across Ohio for years — in older Cleveland doubles, Columbus colonials, and rural farmhouses with cast-iron radiators that have been heating those homes since the 1950s. Boiler work is a specialty, and the pricing reflects that. Here's a straight answer on what a boiler installation costs in Ohio right now, and what drives those numbers up or down.

Boiler Installation Cost in Ohio

For a complete boiler installation in Ohio — new equipment, labor, permits, and basic piping connections — expect to pay in these ranges:

  • Standard efficiency gas boiler (80–84% AFUE), single zone: $3,500–$5,500 installed
  • High-efficiency condensing gas boiler (90–95%+ AFUE), single zone: $4,500–$7,500 installed
  • Oil boiler replacement: $4,000–$7,000 installed
  • Combi boiler (heat + domestic hot water, no separate water heater needed): $5,000–$9,000 installed
  • Multi-zone boiler system (adding zones or converting): $7,000–$12,000+

These are installed prices — equipment, labor, mechanical permit, and standard connections. They assume the existing hydronic distribution (pipes, baseboards, radiators) is in serviceable condition. If the distribution system needs significant work, that's priced separately after the assessment.

What Drives the Cost Up or Down

BTU output and boiler size. A boiler has to be properly sized to the home — undersized and it runs constantly without hitting setpoint; oversized and it short-cycles, wasting fuel and wearing out faster. I always do a Manual J heat load calculation before sizing a replacement. For a 1,500 sq ft Ohio home, you're typically looking at an 80,000–100,000 BTU unit. Larger homes need more output, which costs more in equipment.

High-efficiency vs. standard efficiency. High-efficiency condensing boilers cost $500–$1,500 more upfront than standard models. The condensing unit extracts extra heat from flue gases and vents through PVC rather than a metal flue — which also means a new venting run in many installs, adding to labor. In Ohio, with a heating season running October through April, the fuel savings from a 95% AFUE boiler vs. an 82% model are real over time. Whether the payback period makes sense depends on your gas rates and how long you plan to stay in the home.

Zoning. Single-zone systems are the simplest install. If you want to add zones — separate thermostatic control for different areas of the house — that means additional zone valves, thermostats, and wiring. Each zone adds $400–$900 to the job. Multi-zone systems are more complex but give you real comfort and efficiency gains in two-story homes where heat rises unevenly.

Condition of existing piping. On a boiler swap in an older Ohio home, I'm inspecting the distribution system before I commit to a price. Corroded pipes, failing circulator pumps, waterlogged expansion tanks — these get found and addressed. If the pipes are older cast iron in good shape, we typically leave them. If there's significant sludge buildup, a power flush is added to the scope. Don't let any contractor quote a boiler replacement without looking at what the boiler connects to.

Oil-to-gas conversion. Many Ohio homeowners with oil boilers want to convert to natural gas when they replace. That conversion adds cost — running a new gas line, removing the oil tank (which has its own regulations), and ensuring proper venting. Budget an additional $800–$2,500 for a fuel conversion on top of boiler replacement cost. It's usually worth it; natural gas in Ohio is consistently cheaper than heating oil.

Combi boilers. A combination boiler handles space heating and domestic hot water from a single unit, eliminating the separate water heater. These are popular in smaller Ohio homes and condos where space is limited. They cost more upfront but can save on water heating costs. One thing to know: combi boilers work better in homes with lower simultaneous hot water demand — they're not ideal for large households running multiple showers at once.

Signs Your Ohio Boiler Needs Replacement

Repair or replace is a judgment call, and I'll give you the honest answer when I'm standing in front of the equipment. These are the signs that tip toward replacement:

  • The boiler is 25+ years old — parts availability gets thin and efficiency has degraded
  • Repair cost exceeds 50% of replacement cost (the "half the value" rule)
  • Persistent leaks at the heat exchanger — these don't patch cleanly
  • Yellow or flickering pilot flame — combustion issues that get worse, not better
  • Radiators heating unevenly after bleeding and balancing attempts
  • Fuel bills climbing despite no change in usage — the boiler is losing efficiency
  • Frequent pressure relief valve discharges

For boiler repairs that make financial sense — a failed circulator pump, a faulty pressure relief valve, a bad thermocouple — repair is the right call. See our guide on boiler repair costs in Ohio for typical repair pricing.

Boiler Brands We Install in Ohio

The brands I install most are Weil-McLain, Burnham, Peerless, and Navien for high-efficiency and combi applications. These are well-supported lines with parts availability throughout Ohio — that matters when something needs service five years from now. I've seen lesser-known brands cause headaches for homeowners when parts become unavailable. Stick with established manufacturers and you're protected.

Ohio Rebates and Efficiency Incentives

Columbia Gas of Ohio offers rebates on qualifying high-efficiency boilers — typically $200–$400 for units meeting 90% AFUE or higher. Dominion Energy Ohio has similar programs. The federal tax credit for high-efficiency HVAC equipment (25C) covers 30% of equipment cost up to $600 for qualifying boilers. Check current Columbia Gas and Dominion rebate portals before you buy — these programs have enrollment windows and budget caps.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does it cost to install a boiler in Ohio?
Boiler installation in Ohio typically runs $3,500 to $8,500 total, installed. That range covers the boiler unit itself plus labor, permits, and any necessary piping or zone valve work. High-efficiency condensing boilers with multiple zones or significant piping modifications push costs toward $8,000 and above. Standard single-zone replacements on existing hydronic systems land in the $3,500 to $5,500 range.
How long does a boiler last in Ohio?
A well-maintained boiler in Ohio typically lasts 20 to 30 years. That's longer than a forced-air furnace, which averages 15 to 20 years. Annual tune-ups — checking the heat exchanger, testing pressure relief valves, bleeding radiators, and inspecting the flue — are the single biggest factor in reaching that upper end of the lifespan. Neglected boilers fail faster and waste fuel in the meantime.
Is a boiler more efficient than a furnace for Ohio homes?
High-efficiency condensing boilers reach AFUE ratings of 95% or higher, which is competitive with top-tier furnaces. The real advantage of hydronic heat is comfort — radiant warmth from baseboards or in-floor tubing doesn't dry out the air the way forced air does, and there's no ductwork to lose heat through. For Ohio's long heating seasons, the consistent, even warmth of a boiler system is hard to beat for occupied living spaces.
Can I replace just the boiler without replacing all the pipes?
In most cases, yes. If your existing piping, radiators, and baseboards are in reasonable condition, a boiler replacement typically involves swapping out the heat source and updating the connections, circulator pump, and controls. We do a full assessment of the distribution system before any boiler install — if the pipes have significant corrosion or the baseboards are undersized for the new unit's output, we'll flag that upfront.
Do boilers require permits in Ohio?
Yes. Boiler installation in Ohio requires a mechanical permit in virtually every jurisdiction. The work must be performed by a licensed contractor and is subject to inspection. This is non-negotiable — a boiler installed without a permit creates liability issues for the homeowner and can complicate home sales. Any licensed HVAC contractor will handle the permit process as part of the installation.
