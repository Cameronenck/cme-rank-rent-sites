Boiler Installation Cost in Ohio: 2026 Pricing Guide

Published 2026-05-31 · Ohio HVAC Pros

Home › Blog › Boiler Installation Cost in Ohio: 2026 Pricing Guide

Boilers are less common than forced-air furnaces in Ohio, but they're the primary heat source in a significant portion of older Ohio homes — particularly in Cleveland, Columbus, Cincinnati, and other cities with substantial housing stock from the early-to-mid 20th century. When a boiler fails or needs replacement, understanding what installation actually costs and what's driving the price helps you make a decision without getting taken.

Boiler Installation Cost in Ohio

For most Ohio homes, boiler replacement or new installation runs:

  • Standard gas boiler (80% AFUE, residential): $3,500–$6,500 installed
  • High-efficiency gas boiler (90%+ AFUE, condensing): $5,000–$9,000 installed
  • Oil boiler replacement: $4,500–$8,500 installed
  • Combination (combi) boiler (heat + hot water): $4,000–$8,000 installed
  • Steam boiler (older systems): $4,500–$9,500 installed

These are installed prices including equipment, labor, and standard connections. They don't include system-specific add-ons like zone valve replacement, radiator work, expansion tank upgrades, or flue modifications — which vary by the condition of the existing system.

What Drives Boiler Installation Cost in Ohio

System type matters significantly. Ohio has a mix of hot water boiler systems (hydronic radiant, baseboard, or radiators) and steam boiler systems. Steam boilers are more common in pre-1950 housing stock in cities like Cleveland and Cincinnati. Steam systems require specific boiler types, proper sizing to the system volume, and more careful commissioning — they're more expensive to install correctly than hot water systems.

Existing system condition. When replacing a boiler, the existing distribution system — pipes, radiators, baseboards, zone valves — affects cost. If the distribution is in good condition, replacement is straightforward. Corroded pipes, failing zone valves, or expansion tanks that need replacement add to the total project cost. A qualified contractor inspects the full system before quoting.

Efficiency tier choice. Standard-efficiency gas boilers (80% AFUE) use a natural draft or power-vent system that vents through the existing chimney. High-efficiency condensing boilers (90%+ AFUE) require PVC or CPVC vent pipes through an exterior wall — they don't work with masonry chimneys. The venting modification is typically $300–$800 but is required for condensing equipment. Over time, the efficiency gain pays back the additional cost in Ohio's climate.

Boiler sizing. Boilers need to match the heating load of the home and the distribution system. Oversized boilers short-cycle, reducing efficiency and component life. Undersized boilers can't maintain comfort in Ohio's coldest weather. Proper sizing requires a heat loss calculation — not just matching the old boiler's BTU output.

Federal Tax Credits for High-Efficiency Boilers in Ohio

Under the Inflation Reduction Act, homeowners installing qualifying high-efficiency boilers can claim a 30% tax credit up to $600 on their federal taxes. To qualify, the boiler typically needs to be ENERGY STAR certified with an AFUE of 97% or higher for gas systems. Ask your contractor specifically which models qualify — the threshold is stricter than for heat pumps.

Ohio natural gas rates are among the lower rates in the Midwest, which affects the payback calculation on high-efficiency equipment. Higher efficiency boilers cost more upfront but use less gas — the payback period in Ohio depends on your current equipment efficiency and local gas pricing.

Hot Water vs. Steam: Ohio Considerations

Hot water (hydronic) systems are the more modern standard and more efficient. Condensing boilers work with hot water systems. Zoning is straightforward. If you're replacing a hot water boiler in Ohio and the system is in good shape, you have the full range of modern boiler options available.

Steam systems in Ohio's older housing stock require specific replacement boilers sized to the existing pipe and radiator volume. Modern condensing technology generally doesn't apply to steam. The replacement options are more limited, and proper commissioning matters more — a steam boiler that's improperly set up creates water hammer, uneven heat distribution, and premature boiler failure. This is not a job for a generalist HVAC contractor.

How Long Boilers Last in Ohio

Well-maintained gas boilers typically last 20–30 years in Ohio. Cast iron boilers can last longer. Annual service — cleaning the heat exchanger, checking the burner, testing safety controls, and checking water chemistry — is the single biggest factor in boiler longevity. If your current boiler is over 20 years old and having its first major issue, replacement is usually more cost-effective than major repairs on aging equipment.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does it cost to replace a boiler in Ohio?
Boiler replacement in Ohio typically runs $3,500–$6,500 for a standard gas boiler and $5,000–$9,000 for a high-efficiency condensing boiler, installed. Steam boiler replacement runs $4,500–$9,500 depending on system size and condition. Accurate quotes require a site inspection of the full system — call 614-344-4851 for licensed HVAC contractors in your area.
Is a boiler or furnace more efficient in Ohio?
Modern high-efficiency condensing boilers (97% AFUE) and high-efficiency furnaces (96%+ AFUE) are comparable in efficiency. Radiant heat from a boiler system tends to provide more even comfort than forced air — many Ohio homeowners with older boiler systems prefer the heat quality and choose to stay with boilers when replacing equipment. The operating cost difference at comparable efficiency ratings is minimal.
How long does boiler installation take in Ohio?
A standard boiler replacement in an Ohio home takes 1–2 days for hot water systems. Steam boiler replacement and commissioning may take 2–3 days. More complex projects involving venting modifications, zone valve replacement, or pipe work add time.
Can I convert from a boiler to forced air in Ohio?
Yes, but it's a major project — you'd be adding ductwork throughout the home, which requires significant construction work. For most Ohio homes with functioning radiator or baseboard distribution, replacing the boiler in kind is far less expensive than converting to forced air. Conversion makes more sense during a major renovation when walls are already open.
Are there tax credits for boiler installation in Ohio in 2026?
Yes. The federal Inflation Reduction Act provides a 30% tax credit (up to $600) for qualifying high-efficiency boiler installations. To qualify for the full credit, the boiler typically needs an AFUE of 97% or higher (for gas). Ask your HVAC contractor which specific models meet the IRA requirements before purchasing.
