Why ACs Fail in Columbus During Peak Heat

Capacitor failure. This is the most common emergency call I run all summer. The capacitor is a cylindrical component inside your outdoor condenser unit that provides the startup voltage for your compressor and fan motor. High ambient temperatures accelerate capacitor degradation — the hotter it gets outside, the harder they work, and the faster they fail. A Columbus heat wave in July and August puts serious stress on aging capacitors. The symptoms are recognizable: the outdoor unit hums but the fan doesn't spin, the system short-cycles, or it simply doesn't start. This is almost always a same-day repair with parts on the truck.

Tripped circuit breaker. Before you call anyone, check your electrical panel. A struggling compressor — usually one that's drawing higher amperage due to age, a worn capacitor, or low refrigerant — will often trip the AC breaker. Reset it once. If it trips again within a few minutes of the system running, that's a sign there's an underlying electrical or mechanical problem and you should leave it off and call for service. Running on a repeatedly tripping breaker can damage the compressor.

Frozen evaporator coil. If your AC is running constantly but not cooling, and you notice ice or frost on the refrigerant lines near the air handler, you likely have a frozen coil. This happens when airflow across the coil is restricted — usually a clogged air filter, closed supply vents, or collapsed flex duct. The fix starts with shutting the system down (or switching to fan-only) to let it thaw, then correcting the airflow restriction before restarting. Don't run a frozen system — it puts load on the compressor and can turn a $150 service call into a much larger repair.

Refrigerant leak. If the system is running but the air coming out of your vents isn't getting cold enough — especially if it was keeping up fine earlier in the season — a slow refrigerant leak may have finally dropped the charge below the operating threshold. Refrigerant doesn't consume itself; if it's low, it leaked out somewhere. A proper repair finds and fixes the leak before recharging. Recharging without fixing the leak is money out the window — you'll be back in the same situation within weeks or months.

What to Do While Waiting for the Technician

There are a few things worth checking before the tech arrives that can either resolve the problem or give them useful information when they show up:

  • Check the air filter. A completely choked filter restricts airflow enough to cause coil freeze and shutdown. If it's visibly clogged, replace it and see if the system recovers after 30–60 minutes.
  • Check the circuit breaker. Reset it once if it's tripped. If it trips again immediately, leave it off and report that to the technician — it tells them where to start looking.
  • Check the outdoor unit. Make sure nothing is blocking the condenser — overgrown shrubs, a tarp that blew onto it, debris from a storm. The condenser needs unobstructed airflow on all sides to reject heat properly.
  • Switch to fan-only mode. If the coil is frozen, running fan-only (no cooling) will thaw it without forcing the compressor to work. The tech will diagnose why it froze once they arrive.
  • Close blinds on south and west windows and move to the lowest floor of the house, where heat rises away from you. Have water available and a plan for anyone vulnerable if temperatures climb past safe levels.
