When Is It Actually an Emergency?

Ohio's weather is unpredictable in ways that make HVAC emergencies genuinely dangerous, not just uncomfortable. The state sees polar vortex events that drive temperatures below 0°F in Columbus and Cleveland. It also sees summer heat advisories where heat index values hit 105-110°F in Cincinnati and Dayton. In both cases, HVAC failure stops being a schedule disruption and starts being a health and safety issue.

Gas Smell or CO Alarm? Evacuate First.

If you smell gas or your carbon monoxide detector sounds, leave the home immediately without touching light switches. Call 911 from outside. Call your gas utility company. Only contact an HVAC contractor after the gas company has confirmed safety. No HVAC repair is worth risking carbon monoxide exposure.

What Counts as an HVAC Emergency in Ohio

Winter Heating Emergencies

  • Furnace or heat pump completely failed with outdoor temps below 32°F
  • No heat with elderly residents (65+), infants, or health-compromised individuals in the home
  • No heat with outdoor temps below 20°F (pipe freeze risk within hours)
  • Carbon monoxide alarm triggered by HVAC equipment
  • Gas smell anywhere near furnace or HVAC equipment
  • Visible smoke or burning smell from furnace

Summer Cooling Emergencies

  • AC failure during an NWS heat advisory (heat index above 103°F)
  • AC failure in homes with elderly residents, very young children, or medical conditions sensitive to heat
  • Indoor temperatures above 85°F with no relief possible

What to Do While Waiting for the Technician

Heating Emergency Actions

  1. Check your thermostat first — verify settings, replace batteries if needed
  2. Check your air filter — a completely clogged filter can trigger furnace safety shutoff
  3. Check the circuit breaker for the furnace
  4. Look for the furnace's power switch (resembles a light switch, on or near the furnace)
  5. If none of the above fix it, set up portable electric space heaters in the most-used rooms
  6. Close interior doors to contain heat to occupied spaces
  7. Open cabinet doors under sinks on exterior walls to prevent pipe freeze
  8. Let faucets drip slightly on exterior walls to prevent pipes from freezing

Cooling Emergency Actions

  1. Check circuit breaker and thermostat settings
  2. Check if the outdoor condenser unit is running — if not, look for a tripped breaker at the unit itself
  3. Move to the lowest level of the house — heat rises, lower floors are cooler
  4. Close all blinds and curtains to block solar heat gain
  5. Use fans to move air — ceiling fans in counterclockwise direction push air down and create cooling effect
  6. Stay hydrated; heat exhaustion risk is real at indoor temperatures above 85°F
  7. Consider a hotel for vulnerable household members if indoor temperatures reach 90°F+

Ohio Polar Vortex Events — Why They Matter

Ohio has experienced multiple polar vortex events in the last decade that drove wind chills below -30°F in some areas. During the January 2019 polar vortex, Columbus saw lows of -15°F — wind chill down to -50°F in some areas. HVAC demand across the state surged simultaneously, meaning contractor response times stretched to 6-12 hours even for premium emergency services.

This is precisely why annual maintenance matters. The furnaces that failed during those events were overwhelmingly those that hadn't been serviced. A combustion issue that would have been caught in a $150 fall tune-up became an emergency during the worst possible conditions.

Emergency HVAC FAQ for Ohio Homeowners

When does HVAC failure count as an emergency in Ohio?
In Ohio, no heat below 32°F outside qualifies as a genuine emergency, especially with elderly residents, young children, or pets in the home. AC failure during an official heat advisory (heat index above 103°F) also qualifies. Gas smell, CO alarm, or smoke from HVAC equipment are always emergencies — call 911 first.
How much does emergency HVAC repair cost in Ohio?
Emergency after-hours HVAC repair typically costs $50 to $150 more than standard daytime service, plus the actual repair cost. Most furnace repairs run $150-$600 including emergency premium; AC repairs run $150-$500. The premium is worth paying to avoid 12 hours in dangerous temperature extremes.
What should I do while waiting for emergency HVAC help in Ohio?
For heating: use electric space heaters, close interior doors, drip faucets near exterior walls. For cooling: move to the lowest floor, close blinds, use fans, stay hydrated. Check your thermostat, filter, and breaker before calling — the fix is sometimes that simple.
What if I smell gas from my furnace in Ohio?
Leave the home immediately. Do not turn any electrical switches on or off. Do not use your phone inside. Once outside, call 911, then your gas utility company. Only call an HVAC contractor after the gas company has confirmed safety. This is not a situation where you troubleshoot first.
Are Ohio HVAC emergency contractors available on holidays?
Most emergency HVAC contractors in Ohio operate 365 days per year, including Christmas, Thanksgiving, and New Year's. After-hours and holiday rates apply, but service is available. Ohio HVAC Pros maintains a network of contractors who staff emergency response year-round.
Can HVAC emergencies be prevented in Ohio?
Most Ohio HVAC emergencies come from deferred maintenance. Annual tune-ups (spring AC, fall furnace) catch failing components before they fail at the worst possible moment. A $150 maintenance visit consistently prevents $400+ emergency repair calls. The math strongly favors maintenance.
