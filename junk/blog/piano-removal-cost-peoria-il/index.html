Why Piano Removal Is a Specialty Job

A standard upright piano weighs 400 to 600 pounds. A baby grand tips the scale at 500 to 700 pounds. Full concert grands can exceed 1,200 pounds. None of these move the way a couch or a refrigerator moves. Uprights have to stay mostly vertical or the soundboard and internal components can shift and crack. Grand pianos require the legs and lid to come off before the body can be placed on a specialized skid board.

I've seen plenty of homeowners try to recruit a few buddies and a borrowed truck for this job. It almost never ends well — scraped hardwood floors, gouged doorframes, or worse, someone gets hurt. Piano removal requires moving blankets, piano dollies with proper wheel locks, stair-climbing equipment for multi-level moves, and at minimum two experienced people who have done it before. That's what drives the cost, and it's why the cost is worth it.

Stairs Are the Biggest Cost Factor

A ground-floor piano is a straightforward haul: roll it out, load it up, done. A basement piano is a different calculation entirely. Getting 500 pounds of cast iron, wood, and felt up a staircase — especially if it turns — requires proper rigging, stair-climbing dollies, and an extra set of hands. The same goes for second-floor pianos. Expect to add $75 to $150 to the base price for any job involving a full flight of stairs.

Doorway and Hallway Width

Older homes in Peoria — particularly in the Near North Side, Moss-Bradley, and Richwoods neighborhoods — often have narrower interior doorways than modern construction. A standard upright is around 58 inches wide. Most interior doorways are 32 to 36 inches. We have to tilt and angle the piano through the opening, which takes more time and a practiced technique. If your home has particularly narrow hallways or tight turns, mention that when you call so we can quote accurately.

Can You Donate an Old Piano in Peoria?

If the piano is in playable condition, we always try to find it a home before disposal. Local schools, churches, community centers, and music programs in Peoria are often looking for working instruments. We reach out to our usual contacts before loading it on the disposal truck. Not every piano can be placed — severely out-of-tune instruments, ones with broken keys, water-damaged soundboards, or moth-eaten felts are typically past the point of usefulness — but if yours is functional, there's a real shot at it going somewhere it'll be used.

If donation isn't possible, pianos are disassembled and materials sorted for recycling and disposal at a licensed facility. The cast iron harp is scrap metal. The wood casing and cabinet go through proper channels. We don't dump them.

What About Piano Buyers?

Before you call for removal, it's worth a quick check online. Vintage uprights and working baby grands occasionally have resale value — sometimes enough to offset or eliminate removal costs entirely if a buyer picks it up. Websites like Facebook Marketplace and Craigslist are worth checking, but be realistic: free is the most common price point for used upright pianos in Peoria, and even then, you often can't give them away. If it's been sitting in your basement for a decade and nobody has played it, removal is almost certainly the right call.
