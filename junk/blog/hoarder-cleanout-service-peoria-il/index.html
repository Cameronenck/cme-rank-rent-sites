What the Hoarder Cleanout Process Looks Like

We don't show up with a crew and start ripping things out. Here's how a well-run cleanout actually works:

Step 1 — Walkthrough and Estimate

Before any work begins, I or one of my crew leads walks the entire property. We look at every room, every hallway, every corner. We want to understand what we're dealing with — how much is there, what kinds of materials, whether there are heavy items mixed in, and whether there are any access challenges. At the end of the walkthrough, we give you a price. No surprises on day two.

Step 2 — Sorting and Staging (If Needed)

If you want to keep certain items, we set up a keep zone — usually a clean room or the front lawn — where you or a family member can flag things to hold back. We work around that. What doesn't get flagged gets loaded. If you're not on-site and have pre-authorized the cleanout, we remove only what we've agreed to remove and document the job with photos as we go.

Step 3 — Removal and Load-Out

The crew loads everything systematically — box by box, bag by bag, piece by piece. We don't just shove things in; we load efficiently so each truckload is as full as possible and we're not making unnecessary trips. In a packed house, this is skilled work. Navigating narrow paths, working around structural debris, and maintaining safe footing takes experience.

Step 4 — Sweep and Final Walkthrough

Once the agreed scope is clear, we do a sweep pass and walk you through the space before we leave. You can flag anything we missed. We want the job done right, not just done fast.

What We Can — and Can't — Remove

We handle the full range of household junk that accumulates in hoarding situations: furniture, clothing, bags and boxes, electronics, appliances, yard debris brought inside, books and paper, cookware, sporting goods, and general clutter of every kind.

Things we can't haul without special handling include:

  • Paint cans, chemicals, and solvents (Peoria has a household hazardous waste facility for these)
  • Propane tanks and compressed gas cylinders
  • Active animal waste or biological contamination — we'll refer you to a certified biohazard team
  • Large quantities of gasoline or motor oil

If we come across any of these during the job, we'll flag them and tell you exactly what needs to happen. We won't just skip over it and pretend it's not there.
