What Happens When You Inherit a House in the Midwest?

May 11, 2026 SellMidwestHome Editorial Team

Inheriting a house is one of the most significant and emotionally complex property events most people will ever face. Along with the grief of losing someone, you're suddenly responsible for a piece of real estate you may not have planned for — often in a different city, potentially in rough condition, and with siblings or other co-heirs who have different opinions about what to do with it.

The First 30 Days After Inheriting a Property

Before you can make any decisions about the house, you have a few practical steps to handle — and some of them have real deadlines.

Secure the property immediately. If the home is vacant, change the locks. Vacant homes are targets for vandalism, squatters, and theft — especially if it's known in the neighborhood that the owner recently passed. Make sure utilities are either active (to prevent frozen pipes in Illinois winters) or safely shut off.

Notify the insurance company. Homeowner's insurance policies often contain clauses that void coverage if a home is vacant for more than 30–60 days. Call the insurer immediately to inform them of the change in ownership and get a vacant property rider if needed. Without active coverage, a fire or water damage event during the estate process could be financially devastating.

Get the deed into the right name. You cannot sell, refinance, or make legal decisions about the property until ownership is properly transferred. This typically happens through probate — a court-supervised process — unless the property was in a trust or held in joint tenancy with right of survivorship.

Start a property expense log. Property taxes, insurance, utilities, and maintenance costs continue during the estate process regardless of who pays them. Track every dollar spent — these costs are often reimbursable from the estate or deductible on the final estate tax return.

Probate in Illinois: What Heirs Need to Know

If the deceased didn't set up a living trust, the property will almost certainly need to go through probate before you can legally sell it. Illinois probate is a judicial process supervised by the county circuit court.

The typical Illinois probate timeline runs 6 to 12 months for straightforward estates. More complicated estates — contested wills, multiple heirs, outstanding debts, or unclear ownership history — can stretch to 18 months or longer. During this time, no heir can independently sell or transfer the property without court authorization.

Illinois does offer a simplified small estate affidavit process for estates where the total value of personal property is under $100,000. Real estate is not eligible for this simplified process — if there's a house involved, standard probate is required unless the property was held in trust.

Your Three Options: Keep, Rent, or Sell

Once ownership is settled, you have three basic paths. Here's an honest breakdown of each:

Keep it as a primary residence. This makes sense only if you actually want to live there, the home is in reasonable shape, and you can afford the ongoing costs (mortgage if one exists, taxes, insurance, maintenance). Moving into an inherited home can be emotionally difficult. Be realistic about whether you want the home or whether you're holding onto it because letting go feels like a second loss.

Rent it as an investment property. If the home is in a decent rental market and you have landlord capacity — or budget to hire a property manager — holding and renting can generate income. The challenge: inherited homes often need updates before they're rent-ready, and becoming a landlord is a business decision with real management demands. If you live out of state, factor in property management fees (8–12% of monthly rent) before running the numbers.

Sell it. For most heirs, especially those who are dealing with grief, live far away, or have co-heirs, selling is the cleanest path. Selling converts the asset to cash that can be easily divided, eliminates ongoing carrying costs, and lets everyone move on. Tax Implications of an Inherited Property

Taxes are one area where inherited property is actually more favorable than people expect — thanks to a rule called the stepped-up basis.

When you inherit a property, your cost basis for tax purposes is reset to the property's fair market value on the date of the decedent's death — not what the deceased originally paid. This means if your parent bought the home in 1985 for $60,000, and it was worth $240,000 when they passed, your basis is $240,000.

If you sell the home for $240,000 shortly after inheriting it, you owe zero capital gains tax — because your basis equals your sale price. If you wait and the property appreciates to $280,000 before you sell, you'd only owe capital gains on the $40,000 of appreciation after the inheritance date, not the full $220,000 of gain from the original purchase.

The practical implication: if you're going to sell, selling sooner rather than later generally minimizes your tax exposure. The longer you hold, the more appreciation accrues above your stepped-up basis.

Selling an Inherited House As-Is

Most inherited homes carry deferred maintenance — a reality of owning a property for 30 or 40 years and then passing away. Roofs age, HVAC systems wear out, kitchens and bathrooms haven't been updated since the 1990s. That's completely normal, and it doesn't mean the home isn't worth selling.

Selling as-is means you don't make repairs before the sale. The price reflects current condition. For most heirs managing an estate remotely, dealing with grief, and juggling co-heir dynamics, as-is is the right call. The cost of managing repairs from a distance, coordinating contractors, and waiting for work to be completed often exceeds any price premium a fixed-up home might earn.
