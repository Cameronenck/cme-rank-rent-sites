How We Determine a Fair Cash Offer for Your Home

May 11, 2026 SellMidwestHome Editorial Team

We believe in transparency. When we make a cash offer on your home, we want you to understand exactly how we arrived at that number — so you can evaluate it clearly, compare it to other options, and make the best decision for your situation. There are no algorithms here, no mystery, and no games designed to lowball you into a bad deal.

The Variables That Drive Your Property's Value

Every property's value starts with the same four fundamentals, and no amount of technology can substitute for an honest assessment of each:

Location. In real estate, this isn't a cliché — it's a calculation. School district boundaries, proximity to employment centers, neighborhood sales velocity, and crime data all affect what a fully renovated home in your area can realistically sell for. A 3-bedroom ranch in Peoria's North Side sells for a different number than a nearly identical property in a rural county 30 miles away. We look at the specific block, not just the city.

Size and configuration. Square footage is the baseline, but configuration matters too. A 1,400 sq ft home with 3 bedrooms and 2 bathrooms commands a different price than a 1,400 sq ft home with 2 bedrooms and 1 bathroom. We note bedroom count, bathroom count, garage, and basement (finished vs. unfinished) because buyers do.

Condition. We assess condition honestly — not to find reasons to lower our offer, but because repair costs are a real variable in our math. A home with a functioning 5-year-old roof costs us nothing to address. A home with a failed 25-year-old roof that's actively leaking costs $8,000–$18,000 to fix. That difference has to be in the offer math somewhere, or we'd lose money on every deal.

Recent comparable sales. What have similar homes actually sold for in your area in the last 90 days? Not listed — sold. This is the anchor for everything else we do.

Our Comparable Sales Process

We pull comparable sales (comps) from the MLS and county recorder data — the same sources appraisers use. We are not using Zillow Zestimates or automated valuation models. Automated tools are notoriously inaccurate on individual properties, particularly older Midwest housing stock where condition variance is high.

Our comp criteria:

  • Within 0.5–1 mile radius of subject property (tighter in urban markets, wider in rural)
  • Sold within the last 90 days — 180 days in slower markets
  • Similar size: within 200–300 square feet of subject property
  • Similar bedroom and bathroom count
  • Comparable condition (we note if the comp was fully renovated vs. original condition)

From qualified comps, we calculate an After Repair Value (ARV) — what your home would be worth in fully renovated, move-in ready condition. ARV is not the offer price. It's the ceiling.

After establishing ARV, we estimate the cost to bring your home to that standard. We use contractor-based cost data from our active Midwest market — not national averages that don't reflect Illinois labor and material costs.

We only count repairs that actually need to be done. If your home has a 3-year-old roof that's functioning fine, we don't include a roof replacement in our cost estimate. We're not looking for excuses to lower the number — we're trying to model the actual work required.

How We Calculate Our Offer

The formula we use is the standard real estate investment formula, applied honestly:

Our offer calculation:

Offer = (ARV × 70%) − Estimated Repairs

The 70% factor accounts for our profit margin, holding costs during renovation (typically 3–5 months of carrying the property), selling costs when we eventually list the renovated home (agent commission, closing costs), and the risk premium of buying a home sight-unseen or in uncertain condition.

Let's walk through an example. Say your home has an ARV of $200,000 (what it would sell for fully renovated), and we estimate $25,000 in repairs to get it there:

  • $200,000 × 70% = $140,000
  • $140,000 − $25,000 = $115,000 offer

That's the math. We'll walk you through it when we present the offer. If your repair estimate seems high, ask us to break it down line by line. We can.

Why Our Offers Are Fair — Not Lowball

"Lowball" offers come from buyers who underestimate ARV, inflate repair costs, or add hidden fees at closing. We don't do any of that. Our ARV is based on real, recent comparable sales. Our repair estimates come from actual contractor quotes, not inflated numbers designed to reduce the offer. And there are no fees deducted at closing — what we offer is what you receive.

Is our offer as high as what you'd get on the open market after renovating the home yourself? No — and we're honest about that. The value we provide is the time, certainty, and burden we take off you. We handle the renovation, the resale, the holding costs, and the risk. You get paid now, in cash, without any of that work or uncertainty.

What Happens After You Accept

Once you accept our offer, we move quickly. There is no financing contingency — we have the funds. We typically waive the formal inspection (we've already assessed the property) and proceed directly to title. Our title company runs a title search, handles any outstanding liens, coordinates the closing, and wires your funds. You sign, we fund. Most closings are complete in 10–14 business days from contract execution.
