How to Sell a Probate House in Illinois: What Heirs Actually Need to Know

Going through probate in Illinois and need to sell the house? Here's how the process works, what authority you need before you can sell, and how a cash buyer can close fast — sometimes before probate even wraps up.

I buy houses across Illinois and the broader Midwest, and probate deals are something I do regularly. The process is more manageable than most people expect — you just need to know the rules before you start.

Does the House Actually Have to Go Through Probate?

First thing worth checking: not every inherited property in Illinois requires probate. If your loved one held the home in a revocable living trust, put a transfer-on-death deed on file, or owned it jointly with right of survivorship — the house may pass directly to the beneficiaries without any court involvement. If that's your situation, you've already skipped a lot of headache.

But if the property was held in the deceased person's name alone with none of those mechanisms in place — which is the case for most older homeowners across Illinois — then probate is required before title can legally transfer. The court oversees the process to make sure debts get paid and assets go to the right people.

Who Has the Authority to Sell

This is the piece most families get wrong: you can't just sell a probate house because you're an heir. You need legal authority to act on behalf of the estate — and that comes from the court, not from being mentioned in the will.

The probate court appoints a personal representative — called an executor if there's a will, or an administrator if there isn't one. That person is the only one with authority to negotiate and sign a sales contract on behalf of the estate. If multiple heirs think they can each make decisions independently, that's a recipe for delays and disputes.

Once the personal representative is formally appointed, they can begin marketing the property and entering into contracts. In most Illinois counties, the court will require formal approval of the sale before it closes — either by notifying all heirs and giving them a window to object, or by getting a court order specifically authorizing the sale. Your probate attorney handles this, and it's not as slow as it sounds. Most routine sales move through approval in a few weeks.

Selling During Probate vs. Waiting Until It Closes

Here's a question I get often: should you wait until probate is fully closed before selling, or try to sell while it's still open?

In most cases, selling during probate makes more sense. Here's why:

  • A vacant house costs money every month — insurance, utilities, property taxes, basic maintenance. That's real cash walking out the door while the estate sits idle.
  • Selling converts the property to cash, which is far easier to distribute among heirs than trying to agree on what to do with a physical house.
  • Proceeds from the sale can be used to pay estate debts and creditors, which the court requires before the estate closes anyway.
  • Waiting doesn't actually speed up probate — the court process runs on its own timeline regardless.

The only situation where waiting makes sense is if probate will wrap up in just a few weeks and the heirs plan to take title themselves. Otherwise, selling during probate is almost always the smarter financial move.

Common Questions

Selling a Probate House in Illinois — FAQ

Does every inherited house in Illinois have to go through probate?

+
Not always. If the home was held in a living trust, has a transfer-on-death deed, or was jointly owned with right of survivorship, it may pass outside of probate. But if the deceased owned the property in their name alone without one of those mechanisms, Illinois law generally requires probate before the property can be transferred or sold.

Can you sell a house during probate in Illinois before it closes?

+
Yes, in most cases. Once the court appoints a personal representative (executor), that person can enter into a sales contract and market the property. The sale itself typically requires court approval before it finalizes — but a cash buyer familiar with probate transactions can work within that timeline and hold the deal until approval comes through. The process moves faster with a cash offer because there's no mortgage lender adding additional requirements.

How long does Illinois probate take for a house?

+
A straightforward Illinois probate with no disputes typically runs six to twelve months from the time the estate is opened in court. If there are creditor claims, multiple heirs who disagree, or title issues, it can stretch to two years or more. Selling the house during probate — rather than waiting until it's fully closed — can actually speed up the overall estate settlement by converting the property to cash that's easier to distribute.

What authority does the executor need to sell a probate house in Illinois?

+
The executor (or administrator, if there's no will) must be formally appointed by the probate court before they can legally sell the property. In Illinois, the executor typically has authority to sell real estate under the Illinois Probate Act, but the court may require notice to heirs and formal approval of the sale — especially if the sale price is significantly below market value or if heirs object. A probate attorney in your county can walk you through the specific requirements.

How quickly can a cash buyer close on a probate house in Illinois?

+
Once the executor has authority to sell and court approval is in place, a cash buyer can close in as little as 10 to 21 days. The limiting factor is usually the probate court's schedule, not the buyer.
