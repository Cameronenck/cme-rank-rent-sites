Selling a House With Back Taxes in Illinois: What You Need to Know

Owe delinquent property taxes and need to move the house? Here's how Illinois tax liens work at closing, what they'll cost you, and why a cash sale is usually your fastest way out.

Published May 12, 2026  •  SellMidwestHome

I talk to Illinois homeowners every week who are sitting on a property with two, three, sometimes four years of unpaid property taxes piled up on it. The question I get asked most often is: can I even sell this thing? The short answer is yes — and it's more straightforward than most people think. But you need to understand how Illinois handles tax liens, because the clock is ticking and the penalties add up fast.

I've bought houses across Illinois from people in exactly this spot — Chicago bungalows, Rockford two-flats, downstate farmhouses — and the process is the same every time. Let me walk you through what actually happens so you're not flying blind.

How Property Tax Liens Work in Illinois

In Illinois, property taxes are billed in arrears, meaning you're always paying for the prior year. When taxes go unpaid, the county places a lien on the property. That lien doesn't just sit there politely — it accumulates interest at a rate set by the county, and in Illinois that rate can be steep.

Here's the part that catches people off guard: after roughly two years of delinquency, the county can sell your tax certificate at a public auction to an investor. That investor pays off your tax debt and earns the right to collect from you — with interest — during a redemption period. If you don't pay them back within that window (usually two to three years), they can file for a tax deed and take the property. It's a slow process, but once a certificate has been sold, your clock is officially running.

The practical takeaway: the longer you wait, the more you owe, and the fewer options you have. Selling now — even at a discount — almost always beats losing the property entirely to a tax deed proceeding.

What Happens to Back Taxes When You Sell

Here's the good news most people don't know: you don't have to pay the back taxes before you can sell. When the property goes under contract, the title company does a full lien search. They find exactly what's owed — base taxes, interest, penalties, any sold certificates — and that total gets paid out of your closing proceeds automatically.

Think of it like this: the title company acts as the middleman. Your sale price comes in, the tax payoff goes out to the county or certificate holder, and you receive whatever's left. You never write a check upfront.

The catch is equity. If your back taxes, interest, and any other liens add up to more than what the property is worth, there may not be anything left for you after closing — or you could end up in a short-sale situation where the lienholder has to agree to take less than they're owed. That's more complicated, but it's still a solvable problem, especially with a cash buyer who's done it before.

Why Financed Buyers Can't Usually Help You

If you go the traditional route — list with an agent, find a buyer using a mortgage — you're going to run into a wall. Most lenders require a clear title before they'll fund the loan. A property tax lien is a title defect. That means your deal falls apart at the financing stage unless the lien is cleared first, which brings you back to needing the cash to pay it upfront.

Some financed buyers will agree to a simultaneous closing where the tax payoff and the sale close at the same time — but not every title company or lender will do this, and it adds complexity and time. I've seen deals die over this exact issue.

A cash buyer sidesteps all of that. No lender to satisfy, no title contingency from a bank underwriter, no appraisal requirement. The title company clears the lien from closing funds, everyone signs, and that's it.

Don't Wait Until a Tax Deed Investor Comes Knocking

I want to be honest with you about something: if a certificate has already been sold on your property and you're deep into the redemption period, your window to sell on your own terms is shrinking. Once a tax deed investor files in court, selling becomes significantly harder and more expensive. The investor has legal standing, and clearing them out takes time and attorney fees.

If you're not sure where you stand, call the county treasurer's office and ask whether a tax certificate has been sold on your parcel. They'll tell you. If one has been sold, find out when the redemption period ends. That date is your real deadline.

Selling With Back Taxes — FAQ

Can you sell a house in Illinois if you owe back property taxes?

+
Yes. You can sell a house in Illinois even if you have delinquent property taxes. The back taxes — plus any accumulated interest and penalties — will typically be paid out of the sale proceeds at closing. The title company handles this automatically; you don't need to pay them separately before listing or accepting an offer.

What happens to a property tax lien when you sell in Illinois?

+
In Illinois, property tax liens attach to the property itself, not just the owner. When you sell, the lien is discovered during the title search and must be cleared before or at closing. The title company pays off the lien from your sale proceeds before disbursing anything to you. If the lien amount is close to or exceeds your equity, a cash buyer can still make it work — whereas a traditional buyer using a mortgage lender typically cannot close on a liened property.

How far back can Illinois property taxes go before the county can take your home?

+
In Illinois, if property taxes go unpaid, the county can sell a tax certificate to investors after roughly two to three years of delinquency. The investor then has the right to file for a tax deed after a redemption period (typically two to three years from the sale date). This is a slow process — but once it starts, it's hard to stop without paying off the full amount owed plus interest. If you're behind on taxes and want to avoid losing the property entirely, selling sooner is almost always better than waiting.

Will back taxes prevent a buyer from getting a mortgage on my house?

+
Most mortgage lenders require a clean title before they'll fund a loan. If there's an outstanding property tax lien on the property, a financed buyer's deal will typically fall apart — the lender won't approve the loan until the lien is cleared. That's one of the main reasons homeowners in this situation often end up with a cash buyer: no lender, no title requirements holding up the deal, and the lien gets paid from closing proceeds.
