How to Sell a Rental Property Fast in the Midwest

May 11, 2026 8 min read SellMidwestHome Team

I talk to landlords every single week who are done. Not complaining — just done. They bought a rental property ten or fifteen years ago thinking it would be passive income, and somewhere along the way it turned into a second job they never signed up for. Late rent, maintenance calls at midnight, turnover costs, property taxes going up, and now they just want out. Cleanly. Quickly. Without headaches.

If that sounds familiar, this post is for you. I want to walk you through exactly how we help Midwest landlords sell rental properties fast — and what you need to think about before you do it.

Why Selling a Rental Is Different From Selling a Primary Home

Most sellers picture listing on the MLS, staging the house, and hosting open houses. That works fine for an owner-occupied home. A rental is a different situation entirely.

For starters, you may have tenants in place. Getting a tenant to cooperate with showings is tough. Getting a tenant to vacate so a traditional buyer can close is even harder — especially if they have a lease. Then there's the condition. Rental properties take a beating. Most landlords know this. Buyers using conventional financing will have an inspector nitpicking every deferred repair, and the lender may require fixes before funding.

There's also the carrying cost. Every month you wait is another month of property taxes, insurance, potential vacancy, and maintenance. If you're already burned out on the property, dragging out a 90-day MLS listing feels like punishment.

Here's the thing: A cash buyer doesn't care about the condition of the property, whether it's occupied, or how long it's been since you replaced the roof. We price it based on what it's worth to us as an investment — and we move fast.

How Selling to a Cash Buyer Works for Landlords

The process is straightforward. You reach out, give us the address, and tell us a little about the situation — is it occupied, vacant, what's the general condition. We take a look at the property and come back to you within 24 hours with a cash offer.

If the number works for you, we open title and move toward closing on your timeline. Most of our landlord sales close in 7 to 14 days. Some take 30 days if you need to sort out a tenant situation or want time to plan for tax purposes. We are flexible — you pick the date.

Here's what you do NOT have to do:

  • No repairs. We take the property exactly as it sits today.
  • No cleaning or clearing out. Leave anything you don't want to deal with.
  • No evicting your tenants before we close. We buy occupied rentals regularly.
  • No agent commissions. You keep the full amount we offer — nothing comes out at closing on your end.
  • No open houses, no strangers walking through, no inspection contingencies.

What Kinds of Rental Properties Do We Buy?

We buy across all six states we serve — Illinois, Ohio, Indiana, Michigan, Missouri, and Kentucky. The property type doesn't need to be perfect either:

  • Single-family rentals, whether occupied or vacant
  • Small multi-family — duplexes, triplexes, and four-units
  • Properties with deferred maintenance or code violations
  • Rentals with problem tenants or month-to-month leases
  • Properties that need major updates — kitchens, roofs, HVAC, everything

I've seen properties that hadn't had a professional repair in 20 years. I've seen units with tenants who were six months behind on rent. We've bought them all. The condition or the tenant situation is not a dealbreaker for us — it changes our offer number, but it doesn't stop us from making one.

The Tax Conversation You Need to Have First

I want to be upfront with you here: selling a rental property has real tax implications that selling a primary home doesn't. Two things can happen when you sell a rental:

  1. Capital gains tax — if the property has appreciated since you bought it, you'll likely owe taxes on the gain. How much depends on how long you've owned it and your income bracket.
  2. Depreciation recapture — if you've been taking depreciation deductions on your taxes over the years (which most landlords do), the IRS wants some of that back when you sell.

I'm not a CPA and I'm not giving tax advice here. What I am saying is — before you close, spend 30 minutes on the phone with your accountant. A 1031 exchange might make sense if you want to roll the proceeds into another investment. Or maybe you just want out entirely and you'll pay the taxes and be done. Either way, that's a conversation worth having before you sign.

We can move as fast or as slow as you need while you get that sorted out. We're not going anywhere.

When a Cash Sale Makes the Most Sense

A cash sale isn't always the highest dollar offer on paper — and I'll be honest with you about that. But when you factor in what you'd actually net on a traditional sale, the math often flips. Here's when going direct to a cash buyer clearly wins:

  • The property needs significant repairs you don't want to fund
  • You have tenants in place and can't easily vacate the unit
  • You need to close fast — job relocation, estate settlement, financial pressure
  • You're done being a landlord and want a clean break, not a drawn-out process
  • The property has been sitting vacant and costing you money every month

If your rental is in great shape, fully renovated, and you've got time and energy to run an MLS sale — that might be the right call. But most of the landlords I work with aren't in that situation. They're tired, the property needs work, and they want it gone.
