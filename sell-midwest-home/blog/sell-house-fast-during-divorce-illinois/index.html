How to Sell Your House Fast During a Divorce in Illinois

Divorcing in Illinois and need to sell the house quickly? Here's what actually works — from splitting equity to closing in days without fighting over repairs.

Published May 10, 2026  •  SellMidwestHome

Let me be straight with you: selling a house during a divorce is one of the hardest things I see homeowners go through. You're already dealing with attorneys, court dates, splitting finances — and now you've got to agree on what to do with the biggest asset you own together. I've bought homes from divorcing couples all across Illinois, and the one thing I hear most often is, "We just want this over."

That's a totally reasonable thing to want. And if you're searching for how to sell your house fast during a divorce in Illinois, this post is for you. I'm going to walk through your real options, what slows things down, and why a cash sale is usually the cleanest path forward.

Why the Marital Home Becomes Such a Problem

In Illinois, a home owned by both spouses is considered marital property — and that means it's subject to equitable distribution when you split. Equitable doesn't mean equal. It means the court divides it in a way it considers fair, based on each person's contributions, the length of the marriage, and financial circumstances.

The trouble is, "equitable" takes time to determine. And while you're waiting — for court dates, for appraisals, for your attorneys to agree — that house is sitting there. Property taxes are still due. The mortgage doesn't pause. If neither of you is living there, utilities and upkeep are still eating money. Every month the house sits unsold costs both of you.

On top of that, you've got joint decision-making on repairs, showings, and listing price. If one spouse wants to hold out for top dollar and the other just wants out, a traditional listing turns into a months-long argument. I've seen it drag on past the divorce itself.

Your Three Options

When it comes to the marital home during an Illinois divorce, you've got three main paths:

  • One spouse buys out the other. One party refinances into their name alone and pays the other their share of equity. This requires qualifying for a new mortgage solo — which isn't always possible depending on income.
  • List with an agent. Both parties agree to list, set a price, make repairs, and wait for a buyer. On a good day, this takes 60 to 90 days. On a complicated day, it takes much longer — and every decision needs both signatures.
  • Sell to a cash buyer. Both parties agree to sell as-is, usually closing in 2 to 3 weeks. No repairs, no showings, no haggling with buyers over inspection findings. Clean proceeds that can be divided per your settlement agreement.

For most divorcing couples I work with, option three is the one that actually gets them out the door.

A cash sale strips out all the moving parts that cause conflict. There's no "we need to fix the furnace before we list" conversation. There's no back-and-forth with a buyer's agent over what the inspection found. There's no extended 45-day close while you're both paying attorneys by the hour.

Within 24 hours, I have a written cash offer. If both parties agree, we open title, do a quick walkthrough, and pick a close date. That's it. The title company handles everything — both spouses sign at closing (or separately, if needed), and proceeds are wired out the same day.

No repairs. No showings scheduled during custody exchanges. No staging costs. No agent commission off the top. What you get in your hand is what we agree on upfront — nothing hidden.

What You Actually Need From Both Spouses

I want to be clear about one thing: I can't buy a home where only one spouse agrees. If both names are on the deed, both parties need to sign the purchase agreement and the closing documents. That's not us being difficult — that's Illinois real estate law.

What I can do is work around scheduling. If both parties are represented by attorneys and everything is being coordinated through the divorce proceeding, we can coordinate closings to happen separately, as long as everyone signs. Many of the couples I work with never have to be in the same room.

If one spouse is being uncooperative and refusing to sell, that's a legal matter — your attorney can pursue a partition action through the courts. But in my experience, most people want to move on. The house is the last thread connecting two people who are trying to untangle their lives. Once they see a real number on paper and understand they can close in two weeks, they sign.

What to Expect at Closing

At closing, the title company pays off the existing mortgage first. Whatever equity remains is split per your divorce settlement agreement — or held in escrow if that's still being finalized. You both walk away clear of the property, clear of the mortgage, and ready to start over.

I've closed on homes where the proceeds went directly into a joint escrow pending the final divorce decree. I've closed on homes where both parties agreed on a 50/50 split right there in the title company office. Every situation is a little different, and we work with what you've got.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Do both spouses have to agree to sell the house during a divorce in Illinois?

+
In most cases, yes. If both names are on the deed, both parties need to sign off on a sale. If one spouse is refusing to sell, the other can petition the court for a partition action — but that takes time and money. A cash sale agreed upon by both parties is usually the fastest, cleanest exit.

How are proceeds from a home sale split in an Illinois divorce?

+
Illinois follows equitable distribution — not necessarily 50/50. The court considers factors like each spouse's contribution, length of marriage, and each party's financial situation. Many divorcing couples agree on a split before closing to avoid court delays. A cash sale lets you settle proceeds quickly once that agreement is in place.

Can we sell the house before the divorce is finalized?

+
Yes. Selling before the final decree is finalized is common and often simpler. Both spouses sign at closing, proceeds get held in escrow or split per the settlement agreement, and both parties move on. Some couples use the sale itself to fund their separation.

What if the house needs repairs we can't agree on?

+
This is one of the biggest friction points in a divorce sale. Traditional listings require repairs, staging, and ongoing maintenance — all of which need joint decisions and shared expenses. A cash buyer takes the property as-is. No repair negotiations, no contractor drama, no more shared decisions.

How fast can we close on a cash sale during a divorce?

+
We can typically close in 7 to 21 days once both parties are aligned. The timeline mostly depends on how quickly the title work clears and when both spouses can sign. There's no waiting on bank appraisals or buyer financing — which cuts weeks off the process.
