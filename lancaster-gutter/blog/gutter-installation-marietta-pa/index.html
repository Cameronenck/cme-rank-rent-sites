New Gutter Installation in Marietta, PA

For most homes in Marietta Borough and the surrounding West Donegal Township, we install seamless aluminum gutters fabricated on-site. The standard residential size is 5-inch K-style, which handles normal Lancaster County rainfall volumes without issue on typical pitched roofs. However, Marietta has a higher-than-average concentration of homes that fall outside the standard installation playbook — historic row homes with steep pitches, slate roofs, and existing 6-inch or half-round gutter profiles that were sized for that specific structure. We assess every roofline individually before recommending a size or profile.

Seamless gutters are the right call for almost every home in this area. Unlike sectional gutters that connect in short pieces with joints every 10 to 12 feet, seamless systems are cut to the exact length of each run from a single piece of aluminum. Fewer joints means fewer places for water to leak behind the fascia — and in Marietta's humid river environment, minimizing water intrusion points behind the gutter is especially important.

Historic Home Gutter Matching in Marietta Borough

This is where Marietta jobs get interesting. The Victorian, Federal, and Italianate row homes concentrated along Front Street, Market Street, and the streets running back from the river were built with architectural gutter profiles that you simply don't see on postwar housing. Many of these homes have existing half-round gutters — either the original or a previous replacement — and some have oversized 6-inch profiles on their broad, steep front facades.

Replacing those systems with standard 5-inch K-style aluminum almost always looks wrong. The proportions don't match, and if the home has ornate soffit or crown molding detail, K-style hangers often can't mount cleanly against the fascia geometry. We work with aluminum half-round gutters for budget-conscious replacements and copper half-round for homeowners doing full restorations. Copper ages beautifully on historic facades, develops a natural patina over time, and lasts 50 or more years with minimal maintenance — a meaningful advantage on a home that's already outlasted multiple previous gutter systems.

Slate and metal roofs — both common on Marietta's older housing stock — present their own installation considerations. Metal drip edges on slate roofs are often a different profile than what we'd find on an asphalt-shingled house, and the fascia geometry can require different hanger spacing. We've done enough of these jobs in the borough that we know what to look for before we start hanging anything.

Fascia Board Inspection and Repair Before New Gutters

The single most important thing we do before any Marietta installation is probe the fascia. On homes this old — and many in the borough proper are pushing 150 to 200 years — the fascia boards have been through multiple generations of gutter systems. When any one of those systems had even a minor leak at the back seam, water wicked into the wood. Repeat that a few times across several decades and you get fascia that looks fine on the outside (it's been painted over more than once) but is soft or punky behind the surface.

Hanging new gutters on rotted fascia is a waste of money. The hidden-bracket hangers we use are set every 16 to 24 inches, and they rely on solid wood to hold. If the wood is compromised, the system will sag out of pitch within a season or two — the gutters will hold standing water, promote corrosion from the inside, and eventually pull away from the house. We flag every soft board before we quote, and we can replace damaged fascia as part of the installation rather than leaving it for a separate trip by another contractor.

On Marietta's oldest homes, we sometimes find that the fascia has been sistered — a new board nailed over the old one — which can create a different mounting depth than the adjacent runs. We work through these situations systematically so the finished system is level, properly pitched, and holds its grade for the long term.

Downspout Extensions and Drainage in the Susquehanna Flood Plain

Marietta sits in the Susquehanna River corridor, and flood plain awareness is a real consideration for every homeowner on the lower end of the borough. Proper downspout termination matters here more than in most Lancaster County communities. Water that exits a downspout and pools against a foundation in this area has nowhere easy to go — and in a heavy rain event on top of already-saturated soil, it will find its way into basements and crawl spaces.

At minimum, every downspout in Marietta should terminate with an extension that directs water at least four feet from the foundation. Splash blocks should be properly graded away from the house — many of the older ones have settled over the years and now direct water back toward the foundation rather than away from it. On homes closer to the river grade, we often recommend underground drainage lines that carry downspout discharge away from the structure and release it at a safe daylight point in the yard. It's a modest investment that eliminates the leading cause of water intrusion on an otherwise well-maintained house.

River humidity along the Susquehanna also accelerates oxidation on aluminum gutter systems — particularly on homes that face west or northwest and get direct exposure off the river. We use heavy-gauge aluminum and quality baked-enamel finishes on all Marietta installations, and we apply sealant at every end cap and downspout outlet connection to slow corrosion at the joints. On homes right on the waterfront, copper is worth the premium specifically because it doesn't oxidize the way aluminum does in a riverine environment.

Gutter Guards for Cottonwood Debris on Marietta Properties

If you live anywhere near the Susquehanna River banks in Marietta, you know what happens every May and June. The large cottonwood trees that line the riverbank shed prodigious amounts of cotton seed — a light, fluffy white fiber that drifts on the wind and lands everywhere, including directly inside your gutters. Unlike oak leaves that dry out and can sometimes blow free, cottonwood cotton packs into a dense, wet mat that compresses over downspout openings and causes overflow even in moderate rain. It takes sustained clogging to notice, and by the time you're seeing water pour over the front of a gutter, there's often a solid plug at the downspout collar.

Micro-mesh gutter guards are the most effective solution for cottonwood debris. The fine stainless-steel mesh — typically around 50 microns or finer — blocks the cotton fibers at the surface while water flows through freely. Reverse-curve guards don't work well for cottonwood because the cotton rides the curve and bonds to the surface over time. Screen guards with larger openings let fibers pass through and collect at the bottom of the gutter.

We install micro-mesh guards as a standalone service or as part of a new gutter system. On Marietta properties near the river, the payback in avoided cleaning costs typically runs two to four years — and you're not dealing with cottonwood mats compressing in your downspouts every early summer.

My Marietta home has rotted fascia boards behind the old gutters — does that need to be fixed before installation?

Absolutely, and it's one of the most common issues we find on the older housing stock in Marietta Borough. When gutters have been leaking at the back seam or overflowing for years, the wood fascia behind them absorbs repeated moisture and eventually softens. We probe every fascia board before we quote. If there's soft or rotted wood, we won't hang new gutters over it — the hangers pull loose over time and the system loses pitch. We can replace compromised fascia boards as part of the same installation visit so everything gets done right the first time.

It compresses over downspout openings and causes overflows even in moderate rain. Micro-mesh gutter guards are the most effective solution — the fine mesh blocks the cotton fibers while still allowing water to pass through freely.
