If you're noticing paint peeling along the roofline, soft or spongy wood behind your gutters, or gutters that seem to be pulling away from the house — there's a good chance your fascia boards are rotting. It's one of the most common problems I see on Lancaster County homes, and it almost always traces back to the same root cause: water that got behind the gutters and never had a way to dry out.

I've been doing gutter installations and fascia repairs throughout Lancaster, Ephrata, Lititz, Manheim, and the surrounding areas for years. Here's what fascia repair actually costs in 2026, what drives the price up or down, and why catching it early will save you real money.

What Is Fascia and Why Does It Matter?

The fascia is the horizontal board that runs along the edge of your roofline, right where the gutters are mounted. It's not decorative — it's structural. Your gutters hang from it. It also closes off the rafters and protects the edge of your roof deck from moisture.

When fascia rots, your gutters lose their anchor point. They start to sag, pull away from the house, and eventually fail completely. Water that was supposed to travel through the gutter system ends up sheeting down the siding, pooling at the foundation, and working its way into the soffit and attic. One rotted section, left alone, rarely stays contained to one section.

What Causes Fascia to Rot Here in Lancaster County?

The single biggest cause I see is gutters that are clogged, sagging, or have failed seams. When water can't flow freely through the gutter, it overflows — and most of it goes right behind the gutter, between the gutter and the fascia board. That's a tight space with no airflow. The wood stays wet, the paint fails, and within a season or two you've got rot.

Lancaster County has both of the conditions that accelerate this problem: heavy spring rains that overwhelm poorly maintained gutters, and mature trees — especially the oaks and maples common in older neighborhoods around Lancaster City, Manheim Township, and Mount Joy — that drop a massive leaf load every fall. If you're not cleaning your gutters at least twice a year here, you're gambling with your fascia.

Other causes I see regularly:

  • Gutters pitched wrong — water pools at the low spots and spills over the back edge
  • End caps failing — old caulk cracks and water leaks directly onto the fascia at each run's end
  • Downspouts draining onto the roofline — usually from a bad diverter or missing strap
  • Improper gutter hangers — gutters installed too close to the fascia with no drainage gap

Wood vs. PVC Fascia — Which Should You Choose?

This is one of the first questions I get when I'm on a job and we're talking about materials. Here's the honest answer.

Primed pine or spruce is the traditional choice and works fine when it's properly prepped and painted. The issue is that it requires upkeep. If the paint gets nicked or the caulk around the gutters cracks and you don't catch it within a season or two, moisture gets back in and the cycle starts over.

Cellular PVC fascia costs roughly 15–25% more upfront, but it doesn't rot. Period. It won't absorb water, it holds paint for a decade or more, and it doesn't require annual inspection the way wood does. For most Lancaster County homeowners who are replacing rotted wood fascia, I recommend PVC — especially if you're also getting new gutters installed, because you want the backing to outlast the gutters themselves.

Should You Replace the Gutters at the Same Time?

In most cases, yes. Here's the practical reason: to repair or replace fascia, we have to remove the gutters first anyway. If the gutters are older — say, 10 or more years and showing wear — the labor cost of taking them off and reinstalling them is the same as the labor cost of taking them off and installing new ones. You're essentially getting new gutter installation for close to materials cost only.

More importantly: if failing gutters caused the fascia to rot, putting the same gutters back on new fascia is a short-term fix at best. New seamless aluminum gutters installed over fresh fascia, with proper pitch and solid hangers, gives you a system that should go 20+ years without this problem repeating.

How to Tell If Your Fascia Needs Repair Now

You don't have to climb a ladder to spot early fascia damage. Here's what to look for from the ground:

  • Paint that's bubbling, peeling, or flaking along the roofline — moisture underneath is almost always the cause
  • Gutters that have a visible gap between the back of the gutter and the fascia board
  • Sagging gutters, especially at the middle of a run — the fascia can't hold the hanger anymore
  • Staining on the siding below the gutters — water overflowing from behind, not over the front edge
  • Any visible darkening, softness, or crumbling on the fascia board face

If you're seeing any of these signs, call sooner rather than later. What starts as a $300 spot repair can easily become a $1,500 job if the rot spreads into the soffit or roof decking — and I've seen it happen faster than homeowners expect after a few hard rain weeks.

What causes fascia to rot on Lancaster PA homes?
The most common cause is clogged or failing gutters. When gutters overflow — whether from blockages, sags, or failed seams — water runs down behind the gutter and sits against the wood fascia. Over time, the wood saturates, the paint fails, and rot sets in. Lancaster's heavy spring rains and fall leaf loads make this especially common here.
Can I just replace the fascia without replacing the gutters?
You can, but it often doesn't make sense. If your gutters caused the fascia to rot in the first place, putting new fascia under old failing gutters means you'll be back in the same situation in a few years. Most Lancaster homeowners we work with replace both at the same time — it's more cost-effective and protects the new fascia from day one.
