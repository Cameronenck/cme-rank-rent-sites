How to Choose a Gutter Installation Company in Lancaster PA

Published May 2026 • 6 min read • By Lancaster Gutter Installation Pros

I've been installing gutters in Lancaster County for over 13 years. Bad gutter installations are surprisingly common — poor pitch, wrong hanger spacing, downspouts that dump water against the foundation. By the time homeowners realize there's a problem, they often have water in the basement and damaged fascia. Here's how to hire right the first time.

Seamless vs. Sectional — Insist on Seamless

This is the first question to ask any contractor: are the gutters seamless? Seamless gutters are fabricated on-site using a roll-forming machine that produces a continuous gutter from one piece of aluminum for each run. The only joints are at inside/outside corners and downspout outlets.

Sectional gutters — the kind you can buy at home improvement stores — are pre-cut pieces connected with hangers and sealant. Every connector is a potential leak point. In Lancaster County's freeze-thaw climate, those seams move and crack. A seamless installation that's properly pitched and hung will outlast a sectional system by years.

If a contractor quotes you sectional gutters without mentioning seamless as an option, or if they don't have a roll-forming machine on the truck, keep shopping. See our full gutter installation options.

Pitch and Hanger Spacing — The Details That Matter

Gutters should pitch approximately 1/16 inch per foot toward the downspout — enough to drain reliably but not so much that it's visually obvious from the ground. Installers who eyeball pitch rather than measuring it produce gutters that pool water, breed mosquitoes, and corrode faster at the low spots.

Hanger spacing should be no more than 24 inches for aluminum gutters — 18 inches in areas with significant snow and ice load. Wider spacing causes sagging and pullaway from the fascia over time. Ask what hanger spacing they use.

Ask About Fascia Condition Before Installation

Gutters attach to the fascia board — the trim board that runs along the roofline. In older Lancaster County homes, fascia is often soft or rotted behind existing gutters that have been leaking. Gutters installed over rotten fascia will fail quickly — the screws have nothing solid to grip, and the gutters will pull away within a season or two.

A thorough contractor inspects the fascia before quoting and includes fascia repair in the scope when needed. Ask: "Will you inspect the fascia before installation, and what do you charge to replace rotted sections if you find them?" If they say they'll figure it out when they get there, that's an open-ended cost you haven't accounted for.

Downspout Placement and Discharge

Gutters that work perfectly but discharge water against your foundation are doing more harm than good. Ask where each downspout will be located and where the water goes. In Lancaster County, proper downspout termination means:

  • Discharge at least 5–6 feet from the foundation
  • Discharge directed toward ground that slopes away from the house
  • Splash blocks or extensions at ground level to prevent erosion

For homes with persistently wet basements, we sometimes recommend underground downspout extensions that pipe water well away from the foundation. Ask if that's appropriate for your property.

Gauge of Aluminum

Not all aluminum gutters are the same thickness. The standard minimum for residential work is .027 gauge. Thicker .032 gauge aluminum costs a bit more but is noticeably more rigid, holds its shape better over time, and resists denting from ladder contact or falling branches. Ask what gauge the contractor uses and verify it's in the quote.

Get Multiple Written Quotes

For a full gutter replacement on a typical Lancaster County home, you should get at least two or three written quotes. Make sure each quote specifies footage, profile, material gauge, number of downspouts, and what happens to old gutters. Comparing apples-to-apples is hard if one contractor is vague and another is specific — make them all be specific.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between seamless and sectional gutters?

Seamless gutters are fabricated on-site to the exact length of each run with no seams along the straight sections — only at corners and downspout outlets. Sectional gutters are pre-cut pieces joined with connectors. Seamless gutters have fewer leak points and last longer. For a permanent installation, seamless is the industry standard.

How do I know if a gutter quote is complete?

A complete gutter quote should specify: linear footage of gutter, gutter profile (K-style vs. half-round), material and gauge (aluminum thickness), number and placement of downspouts, downspout extensions, removal and disposal of old gutters if applicable, and any fascia repair needed before installation.

What gauge aluminum should gutters be?

.027 gauge aluminum is the minimum for residential gutters. .032 gauge is better and noticeably more rigid — it holds its shape better and dents less from ladder contact or debris. Ask what gauge the contractor uses. Budget installers often use thin material that sags between hangers.

How important is downspout placement?

Very. Even perfect gutters fail if downspouts discharge water too close to the foundation. Water should be discharged at least 5–6 feet away from the house and directed toward grade that slopes away from the foundation. Ask where downspouts will terminate and whether extensions are included.

What warranty should I expect on gutter installation?

Look for a minimum 1-year labor warranty plus the manufacturer's material warranty (typically 10–20 years on aluminum). Warranty on seamless gutter systems should cover leaks at seams and hangers, not just material defects.
