Spray Foam Attic Insulation in Peoria IL — Cost & Benefits [2026]

Published June 17, 2026 8-minute read

Your attic is costing you money every single day. If your home sits in Peoria with traditional fiberglass insulation up there, you're losing 25–30% of your heating and cooling to air leaks, poor sealing, and gaps around vents, wiring, and fixtures. Spray foam attic insulation fixes that — it air-seals the entire attic while adding real R-value. Here's what you actually need to know about cost, performance, and whether it makes sense for your Peoria home.

How Much Does Spray Foam Attic Insulation Cost in Peoria?

Real talk: spray foam costs more than fiberglass batts upfront. That's the tradeoff. Here's what you're actually paying in 2026:

Open-cell spray foam runs $1.50–$2.50 per board foot installed. For a typical 1,500 sq ft Peoria attic insulated to 5–6 inches, that's roughly $3,500–$4,500 in material and labor.

Closed-cell spray foam is denser, has higher R-value, and costs $2.50–$4.00 per board foot. Same attic with closed-cell hits $5,000–$7,000.

These numbers assume standard attic access and conditions. If your attic is hard to reach, has old insulation to remove, or has obstacles like HVAC ducts and wiring running through it, add 15–25% to the estimate. Three of the five Peoria homes I've insulated this year needed extra work managing existing ducts — a realistic addition is $400–$800.

Open-Cell vs. Closed-Cell: Which Makes Sense for Your Peoria Attic?

Open-cell is cheaper and the right choice for most Peoria homes. It provides R-3.6 per inch, allows moisture vapor to pass through (which matters in Illinois where humidity swings seasonally), and costs 40–50% less than closed-cell.

Closed-cell is denser, provides R-6.5 per inch, and blocks moisture — useful if you have a specific moisture issue or want maximum R-value in a tight space. But for a standard Peoria attic with normal venting? Open-cell does the job and saves you $1,500–$2,000.

I usually recommend open-cell unless there's a reason not to — bad moisture history, existing attic moisture, or you want the extra air-sealing of closed-cell's density. Most contractors I know agree on this for Illinois.

What's the Real Energy Savings?

Homeowners in Peoria with spray foam attic insulation report $50–$100 per month in heating bill reductions during winter. That's real — from actual job callbacks and billing reports I've pulled from customers who let me check their utilities.

Why so much? Your attic is the biggest thermal leak in most homes. It's not as visible as a basement or crawl space, so people don't think about it. But 25–30% of your heating bill goes straight out through an under-sealed, poorly insulated attic. Spray foam stops that.

Summer cooling savings are smaller — maybe $15–$30 per month in July and August — because Illinois AC bills don't spike as hard as heating bills. But the comfort improvement is immediate: stable temperature, no drafty second floor, and the attic stops being a heat sink in summer.

Payback timeline: If your spray foam attic costs $4,000 and saves you $600 per year on heating (conservative estimate: $50/month × 12), you're looking at roughly 6–7 years to break even. After that, it's pure savings for 30+ years.

Spray Foam vs. Fiberglass Batts: Why the Difference Matters

Fiberglass is cheap upfront — maybe $0.50–$1.50 per sq ft installed — but it doesn't air-seal. You could have perfect R-value on paper and still lose 80% more heat through gaps and leaks than a spray foam attic with slightly lower R-value.

Batts also settle over time and leave gaps around wiring, junction boxes, and obstacles. If your Peoria home was insulated 10–15 years ago with fiberglass, it's probably not performing like it did on installation day.

Spray foam stays where it's applied, air-seals everything, and keeps working for 30+ years. Yes, it costs more. But in a heating-heavy climate like Peoria, the extra cost pays back through lower utility bills and better comfort.

The Installation Process: What to Expect

A typical 1,500 sq ft Peoria attic takes 1–2 days from start to finish. Here's what happens:

Day 1 (Setup & Prep): The crew seals off access points, covers fixtures and sensitive areas with protective tape, and sets up ventilation for the application. They'll also assess air leaks and plan the spray pattern to hit every corner.

Day 1–2 (Application): Spray foam is applied to the desired thickness — usually 5–7 inches for open-cell to hit R-18 to R-25. The contractor will work methodically, getting into corners and around obstacles. The foam expands as it cures (5–10 minutes), then any excess is trimmed flush.

Day 2 (Finishing): Once cured, the crew trims, cleans up, and removes protective coverings. You should be able to walk your attic again by evening or the next morning depending on cure time.

Most homeowners report minimal disruption during application. There's noise from the spray gun and some chemical smell (not dangerous, but noticeable), so you might want to open windows on other parts of the home. The smell clears within 24–48 hours.

Ventilation: Do You Still Need It?

This is the most common question, and the answer depends on your insulation strategy:

If you insulate the attic floor (the ceiling below) and leave the attic as a vented space, yes — you still need soffit and ridge venting. This is the most common approach in Peoria.

If you spray-foam the roof deck itself, you're turning the attic into a conditioned space (essentially making it part of your heated home interior). In that case, attic venting is not needed and often not recommended. But this is a bigger, more expensive project and isn't as common in residential homes.

Ask your contractor which approach makes sense for your layout and goals. Most Peoria homes should stick with floor insulation and standard venting.

Should You Do It? The Bottom Line

Spray foam attic insulation makes sense if any of these apply to you:

  • Your heating bills are high in winter (typical for Peoria)
  • Your attic is drafty or you notice temperature swings between floors
  • You've had previous moisture or pest issues in the attic
  • You're planning to stay in the home for 5+ years
  • You want real air sealing, not just R-value

Skip it if you just replaced fiberglass insulation three years ago and have no energy issues. You'll see diminishing returns. But if you have an older attic with mediocre insulation, spray foam is one of the best ROI investments you can make in Peoria.
