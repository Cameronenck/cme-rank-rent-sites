Why Dunlap IL Homes Still Need Spray Foam

Dunlap sits in northwestern Peoria County, and the weather out there is genuinely brutal in both directions. We get hard freeze winters where temperatures drop into the single digits for days at a stretch, and summers with heat indexes that push 100 degrees with humidity to match. Your home's insulation system has to perform in both extremes — and fiberglass batts alone don't cut it.

Here's the thing most people don't understand about insulation: R-value ratings are measured under laboratory conditions with no air movement. In the real world, any gap, penetration, or unsealed framing cavity creates an air pathway that bleeds heat like a faucet. Fiberglass batts are insulation — they slow heat transfer. But they're not an air barrier. Spray foam does both simultaneously: it expands to fill every gap and crevice while also creating an air-tight seal that stops infiltration cold.

In newer Dunlap homes, the main culprits are usually the rim joist, attic bypasses around recessed lights and plumbing, and in some cases, knee walls in homes with finished upper floors. In older properties on the edges of the Dunlap school district — some of which were built in the 1960s and 1970s — the problems go deeper and often include crawl spaces and basement walls that have never been properly addressed.

Top Spray Foam Applications for Dunlap IL Homes

Not every part of your home needs spray foam. Here's where the investment makes the most sense in a typical Dunlap property:

  • Rim joists. This is the single highest-return spray foam application in most Central Illinois homes, and Dunlap is no exception. The rim joist runs around the perimeter of your foundation where the floor framing meets the foundation wall. It's almost always air-leaky, and two inches of closed-cell foam seals it completely. Most homeowners in Dunlap notice a real difference in floor warmth the first winter after we do this work.
  • Attic air sealing. Even with adequate blown insulation depth, most Dunlap attics have bypasses — gaps around recessed can lights, top plates, plumbing stacks, and HVAC penetrations — that allow heated air to escape directly into the attic. We seal these with spray foam before or in addition to any blown insulation work. This step alone often cuts heating costs by 10 to 20 percent.
  • Crawl spaces. Some Dunlap homes — particularly on the older eastern fringe of the district near Route 91 — have vented crawl spaces that pull cold, moist air into the building envelope every winter. Converting to a conditioned crawl space with closed-cell spray foam on the walls is one of the more impactful upgrades we do. It solves cold floors, high humidity, and mold risk in one project.
  • Basement walls. For homes with poured concrete or block foundations, spray foam on the interior basement walls creates a warm, dry space year-round. The moisture control is especially important in Dunlap, where the soil can hold significant ground moisture during spring thaw and after heavy rain events.
  • Cathedral ceilings and bonus rooms. Dunlap has a lot of two-story homes with bonus rooms over garages and cape cod-style upper floors with knee walls. These areas are notoriously difficult to insulate properly with batts because of the limited depth available. Closed-cell spray foam applied directly to the roof deck or knee wall framing is usually the only way to get adequate R-value in these tight spaces.
  • New construction wall cavities. Builders in the Dunlap area increasingly spec open-cell spray foam in exterior wall cavities instead of batts. The continuous air barrier it creates — with no gaps at outlets, plumbing, or framing — is simply something you can't achieve with any batt product. For production builders trying to hit tighter energy codes, spray foam walls have become the standard.

Closed-Cell vs. Open-Cell Spray Foam for Dunlap IL

Both types work well in this climate — the choice comes down to where you're applying it and what you're trying to accomplish.

Closed-cell spray foam runs R-6 to R-7 per inch and acts as a Class II vapor retarder. That makes it the right call for anything below grade or anywhere moisture needs to be managed: rim joists, crawl space walls, basement walls, and cathedral ceiling assemblies where you can't afford to let vapor in. It also adds measurable structural stiffness to walls and roof decks, which is a bonus in high-wind events.

Open-cell spray foam runs about R-3.7 per inch and is vapor-permeable, which matters in assemblies that need to be able to dry to the interior if moisture ever gets in. It's the better choice for full attic fills and interior wall cavities, and it's meaningfully less expensive per square foot — which matters a lot when you're filling an entire attic or a full run of exterior walls.

Most Dunlap homes end up with both: closed-cell in the crawl space and rim joist, open-cell in the attic. If you want a deeper breakdown before we meet on-site, our open-cell vs. closed-cell guide covers the tradeoffs in detail.

What to Expect During a Spray Foam Job in Dunlap

For a focused application — rim joists, crawl space walls, or attic air sealing — expect the crew to be there two to four hours. A larger project like a full attic spray or basement insulation takes a full day. The foam is mixed and applied on-site from our rig. It expands to 30 to 100 times its liquid volume depending on the product and cures within a few hours.

You'll need to vacate the area being sprayed while we're working and stay out for about 24 hours while the foam fully cures. That's standard for all spray foam applications — not a Peoria-specific thing, just the chemistry. We clean up after ourselves, trim any excess foam, and leave the space ready to use.

We pull permits when the project and Peoria County code require it. If a contractor tells you that a significant spray foam project doesn't need permits, that's a red flag worth paying attention to.
