How Much Does Spray Foam Insulation Cost in Peoria IL?

Real Pricing, Hidden Costs, and ROI You Need to Know

Published: June 22, 2026 Reading time: 8 min

I get asked this question at least twice a week. Homeowners see spray foam on HGTV, they hear it's incredible for energy efficiency, but then they look at the price tag and freeze. The sticker shock is real.

Here's what they usually don't realize: the cost of spray foam isn't the problem. It's not knowing what the cost actually covers that creates the confusion.

So let me walk you through exactly what spray foam costs in Peoria IL in 2026, why the prices vary so much, and whether it's actually worth it for your home.

The Real Numbers

Open cell spray foam runs $0.55 to $0.75 per board foot in Peoria. Closed cell is $1.25 to $1.50 per board foot. A board foot is one foot long, one foot wide, and one inch thick—it's how insulation is priced.

For a typical 2,000 square foot attic:

Insulation Type Depth Estimated Cost
Open Cell (3.5") R-13 $4,500 - $6,000
Open Cell (5.5") R-19 $7,000 - $8,500
Closed Cell (2") R-12 $5,500 - $7,000
Closed Cell (3") R-18 $8,000 - $10,000

These are ballpark numbers for new spray foam in a clean attic with no existing insulation. Your actual price will depend on five factors I'm going to explain right now.

What Changes Your Spray Foam Insulation Cost

1. Type: Open Cell vs. Closed Cell

Open cell is cheaper upfront but it's more permeable—it lets moisture pass through. In Peoria's humid summers, that matters. It's better for interior walls where you want some breathability.

Closed cell is denser, more expensive, but it acts as a vapor barrier. If water vapor hits it, it stops. That makes it the safer choice for attics, crawl spaces, and exterior walls in Illinois. The extra cost typically pays for itself in durability and performance.

2. Existing Insulation: Removal Adds Cost

If your attic is full of old fiberglass batts, that fiberglass has to come out before we spray. Removing insulation typically costs $1,000 to $2,500 depending on the amount. We haul it away, dispose of it properly, and prep the space.

A brand new attic with no existing insulation? That saves you that removal cost.

3. Attic Size and Access

Larger attics cost more in absolute dollars but less per square foot. A 3,000 square foot attic will run you more than a 1,000 square foot attic, but the per-square-foot price drops.

Poor attic access drives cost up. If we have to work through a small hatch, set up scaffolding, or work around roof trusses all day, labor time increases. A wide attic hatch and clear space speeds the job and saves you money.

4. Condition of the Attic

If your attic has roof leaks, mold, or damaged framing, we fix those before spraying. A moldy attic needs remediation first—that's an extra $500 to $2,000 depending on severity. Damaged wood might need replacement. These aren't fun surprises, but they're necessary before spray foam can be properly installed.

5. Ventilation and Building Code Requirements

Peoria is in a mixed-humid climate. If you're spraying the underside of the roof deck with closed cell, you might not need attic ventilation. With open cell, you do. That affects the job scope and sometimes adds cost for proper vent installation.

Your home's age also matters. Newer homes built to current code might need different prep than older homes. We always work to current Peoria building code.

What Most Contractors Won't Tell You

The cheapest estimate isn't always the best value. I've seen contractors spray foam at rock-bottom prices, then the homeowner calls back two years later because the insulation is settling, or there are gaps, or there's condensation in the walls.

The best contractors charge fair prices because they use quality materials and take the time to do it right. They also guarantee their work. A cheap job comes with no warranty and a headache.

Also, nobody talks about this: spray foam application requires specific weather. If it's too cold, too humid, or too rainy, it won't cure properly. A contractor who won't postpone the job because of weather is cutting corners. It costs more to schedule around good weather, but it's the difference between a 50-year job and a 10-year job.

The ROI Question: Does It Pay for Itself?

Let's say you spend $7,500 on spray foam insulation for your attic. Your energy bill is $200 a month in summer and winter combined. A well-sealed attic can reduce that by 20 to 30 percent. That's $40 to $60 per month in savings, or $480 to $720 a year.

At $500 per year, your $7,500 investment pays for itself in 15 years. But you're also buying comfort—no more cold spots in winter, no hot upstairs rooms in summer. And spray foam lasts 50+ years. The payoff extends far beyond energy savings.

If you're staying in your home for 10+ years, spray foam almost always makes sense financially. If you're selling in 3 years, it's a harder sell unless the energy efficiency significantly boosts your home's resale value.

Hidden Costs People Miss

Spray foam isn't just the foam itself. Here's what might add to the bill:

  • Air sealing prep: Before we spray, we seal big gaps, holes, and bypasses. That takes time.
  • Vapor barrier installation: Some jobs need a poly sheet or other vapor management. That's extra material and labor.
  • Vent installation or modification: If you need soffit vents or ridge vents installed or adjusted, that's labor on top of the spray foam cost.
  • Drywall patching: If we have to cut access holes or patch existing damage, that adds labor.
  • Cleanup and haul-away: Good contractors include this. Some charge extra.

Always ask for a detailed breakdown. "Spray foam attic" should tell you exactly what's included and what's extra.

How to Get an Accurate Estimate in Peoria

Call three contractors. Don't just look at the price. Here's what to ask:

  • Will you remove existing insulation, and is that included or extra?
  • What type of spray foam are you recommending and why?
  • Do you have liability and workers' comp insurance? (This should be yes.)
  • What's your warranty, and what does it cover?
  • When can you do the job, and will you reschedule if the weather turns bad?
  • Will you provide before-and-after photos and a detailed report?

Any contractor who won't answer these questions clearly is not someone you want in your attic.

Bottom Line: Is It Worth It?

Spray foam costs more upfront than fiberglass batts. A lot more. But fiberglass settles, it doesn't seal air leaks, and it degrades over time. Spray foam does none of those things.

If you're staying in your Peoria home long-term and you want real insulation performance, real comfort, and real energy savings, spray foam is worth the cost. The question isn't whether it's worth it. The question is whether you can afford not to do it when you're looking at 50+ years of benefit.
