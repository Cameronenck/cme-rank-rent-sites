How to Insulate a Crawl Space in Peoria IL — What Actually Works Here

May 13, 2026 Crawl Space Installation Guide

Crawl spaces are one of those things homeowners in Peoria tend to ignore until there's a real problem — frozen pipes in January, high energy bills they can't explain, or a musty smell creeping into the living room. I've been insulating crawl spaces across central Illinois for years, and the same issues show up over and over. Bad moisture control, inadequate insulation, and materials that aren't suited for what Peoria weather actually throws at them.

Here's how we approach crawl space insulation in Peoria, what the options actually cost, and how to know which method is right for your house.

Two Approaches: Vented vs. Sealed Crawl Space

Before we talk insulation materials, we need to talk strategy — because the two approaches are fundamentally different and lead to very different outcomes.

Vented Crawl Space (Old School)

The traditional approach is to insulate the floor above the crawl space — typically fiberglass batts stuffed between the floor joists — and leave the crawl space itself vented to outside air. This made sense as a code standard decades ago when the thinking was that ventilation would dry out any moisture that got in. We now know this strategy backfires badly in the Midwest.

In summer, warm humid outdoor air flows into a vented crawl space, hits cool surfaces, and condenses. That moisture feeds mold, rots wood, and eventually works its way into the living space above. It also means any ductwork or plumbing running through the crawl space is exposed to exterior temperature swings — which is why Peoria crawl spaces with ductwork are notorious for comfort problems and equipment inefficiency.

Sealed Crawl Space (What We Recommend)

The modern approach — and what we install on almost every crawl space job — is to seal the crawl space completely. That means closing the vents, insulating the foundation walls and rim joists rather than the floor above, and installing a vapor barrier on the ground. The crawl space becomes a semi-conditioned space that stays close to interior temperatures year-round.

The result: no more moisture cycling, no condensation, no freeze risk for pipes and ductwork, and a noticeably warmer floor in winter. It's the right way to build and the right way to retrofit.

Peoria's clay soil holds moisture. Unlike sandy soils that drain, central Illinois clay stays saturated well into spring and releases ground moisture continuously upward. A vapor barrier isn't optional here — it's the foundation of any crawl space solution that actually lasts.

Crawl Space Insulation Options for Peoria Homes

Closed-Cell Spray Foam (Best Overall)

This is what we install on the majority of our crawl space jobs. Closed-cell spray foam applied to the interior foundation walls and rim joists gives you insulation, air sealing, and a vapor retarder all in one material. It adheres directly to concrete block, poured concrete, and wood — no fasteners, no gaps, no future sagging.

For Peoria's climate zone (Zone 5), we typically apply 2 inches of closed-cell foam to the foundation walls, which gets you to roughly R-13. That meets code minimums and makes a dramatic difference in comfort and pipe safety. We can go to 3 inches (R-19) for higher performance on homes where the crawl space is particularly exposed or where the homeowner is chasing maximum efficiency.

Open-Cell Spray Foam

Open-cell foam works fine for air sealing and insulation on foundation walls, but it's vapor-permeable — which means you need to pair it with a vapor barrier on the ground and sometimes a separate vapor retarder on the walls. In a damp central Illinois crawl space, closed-cell is the more complete solution. We use open-cell in crawl spaces when budget is tight and conditions are favorable, but we're always upfront about the tradeoffs.

Rigid Foam Board

Rigid extruded polystyrene (XPS) or polyisocyanurate board is a solid alternative to spray foam, especially for DIY-minded homeowners. The board gets cut and glued against foundation walls, seams get taped, and it's covered with a fire-rated material where required. It's labor-intensive to install correctly, and getting full coverage around pipes, joists, and irregularities takes time. When our crews do it, we get it right. When homeowners try it themselves, the most common problem is gaps — and gaps in a crawl space insulation system are where moisture and cold air find their way back in.

Fiberglass Batts (What Not to Do in the Crawl Space)

Fiberglass batts between the floor joists used to be the standard for crawl space insulation. I'll be straight with you: it's a mediocre solution for Peoria conditions. Batts absorb moisture, lose R-value when wet, fall out of the joist bays over time, and do nothing for air sealing. If you have batts in your crawl space right now and you're having problems, that's not a coincidence.

We still get calls asking us to replace fallen batts. We do it if the homeowner insists, but we always explain why a sealed crawl space with spray foam to the walls is a better long-term investment.

What to Expect During the Job

When we show up to do a crawl space insulation job, here's what happens. First, we assess the space — moisture levels, wood condition, existing insulation, vapor barrier status, vent locations. If there's active water intrusion, we flag it and won't install insulation until that's resolved. Insulation over a wet problem is just sealing in damage.

We close and seal the crawl space vents, clean out any debris or old fallen batts, and install the ground vapor barrier if it's not already in place (or if what's there isn't adequate). Then we spray or install the insulation on the foundation walls and rim joists. Cleanup, a final walkthrough, and you're done — usually in a single day.

Signs Your Crawl Space Needs Attention Now

  • Floors feel cold in winter — even with the heat on, cold crawl space air transfers right through uninsulated floor assemblies
  • Musty smell in the house — ground moisture and mold in the crawl space circulates upward into living areas
  • High heating and cooling bills — an uninsulated or poorly insulated crawl space is a major source of energy loss
  • Visible mold or wood rot — moisture has been sitting long enough to cause structural damage; stop here and call immediately
  • Pipes that froze last winter — exposed plumbing in a vented crawl space is a genuine freeze risk in Peoria January temperatures
  • Fallen fiberglass batts — they never stay up forever, and once they fall, you've lost your insulation coverage entirely

Frequently Asked Questions — Crawl Space Insulation in Peoria IL

How much does crawl space insulation cost in Peoria IL?
Most Peoria homeowners pay $1,500–$4,500 for crawl space insulation depending on the method. Spray foam applied to the foundation walls and rim joists runs $2,000–$4,500 for a typical 800–1,200 sq ft crawl space. Rigid foam board is slightly less at $1,500–$3,200. Full crawl space encapsulation — which includes a vapor barrier, dehumidifier, and insulation — typically runs $4,000–$8,500 depending on the size and condition of the space.
Should I insulate the floor above the crawl space or the crawl space walls?
In most cases, insulating the crawl space walls and rim joists (creating a conditioned crawl space) is the better approach for Peoria homes. When you insulate the floor above, you leave the crawl space itself exposed to exterior temperatures — pipes can freeze and ductwork suffers. Sealing and insulating the perimeter walls brings the crawl space inside the thermal envelope, protecting your plumbing, HVAC, and the floor above.
Do I need a vapor barrier in my Peoria crawl space?
Yes — always. Peoria's humidity levels and clay-heavy soil create significant ground moisture that will work its way into an unprotected crawl space. A minimum 6-mil poly vapor barrier on the ground is required by code in most applications. For a truly sealed crawl space, we use a 12–20 mil reinforced liner that covers the ground and runs up the walls, completely separating the space from ground moisture.
Is spray foam the best insulation for a crawl space?
For most Peoria crawl spaces, yes — spray foam applied to the foundation walls and rim joists is the most effective solution. It provides both insulation and air sealing in one application, it adheres directly to concrete and block walls, and it doesn't absorb moisture. Closed-cell spray foam is especially effective in crawl spaces because it also acts as a vapor retarder. Rigid foam board is a solid budget alternative when applied correctly with taped seams.
How long does crawl space insulation take to install?
A standard spray foam crawl space job in the Peoria area typically takes one day for a crew of two. That includes prepping the space, foaming the walls and rim joists, and cleanup. If we're also installing a vapor barrier, add two to four hours. A full encapsulation project — vapor barrier, spray foam, and dehumidifier installation — usually runs one to two days depending on the size and current condition of the crawl space.
