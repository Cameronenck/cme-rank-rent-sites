Your band board is responsible for one of the biggest energy losses in your home, and most Peoria homeowners don't even know they have one. It's the wooden rim that sits right where your foundation meets your first floor — the transition point between the unconditioned crawl space or basement and your living space. Every winter, cold air leaks around it, your HVAC works harder, and your heating bills stay higher than they should. Insulating your band board fixes this.

We've sealed and insulated thousands of band boards across Peoria, East Peoria, and central Illinois. Here's what you need to know about the work, the cost, and why it matters more than most contractors make it sound.

Why Band Board Insulation Actually Matters

The band board is often called the "thermal bridge" of a home. Picture the structure: your foundation is concrete (no insulation). Your crawl space is unconditioned. The band board is the wooden connection between them, and it's exposed to outside air and ground temperature on three sides. In winter, that cold travels straight up through the wood and into your living space above.

An uninsulated band board accounts for 15-25% of the total air leakage in a typical Peoria home. The worst part? It's invisible. You don't feel the cold coming from it the way you feel drafts around windows, so many homeowners ignore it. But your heating system doesn't ignore it — it runs harder every winter to compensate.

Band board insulation solves this quickly. Properly sealed and insulated, it stops air leakage and dramatically reduces heat loss at that critical junction.

Open-Cell vs. Closed-Cell Spray Foam on Band Boards

Both work, but they work differently.

Open-cell spray foam is lighter and more flexible. It expands more, so it fills gaps well. R-value is about 3.5 per inch. Cost is $1.50-$2.50 per board foot. It's a good budget option for dry crawl spaces, but it absorbs moisture and can degrade if the crawl space gets damp.

Closed-cell spray foam is denser and more rigid. It has an R-value of 6-7 per inch — almost double the open-cell. It doesn't absorb water, so it won't sag or support mold growth if the crawl space gets moisture. Cost is $3-$5 per board foot. In Peoria's climate, with our humidity and occasional water issues, closed-cell is the better choice for band boards.

Think of it this way: open-cell is cheaper upfront. Closed-cell pays you back through better performance and durability. For a band board that has to handle seasonal moisture changes, closed-cell is worth it.

What the Work Actually Involves

Don't picture a quick spray-and-go job. A thorough band board insulation project includes:

  1. Inspection and measurement. We measure the linear footage and height of band board, assess any existing insulation or damage, and check the condition of the rim joist wood. If there's rot or termite damage, it needs repair before insulation goes in.
  2. Cleaning and prep. Any loose or failing insulation gets removed. The band board surface is cleaned of dust, cobwebs, and debris. Any holes or gaps that could trap spray foam unevenly get taped.
  3. Air sealing first. Before foam goes on, we seal any visible cracks, holes, or gaps in the rim joist itself with foam sealant or tape. This ensures the final foam layer is continuous and doesn't have voids.
  4. Spray foam application. Foam is applied in 2-3 inch passes, building up to create a continuous insulation layer. Each pass cures slightly before the next one is applied. This gives a solid, uniform finish.
  5. Trim and finish. Once cured, any excess foam is trimmed flush with the rim joist surface. Some contractors leave it as-is; others paint over it for appearance (though this is optional).

The entire job typically takes 4-6 hours for an average home. Most teams work from below (crawl space side), so minimal disruption to your living space above.

What Factors Affect the Cost?

Linear footage of band board. Larger homes have more perimeter, so more band board. A 1,500 sq ft ranch will have more band board than a 1,500 sq ft two-story home because of the footprint difference. Expect 140-180 linear feet for a typical ranch.

Crawl space accessibility. If you have tight access, low headroom, or a cramped crawl space, labor costs increase. Easy access saves 20-30% on labor vs. difficult crawl spaces.

Existing insulation and damage. If you have old fiberglass batts or mineral wool to remove, that's extra labor. If there's rotted wood or rim joist damage, repairs happen first (add $300-$1,500 depending on extent).

Foam type (open vs. closed-cell). Closed-cell costs 40-60% more than open-cell, but delivers better R-value and moisture resistance. For Peoria homes, it's the right choice.

Sealing scope. If you're just doing the band board, cost is lower. If you're also sealing rim joist cracks, adding a vapor barrier on the crawl space floor, or doing dehumidification, the overall project cost grows.

Band Board Insulation + Crawl Space Moisture Issues

Here's an important caveat: if your crawl space has active moisture or water intrusion, insulating the band board alone won't solve the problem. Moisture will seep in behind the insulation, which can lead to mold growth and wood rot. Band board insulation works best when:

  • Your crawl space stays dry year-round, OR
  • You're addressing moisture control first (vapor barrier, sump pump, drainage), then adding band board insulation

If we find moisture when we evaluate your crawl space, we always recommend fixing that first. Band board insulation is the finish, not the foundation of the job.

Why Hire a Spray Foam Contractor (Not a DIY Project)

Band board insulation is not a DIY project. Here's why:

Equipment and safety. Spray foam application requires specialized equipment, proper ventilation, and safety gear. It's not something you rent and attempt yourself. The foam is reactive and can cause skin and respiratory irritation if mishandled.

Uniform coverage and seal. Poorly applied spray foam leaves voids and air gaps. A professional contractor applies foam at the right pressure and speed to ensure complete, continuous coverage. Voids defeat the purpose of the work.

Warranty and accountability. If the foam fails or moisture gets behind it, a licensed contractor warrants the work and can fix it. DIY work has no recourse.

The cost of hiring a professional is far lower than the cost of redoing failed DIY insulation or dealing with mold remediation later.

Frequently Asked Questions

What exactly is a band board and why does it need insulation?
The band board (also called rim joist or rim board) is the wooden beam that sits on top of your foundation and supports the house floor system. It's exposed to the outside air and ground and it's one of the biggest sources of air leakage and heat loss in a Peoria home. An uninsulated band board can account for 15-25% of your total heating loss because it sits directly between the conditioned living space above and the unconditioned crawl space or basement below. Insulating it reduces that air leakage and keeps your floors warmer.
Why is spray foam better than fiberglass for band boards?
Spray foam creates a complete air seal around the band board. Fiberglass batts leave gaps around pipes, wires, and the rim joist itself — air still flows around and through them. Spray foam expands to fill every cavity, crack, and irregular shape, creating a continuous barrier. In Peoria's cold winters, that air seal saves real money. Plus, spray foam doesn't absorb moisture like fiberglass, so it won't degrade or support mold growth if your crawl space gets damp.
How much does band board spray foam insulation cost in Peoria?
Band board insulation with spray foam in Peoria typically costs $500 to $1,500 depending on the linear footage of band board, the height of the rim joist, and whether you're also doing crawl space work. A typical 1,200 square foot home has about 140-150 linear feet of band board. Budget $1,000 to $1,200 for spray foam applied to the entire band board. If you're doing a full crawl space encapsulation, band board insulation is usually bundled into the overall package.
Can you insulate a band board if you have an active crawl space leak?
Not effectively. If water is seeping into your crawl space, insulating the band board alone won't stop it. Moisture will still enter behind the insulation and cause rot. Band board insulation works best when the crawl space is dry or when it's paired with a complete moisture control system (vapor barrier, sump pump, proper drainage). If you have active water problems, address the moisture source first, then insulate. Our free crawl space evaluations always check for moisture before we recommend band board work.
Is band board insulation alone enough to make a difference in my energy bills?
Yes. Sealing and insulating the band board reduces air leakage and can save 5-15% on heating and cooling costs depending on your home's current condition. For a Peoria home with a $150 monthly utility bill, that's $7.50 to $22.50 per month in savings — or $90 to $270 per year. The work usually pays for itself in 4-6 years. If you're also doing attic or basement insulation, band board work amplifies those savings.
What's the R-value of spray foam on a band board?
Closed-cell spray foam has an R-value of about 6-7 per inch. A 2-inch application (typical for band boards) gives you R-12 to R-14. Open-cell spray foam is R-3.5 per inch, so 2 inches is about R-7. For band board work in Peoria's climate, closed-cell is worth the extra cost because of the better R-value and superior moisture resistance. The air seal it creates is actually more valuable than the R-value alone.
