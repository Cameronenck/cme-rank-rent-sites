Spray Foam vs Fiberglass Insulation in Peoria IL — Which One Actually Wins?

Published May 10, 2026 • By Peoria Spray Foam Pros • Peoria, IL

I get this question at least three or four times a week: "Should I use spray foam or just stick with fiberglass?" It sounds simple, but the honest answer depends on where in the house you're insulating and what problems you're actually trying to solve. After doing this work in Peoria for years, I've seen both materials used well and used wrong. Here's what I tell homeowners when they ask me face-to-face.

The Core Difference: Air Sealing vs. Fill Insulation

The biggest thing people get wrong when comparing spray foam and fiberglass is thinking they're doing the same job. They're not. Fiberglass is a fill insulation — it slows heat transfer through conduction. Spray foam does that AND seals air gaps. That second part is where most of your energy loss is actually happening.

A house in Peoria loses a lot of heat through air movement — through rim joists, around light fixtures, through gaps in the framing. Fiberglass batts, even perfectly installed ones, don't stop that. Air blows right through them. Spray foam expands into every crack and creates an airtight barrier. That's the real performance difference, and it's why a spray foam job often reduces heating bills more than the R-value numbers alone would suggest.

Where Spray Foam Wins in a Peoria Home

Illinois weather makes the air-sealing argument especially strong. We get hard freezes in January and humid 90-degree summers in July. Those conditions punish any gap in your building envelope. Here's where I consistently see spray foam outperform fiberglass in local homes:

  • Rim joists: This is the single highest-ROI spot in most Peoria homes. Fiberglass batts stuffed into rim joists are the old standard — they don't seal, they get wet, and they're basically just packing material. Two inches of closed-cell spray foam here makes a dramatic difference in winter comfort.
  • Crawl spaces: Moisture is the issue in Peoria crawl spaces. Fiberglass between floor joists collects moisture from the ground, sags, and eventually falls down. Spray foam applied to the crawl space walls creates a conditioned, dry envelope that lasts decades.
  • Attic rooflines (cathedral ceilings, knee walls): Anytime you're insulating a sloped or irregular surface, spray foam conforms to the geometry in a way batts simply can't match.
  • Garage ceilings and bonus rooms: Any living space above an unheated garage needs real air sealing, not just fill insulation. Spray foam is the right call here.

Where Fiberglass Still Makes Sense

I'm not anti-fiberglass — it has its place. A flat attic floor with good air sealing already done at the ceiling plane is a perfectly reasonable application for blown-in fiberglass or cellulose. You can get to R-49 pretty cheaply with blown material, and if air movement isn't the issue, the cost savings are real.

Interior partition walls for sound dampening are another case where fiberglass batts work fine. No moisture risk, no air pressure driving through — batts do the job at a fraction of the cost.

The mistake I see homeowners make is using fiberglass in spots where air movement and moisture are active problems — rim joists, crawl spaces, vented attic assemblies — just because it's cheaper up front. That decision costs more in the long run between energy waste, moisture damage, and eventual remediation.

The Cost Conversation

Spray foam costs more per square foot, full stop. But cost-per-square-foot isn't the right metric when you're comparing an air barrier to a fill insulation. A better question is: what's the 10-year cost including energy bills, moisture repairs, and re-insulation?

In my experience, a well-done spray foam job on rim joists and a crawl space in a 1,500-square-foot Peoria home typically runs $2,500–$4,500 depending on conditions. Most homeowners in older homes with drafty basements see $300–$600 per year in energy savings. At that rate, the upgrade pays for itself in 5 to 8 years — and the comfort improvement starts day one. My Recommendation for Most Peoria Homeowners

If you're doing a full insulation upgrade, use spray foam in the air-movement and moisture-risk zones — rim joists, crawl spaces, knee walls, anywhere the building envelope meets the outside. Use blown-in fiberglass or cellulose for attic floors where you're just adding depth to an already-sealed plane. That combination gets you the best performance at a reasonable total cost.

If budget is tight and you can only do one thing, do the rim joists with closed-cell spray foam. That single upgrade has the highest impact-per-dollar of anything in most Illinois homes. We've seen it knock $40–$60 off a monthly gas bill in older Peoria houses. Frequently Asked Questions

Is spray foam worth the extra cost over fiberglass in Peoria IL?

In most cases, yes. Peoria winters are brutal and our summers are humid. Spray foam's air-sealing ability addresses both — it stops cold drafts in January and keeps moisture out in July. Most homeowners see enough savings on their utility bills to recover the cost difference within 5 to 8 years, and the comfort improvement is immediate.

Can I put spray foam on top of existing fiberglass insulation?

You can layer blown-in insulation over fiberglass batts in an attic floor, but spray foam is different — it bonds directly to the surface and works best applied to clean substrate. In most upgrades, we remove the old fiberglass from rim joists and crawl spaces before spraying, because leaving it in place defeats the purpose of the air seal.

Does fiberglass insulation cause moisture problems in Peoria basements?

Yes, this is one of the most common problems we see. Fiberglass batts in basement rim joists and crawl space walls trap moisture between the paper facing and the concrete. Over time that leads to mold, rot, and pest problems. Closed-cell spray foam is vapor-impermeable, so it eliminates that gap where moisture collects.

How long does fiberglass insulation last compared to spray foam?

Fiberglass batts can last 20 to 30 years if they stay dry, but they settle and lose R-value over time. They're also vulnerable to moisture and pests. Spray foam — both open and closed cell — is inert, won't settle, and doesn't absorb moisture. Properly installed spray foam will outlast the house.

What areas of my home benefit most from spray foam over fiberglass?

Rim joists, crawl spaces, attic knee walls, garage ceilings, and anywhere you have air movement or moisture risk. These are the spots where fiberglass consistently underperforms because it doesn't air-seal. Flat attic floors with no moisture issues are the one place fiberglass blown-in still makes economic sense.
