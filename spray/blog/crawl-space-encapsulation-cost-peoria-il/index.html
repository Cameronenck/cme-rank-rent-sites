Crawl Space Encapsulation Cost Peoria IL: 2026 Pricing Guide

Published 2026-06-19 · Peoria Spray Foam Pros

Home › Blog › Crawl Space Encapsulation Cost Peoria IL

A crawl space is the most expensive part of your home to ignore. Moisture creeps in from the ground, your HVAC system tries to condition an unsealed space, and the next thing you know you're paying to replace rotted band board, treat mold in the insulation, or rebuild compromised rim joists. Crawl space encapsulation stops this before it happens — but you want to know what it actually costs before you call for estimates.

We've encapsulated hundreds of crawl spaces across Peoria and the surrounding areas. Here's what the real cost looks like, what's included, and why this investment usually pays for itself.

What Does Crawl Space Encapsulation Cost in Peoria IL?

Crawl space encapsulation in Peoria typically runs $2,500 to $5,000 for an average 1,200 to 1,500 square foot crawl space, depending on existing conditions and what's included in the scope.

Here's the breakdown by project size and insulation type:

  • Small encapsulation (800–1,000 sq ft, open-cell or rigid board insulation): $1,800–$2,800
  • Medium encapsulation (1,200–1,500 sq ft, mixed insulation type): $2,500–$4,200
  • Large encapsulation (1,600–2,500 sq ft, closed-cell spray foam on perimeter): $4,000–$7,000
  • Complex encapsulation (structural damage, high moisture, sump system, dehumidification): $6,000–$9,000+

These are installed costs including labor, materials, and disposal of old insulation if needed.

What Gets Included in That Price?

This is where the confusion happens. Some contractors quote just vapor barrier and insulation. Others quote the complete system. Here's what a full encapsulation should include:

  1. Floor vapor barrier installation. Heavy-duty 6-mil polyethylene over the entire crawl space floor, sealing and taped at all seams. Cost: $500–$800 for a 1,200 sq ft space.
  2. Perimeter wall insulation. Closed-cell spray foam (2–3 inches) on rim joists and band boards, or rigid foam board as a more budget-conscious option. This is where the cost varies significantly. Closed-cell foam is $1,000–$1,800. Rigid board is $400–$700.
  3. Air sealing of all rim joists and band board. Every gap, crack, and hole gets sealed before insulation. This keeps conditioned air in and ground moisture out. Included in spray foam work, but sometimes overlooked with rigid board. Verify it's in the quote.
  4. Foundation crack repair. Any visible cracks in the foundation walls need to be sealed to prevent water intrusion. If you have minor cracks, $200–$500. Major cracks or evidence of active water: $1,000+.
  5. Sump pump installation (if needed). If water is pooling or seeping in the crawl space, a sump pump drains it before it becomes a problem. Basic sump pit and pump: $600–$1,200. Includes discharge to daylight or drainage away from the foundation.
  6. Dehumidification equipment. If the crawl space stays damp even after encapsulation, a dehumidifier removes moisture that condensation would trap. Compact crawl space dehumidifier: $800–$1,500 installed. Smart models with remote monitoring cost more.

A legitimate quote lists each of these items separately so you know what you're paying for. If the quote just says "encapsulation: $3,500," ask for details.

What Factors Drive the Cost Up?

Access difficulty. If your crawl space is tight, has low headroom, or requires special equipment to navigate, the labor time increases. Pricing up 15–25% is reasonable for difficult access.

Existing moisture and mold. If mold is present, it needs professional remediation before encapsulation. This adds $1,500–$4,000 depending on extent. Some contractors include this; many don't. Verify what's covered.

Structural damage. Rotted band board, compromised rim joists, or failing insulation might need repair or replacement before encapsulation. Foundation repairs typically add $2,000–$5,000+. This is a red flag — if your structure is already failing, encapsulation is step two, not step one.

Insulation choice. Closed-cell spray foam costs 40–60% more than rigid board or open-cell, but it provides a better air seal and moisture resistance. If your crawl space has a history of moisture, it's worth it. If it's dry, board insulation is sufficient.

Sump and drainage. If your lot has poor drainage or the water table is high, you might need a sump system, interior perimeter drain lines, or exterior grading work. This easily adds $2,000–$4,000 to the project.

What Questions to Ask Before You Get a Quote

Ask these and get everything in writing:

  • Is the vapor barrier included? What thickness and coverage?
  • What insulation are you recommending — open-cell, closed-cell, or rigid board — and why?
  • Will you air seal the rim joists before insulation, or just insulate over existing gaps?
  • Do you assess for mold before quoting, or is mold remediation handled separately?
  • Will a dehumidifier be installed, or is that optional?
  • If I need a sump pump, do you install it, or do I need a plumber?
  • What's your warranty on labor and materials, and for how long?
  • Can you provide references from three recent Peoria-area encapsulation jobs?

A qualified contractor answers all of these without vagueness. If you get evasive answers, call someone else.

Why the Investment Pays Back

Crawl space encapsulation saves you in three ways:

Energy savings. An unencapsulated crawl space is like leaving a basement door open all winter. Your HVAC has to condition that space. Encapsulation can reduce heating and cooling costs by 10–20%, depending on your current situation. For a typical Peoria home, that's $100–$300 per year in utility savings.

Structural protection. Moisture is the enemy of wood. Encapsulation prevents the rotting band board, moldy rim joists, and failed insulation that cost thousands to repair. One structural repair job easily costs more than a full encapsulation.

Health and air quality. Mold and moisture in the crawl space don't stay in the crawl space — they enter your home through the floor system. Encapsulation stops mold growth before it spreads, especially important if anyone in your home has allergies or respiratory issues.

Frequently Asked Questions

What exactly gets included in a crawl space encapsulation quote in Peoria?
A complete crawl space encapsulation includes: vapor barrier installation on the floor and walls, air sealing of all rim joists and band board areas, insulation of perimeter walls (typically spray foam or rigid board), foundation crack repair, sump pump installation if needed, and dehumidification equipment. Some contractors quote just the vapor barrier plus spray foam on walls. Others include everything. Get an itemized estimate so you know what you're actually paying for.
Is it worth spending more for closed-cell spray foam on crawl space walls instead of open-cell?
Yes, if your crawl space is damp or has a history of moisture. Closed-cell spray foam creates an air seal and is more resistant to moisture than open-cell. For a 2,000 sq ft crawl space in Peoria with existing moisture problems, the extra $1,500-$2,500 for closed-cell typically saves thousands later in mold remediation and structural repairs. If your crawl space stays dry year-round, open-cell or rigid board is often sufficient.
How long does crawl space encapsulation last in a Peoria home?
Properly installed spray foam and vapor barriers last 20-30+ years under normal conditions. Dehumidification equipment typically needs replacement every 5-10 years depending on use and humidity levels. The main wear item is the dehumidifier, not the insulation or barrier. This is a long-term investment that pays for itself through energy savings and avoided mold remediation.
Will encapsulation really lower my heating and cooling costs in Peoria?
Yes, but the savings depend on your current situation. If your crawl space is uninsulated and has significant air leakage, you'll see 10-20% savings on heating and cooling costs. A Peoria home spending $150/month on utilities might save $15-30 monthly. Over 10 years, that's $1,800-$3,600 in savings — enough to recover the encapsulation cost plus profit.
What's the difference between sealing and encapsulation?
Sealing is closing air leaks and cracks in the crawl space. Encapsulation is a complete system: vapor barrier on floors, spray foam or rigid insulation on walls, air sealing of rim joists, dehumidification, and sump management. Sealing alone doesn't fully protect from moisture. Encapsulation is the complete solution and costs more, but it actually prevents the moisture problems that sealing can miss.
