Energy Efficient Insulation in Peoria IL: Spray Foam Solutions for Lower Heating Bills

Published June 21, 2026 by Peoria Spray Foam Pros
Running the AC in summer? You'll notice the difference spray foam makes immediately. But it's your heating bills that'll really tell the story. In Peoria, where we go from 85°F in July straight to 10°F in January, picking the right insulation is the difference between a $150 heating bill and a $300 one. Here's what you actually need to know about energy efficient insulation—and why spray foam isn't just marketing hype.

Why Spray Foam Is the Most Energy Efficient Insulation

Let me be straight with you: I've installed batts, blown-in cellulose, and every other insulation type. Spray foam performs differently. It's not just better R-value on paper—it actually works.

When fiberglass batts go in, you get gaps around studs, settling over time, and heat bleeding through seams. You're insulating maybe 80% of the space. Spray foam fills every cavity completely—the attic corner nobody can reach, the rim joist, behind electrical outlets. It creates a continuous air barrier that fiberglass can't match.

Here's the real comparison:

Insulation Type R-Value/Inch Air Sealing Settles Over Time
Fiberglass Batt R-3.2 No (gaps remain) Yes (loses 10-15% effectiveness)
Open Cell Spray Foam R-3.6 to R-3.8 Yes (complete seal) No (stays constant 80+ years)
Closed Cell Spray Foam R-6.0 to R-7.0 Yes (complete seal) No (stays constant 80+ years)
Blown Cellulose R-3.5 Partial Yes (settles significantly)

In practice, that air sealing matters more than the R-value number. A study of real-world energy savings showed spray foam delivers 30-40% better performance than fiberglass on the same square footage. That's the difference between heating bills you can live with and ones that make you wince.

How Much Will Energy Efficient Insulation Actually Save You?

Let's talk money because that's what this is really about.

Average Peoria household heating costs run about $1,500-$2,000 per winter (December through March). Most homes I work on see a 15-30% reduction after spray foam. That's $225-$600 in annual savings, every single year.

A typical Peoria attic project—about 1,200-1,500 square feet—costs $2,500-$4,500 depending on access and existing insulation. At $350 in annual savings (conservative middle estimate), your payback is 7-12 years. After that, it's pure savings.

But here's what most homeowners miss: energy codes keep getting stricter. If your home was built before 2010, your insulation probably doesn't meet current code. When you sell, the inspection will catch that. You might have to bring it up to code anyway. Spray foam now means you're not scrambling to insulate before closing.

Why R-Value Alone Doesn't Tell the Story

R-value is important, but it's not everything. It's the R-value per inch in a lab-controlled test. Real homes aren't labs.

Fiberglass batts have an R-3.2 per inch rating. But your attic has gaps around ductwork, electrical runs, and the ridge vent. Heat escapes through those gaps. Your effective R-value in practice might be R-2.8 or lower. The batt has "settled" before winter even hits because the weight of dust and humidity pulls it down slightly.

Spray foam hits R-3.8 per inch and maintains it. No settling. No air paths around it. The effective performance is closer to its stated R-value—sometimes better once air sealing is factored in.

For Peoria specifically, current energy code recommends R-38 to R-49 in attics. That's 10-14 inches of fiberglass batts. With spray foam, you need about 10-13 inches of open cell to match. Sounds similar until you realize the spray foam does air sealing on top of the R-value. You're actually getting more than the number suggests.

Open Cell vs. Closed Cell: Which Is More Energy Efficient?

Both are more efficient than fiberglass. The choice depends on your situation.

Open Cell spray foam (R-3.6 to R-3.8 per inch) costs less—typically $0.55-$0.75 per board foot. It works great for attics and interior walls where moisture isn't a concern. It's also more forgiving if you make a mistake during installation. It cures faster and allows minor drying of incidental moisture.

Closed cell spray foam (R-6 to R-7 per inch) costs more—$1.00-$1.50 per board foot—but delivers nearly double the R-value. It's worth the extra cost for crawl spaces, basements, rim joists, and anywhere moisture is a risk. The higher density also adds structural strength to walls (some builders use it for wind bracing).

For most Peoria homes, I recommend open cell in the attic and closed cell in the basement or crawl space. You get efficient attic insulation at reasonable cost, plus bulletproof moisture protection below grade.

The Hidden Energy Losses That Insulation Alone Can't Fix

Here's something I see all the time: homeowners upgrade insulation but don't fix air leaks. Then they're surprised the savings are smaller than expected.

Spray foam actually solves this because the foam seals as it goes. But if you're adding fiberglass batts, you need to separately air-seal:

  • Attic bypasses (gaps where warm air escapes from the conditioned space)
  • Electrical outlets and switches in exterior walls
  • Ductwork seams and penetrations
  • Rim joists (the area where the house foundation meets the framing)
  • Gaps around pipes and wiring

Spray foam fills most of these automatically. It's another reason the energy savings are usually higher than you'd expect from insulation alone.

How Spray Foam Improves Year-Round Comfort (Beyond Just Energy Savings)

Energy bills are the obvious benefit. But there's more.

Summer cooling: Spray foam stops heat from radiating into your attic. An uninsulated attic in July can hit 150°F+. That heat radiates down into the living space below. Spray foam keeps it out.

Comfort consistency: Fiberglass doesn't stop drafts. Cold exterior walls will feel cold in winter even if the insulation has good R-value. Spray foam's air seal means no cold spots. Temperature is uniform across the room.

Moisture control: In Peoria's humid summers and cold winters, moisture is a real risk. Fiberglass absorbs it. Spray foam doesn't. It keeps moisture from condensing in the building envelope, which prevents mold and wood rot.

Noise reduction: Spray foam dampens outside noise. If you're near a road or railway, you'll notice the difference immediately. Your bedroom stays quieter.

FAQ: Energy Efficient Insulation for Peoria Homes

What R-value do I actually need in Peoria?

Current Illinois energy code calls for R-38 to R-49 in attics (depending on whether you're doing a full retrofit vs. new construction). Walls should be R-13 minimum. Basements R-10 to R-13 on the walls, R-10 under the slab. These aren't suggestions—they're what lenders and inspectors now require. Spray foam makes hitting these easy because you're getting air sealing on top of the R-value.

Is spray foam insulation eco-friendly?

The manufacturing has environmental cost, like everything. But the energy savings over 80 years—the lifespan of spray foam—typically offset the manufacturing footprint within 2-3 years. Fiberglass saves energy too, but it loses effectiveness as it settles, so you're not actually getting those savings long-term. On a 50-year lifecycle, spray foam usually wins on the environmental calculation.

Can I DIY spray foam insulation to save money?

No. Spray foam requires specific equipment, training, and PPE. Bad application leads to incomplete fill, off-gassing issues, and structural problems if it expands wrong. A contractor mistake is fixable. A DIY mistake could damage your home. This is one you should outsource. The cost difference between DIY and professional isn't actually that large once you factor in equipment rental and waste.

Will spray foam insulation reduce my heating bills the first winter?

Yes, usually noticeably. Most homeowners see the drop on their first December-January utility bill. If the project was done in early fall, you might see it sooner. The efficiency gain is immediate. The money shows up on the bill within weeks.

How do I know if my home needs energy efficient insulation?

If your home was built before 2010, it probably needs it. Simple test: touch your exterior walls in winter. Cold? You need more insulation. Check your attic—if you can see the rafters through the insulation, you're below code. If heating bills are above $150/month in winter, insulation upgrade will probably pay for itself.
