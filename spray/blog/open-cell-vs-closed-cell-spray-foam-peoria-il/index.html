Open Cell vs Closed Cell Spray Foam

Which Type Should You Choose for Your Peoria Home?

Published: June 17, 2026 Reading time: 5 min Local Peoria Contractor

I've been installing spray foam insulation in Peoria and Central Illinois for twelve years, and this is the question I hear more than any other: "Should I use open cell or closed cell spray foam?" The answer isn't one-size-fits-all—it depends on your specific situation. Let me break down what I've learned from thousands of installations, so you can make the right choice for your property.

The Basic Difference

Here's the simplest way to think about it: Both types of spray foam start as a liquid that expands and hardens. The difference is in how their cells are structured.

Open Cell Spray Foam

Open cell foam has cells that aren't completely sealed—they're open to each other, like a sponge. This makes it lighter, softer, and less dense. It expands more aggressively during application, which is why it fills gaps so effectively. The downside is that it's more permeable to moisture and offers a lower R-value per inch.

Closed Cell Spray Foam

Closed cell foam has cells that are completely sealed off from each other. This creates a rigid, dense, moisture-resistant barrier. It's heavier, more expensive, but delivers superior insulation performance and acts as a vapor barrier all by itself.

When Open Cell Is the Right Choice

I recommend open cell spray foam for:

  • Interior attic insulation where moisture isn't a concern and budget matters
  • Basement walls that are already dry and need acoustic benefits
  • Interior stud cavities in above-grade walls
  • Sound control projects—open cell absorbs sound better than closed cell
  • Budget-conscious jobs where maximum coverage area matters more than R-value per inch

Real Example: A Peoria homeowner wanted to insulate their second-floor attic with a $2,000 budget. I used open cell to cover 1,200 sq ft with 5.5 inches (delivering R-19). Closed cell would have only covered 700 sq ft. The open cell choice was perfect for their budget and their attic's low-moisture environment.

When Closed Cell Is Worth the Investment

I always recommend closed cell spray foam for:

  • Crawl spaces—Peoria's clay soil means ground moisture is constant. Closed cell is a true vapor barrier
  • Below-grade applications like basement rim joists and foundation walls
  • Attics in humid climates or homes with moisture history
  • Roofs and exterior applications where weather exposure requires moisture protection
  • Areas needing structural support—closed cell adds rigidity
  • Maximum R-value in minimum space—when depth is limited, closed cell delivers

Real Example: I worked with a homeowner in East Peoria who had mold growth in their crawl space. The previous contractor used fiberglass—moisture just soaked through. I removed it all and installed closed cell spray foam. Five years later, zero moisture issues. The vapor barrier built into the foam changed everything.

The Cost Reality for Peoria Homeowners

For a typical 1,500 sq ft attic or crawl space application:

  • Open cell (4 inches, R-14): $660-$975 total
  • Closed cell (2 inches, R-12-14): $1,500-$2,250 total

Yes, closed cell costs more upfront. But here's what I tell my customers: Peoria winters are cold. A properly insulated home with closed cell foam can save $1,000+ annually on heating compared to old fiberglass. The extra upfront cost often pays for itself in 18-24 months through energy savings.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use open cell in my crawl space?
Technically you can, but I don't recommend it in Peoria. Our clay-heavy soil means crawl spaces are naturally damp. Open cell will absorb moisture over time, defeating the purpose. Closed cell is the safer choice for below-grade applications here.
How long does spray foam last?
Both open and closed cell last 80+ years when properly installed and protected from UV light. They don't settle, compress, or degrade like fiberglass. Once it's installed, you're done—no maintenance required.
Will spray foam damage my roof?
Not at all. When applied correctly, spray foam actually protects your roof by sealing air leaks and preventing ice dams. It reduces the temperature swings that damage shingles over time. Your roof will thank you.
Is spray foam more expensive than fiberglass?
Upfront, yes. But spray foam seals every gap and crack—no thermal bridging. Fiberglass leaves 50+ tiny air leaks in an average wall. Over 10 years, your energy bill difference more than covers the spray foam premium.
Can I install spray foam myself?
You can buy the kits, but I wouldn't recommend it unless you have training. Spray foam requires precise temperature, humidity, and application technique. A bad DIY installation is expensive to remove and fix. Professional installation is worth it.
Which type do contractors prefer?
Honest answer? Closed cell is easier to apply and forgives more mistakes. Open cell requires more precision and attention to moisture management. But the right choice always depends on your specific application, not what's easier for me.

My Bottom Line for Peoria Homeowners

If you're insulating a crawl space, rim joist, or any area with moisture risk—invest in closed cell. The vapor barrier it provides is worth every penny in our climate. If you're doing a dry attic on a tight budget, open cell gets the job done and saves you money without sacrificing performance.

The worst choice? Doing nothing. Peoria winters demand proper insulation. Whether you choose open or closed cell, upgrading from old fiberglass or missing insulation will pay for itself through lower energy bills and a more comfortable home.
