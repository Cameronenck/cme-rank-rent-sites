Open Cell vs Closed Cell Spray Foam Peoria IL — Which One Do You Actually Need?

May 14, 2026 Comparison Guide Spray Foam

When I give estimates in Peoria, this is one of the most common questions I field: should I use open cell or closed cell spray foam? People have done some reading online, they've gotten a few quotes, and the contractor they're talking to is recommending one or the other without really explaining why. I'm going to break it down the way I explain it in someone's driveway — no jargon, just the practical difference and where each one belongs.

The short version: both are spray foam, but they behave completely differently on the job. Open cell is softer, cheaper, and expands dramatically. Closed cell is rigid, more expensive, and acts as a moisture barrier on top of an insulator. Choosing the wrong one for the wrong application wastes money at best and causes real moisture or structural problems at worst.

What Is Open Cell Spray Foam?

Open cell spray foam gets its name from the microscopic cell structure — the cells are intentionally left "open," which makes the cured foam soft and flexible, almost like rigid foam rubber. It expands aggressively during application, sometimes 100 times its liquid volume. That expansion is what makes it great for irregularly shaped spaces and for sealing complex air bypass points.

The R-value for open cell runs R-3.5 to R-3.7 per inch. To hit the DOE-recommended R-49 for an attic in Peoria (we're in Climate Zone 5), you'd need roughly 13–14 inches of open cell foam. In practice, most contractors apply open cell to the underside of the roof deck at 5–6 inches to create a conditioned attic — the rest of the R-value credit comes from the thermal mass and air sealing effect.

One important thing to know: open cell foam is vapor-permeable. Moisture can pass through it over time. That's fine in most above-grade applications, but it disqualifies open cell for anywhere that sees significant ground moisture or bulk water exposure.

What Is Closed Cell Spray Foam?

Closed cell foam has a rigid, dense structure because all the cells are sealed. It expands much less during application — typically 30–40 times its liquid volume — which means you need more product to fill a space, which is part of why it costs more. The payoff is a dramatically higher R-value and an integrated vapor barrier.

Closed cell delivers R-6.0 to R-6.5 per inch. That means 2 inches of closed cell on a crawl space wall gives you R-12 to R-13 — plus you're blocking vapor migration from the ground. That's a combination open cell simply cannot provide.

Closed cell also adds structural rigidity. When applied to wall cavities or rim joists, it stiffens the framing members noticeably. That's not the primary reason to choose it, but it's a genuine side benefit in older Peoria homes where framing has loosened over decades.

Where to Use Each Type in a Peoria Home

Attic Roof Deck — Open Cell Wins

For most Peoria attics, open cell spray foam applied to the underside of the roof deck is the cost-effective choice. You're creating a conditioned attic — bringing your HVAC equipment and ductwork inside the thermal envelope. Open cell's aggressive expansion means it gets into every rafter bay completely, and you don't need the vapor barrier function up here. A 5.5-inch application runs $2,100–$3,300 for a typical 1,200 square foot attic, compared to $3,600–$5,400 for closed cell at equivalent coverage.

The exception: if your roof deck has a known moisture infiltration issue or if the slope is too shallow to allow enough foam thickness, closed cell is the better call. Talk to your contractor about the specifics.

Crawl Spaces — Closed Cell Wins

Peoria crawl spaces are notoriously damp. Between the Illinois summer humidity and the proximity to the water table in lower-lying neighborhoods, putting open cell foam in a crawl space is asking for trouble. Open cell will absorb ground moisture over time, lose R-value, and potentially harbor mold. Closed cell on crawl space walls and floor rim joists is standard practice for a reason — the vapor barrier function is essential.

We typically apply 2–3 inches of closed cell to crawl space walls and 2 inches minimum on rim joists. That's enough to get above the vapor barrier threshold and deliver meaningful R-value. See our post on crawl space insulation in Peoria for full guidance on what a proper crawl space job looks like.

Basement Walls — Closed Cell Wins

Same reasoning as crawl spaces. Below-grade walls are in contact with soil and are subject to vapor drive pushing inward. Closed cell blocks that vapor transmission. We apply it directly to the poured concrete or block wall before any framing or finish work goes in. Two inches of closed cell gives you R-12 and a proper moisture barrier — a combination no other insulation product can match in that application.

Exterior Walls (New Construction or Gut Remodel)

Both foam types work in wall cavities, and this is where budget often makes the decision. Open cell completely fills a 3.5-inch 2x4 stud bay at R-13 and provides excellent air sealing. Closed cell in the same bay delivers R-21–22 but at roughly twice the material cost. For new construction here in Peoria, we see a mix — budget-conscious builders go open cell, performance-focused builders go closed cell, and some do a hybrid: 1 inch of closed cell for vapor control plus open cell to fill the remainder.

Peoria climate note: We're in a mixed-humid climate zone (Zone 5) with cold winters and hot, muggy summers. Vapor drive reverses seasonally — it pushes inward in summer and outward in winter. That bidirectional vapor drive is part of why the vapor barrier properties of closed cell matter in below-grade and crawl space applications here specifically.

Mixing Both in the Same House

This is more common than people think, and it makes a lot of sense. The most efficient approach for a Peoria home we see regularly is open cell on the attic roof deck for the conditioned attic, closed cell on the crawl space walls and rim joists for moisture control, and open cell in any wall cavities being insulated. You're using each product where its strengths are relevant and not overpaying for closed cell performance you don't need in low-moisture zones.

There is no compatibility issue between the two products. They simply serve different roles in the same building envelope.

The Cost Conversation — What's Actually Worth It?

Closed cell costs roughly 60–80% more per square foot than open cell, and that price difference adds up fast on larger jobs. Here's how I frame it for homeowners: pay for closed cell where moisture control is non-negotiable, use open cell everywhere else.

  • Crawl space walls and rim joists: Always closed cell. The vapor barrier function is not optional in a Peoria crawl space.
  • Basement walls: Always closed cell below grade. Above the sill plate, open cell is fine.
  • Attic roof deck: Open cell is usually the right value. Closed cell if there are moisture problems or if the rafters are shallow.
  • Interior wall cavities: Open cell covers most needs at a lower price point.

If a contractor is quoting you closed cell across your entire attic without explaining why your specific attic warrants it, ask them to justify the recommendation. Sometimes closed cell is the right call — but it should be explained, not just assumed.

Frequently Asked Questions — Open Cell vs Closed Cell Spray Foam in Peoria IL

What is the difference between open cell and closed cell spray foam?
Open cell spray foam has a softer, spongy texture and an R-value of about 3.5–3.7 per inch. It is vapor-permeable and expands significantly during application. Closed cell spray foam is rigid and dense, delivering R-6 to R-6.5 per inch. It acts as both an insulator and a vapor barrier, making it ideal for moisture-prone areas and below-grade applications.
Which spray foam is better for attics in Peoria IL?
For most Peoria attics, open cell spray foam is the better value. Applied to the underside of the roof deck, it creates a conditioned attic at a lower cost per square foot than closed cell. You typically need 5–6 inches to hit the recommended R-value. Closed cell is the stronger choice if you have moisture infiltration through the roof deck or if your attic has a very low slope that limits foam thickness.
Which spray foam is better for crawl spaces in Peoria IL?
Closed cell spray foam is almost always the right call for crawl spaces in Peoria. Crawl spaces deal with ground moisture, occasional flooding, and high humidity in summer months. Closed cell's vapor barrier properties prevent moisture from migrating through the foam into your floor system, which open cell cannot do. For below-grade applications, closed cell is the standard.
How much does open cell spray foam cost vs closed cell in Peoria IL?
In Peoria, open cell spray foam typically runs $1.75–$2.75 per square foot installed, while closed cell ranges from $3.00–$4.50 per square foot. For a 1,200 square foot attic application, expect $2,100–$3,300 for open cell versus $3,600–$5,400 for closed cell. The higher cost of closed cell is justified in applications where moisture control is critical.
Can you mix open cell and closed cell spray foam in the same house?
Yes, and we do this regularly in Peoria homes. A common approach is open cell on the attic roof deck for conditioned attic space, and closed cell on the crawl space walls and floor rim joists. Each foam type is used where its specific strengths matter most. There is no compatibility issue — they are just different applications within the same structure.
