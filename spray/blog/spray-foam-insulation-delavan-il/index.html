Why Delavan IL Homes Lose So Much Heat

Most of the housing stock in Delavan was built between the 1940s and the 1980s. That era of home construction prioritized affordability over energy performance — insulation code didn't catch up to modern standards until the late 1990s and early 2000s, and even then, code minimums are still just minimums. Fiberglass batts were the standard product for decades, and while they do slow heat conduction, they have one critical weakness: they don't stop air movement.

Air infiltration — the movement of outside air through gaps, cracks, and building penetrations — accounts for 25 to 40 percent of the energy loss in a typical older Central Illinois home. Insulation that doesn't air-seal leaves the biggest source of heat loss completely unaddressed. Spray foam is one of the only insulation products that seals and insulates in the same step, which is why it consistently delivers energy savings that blown insulation or batt upgrades alone can't match.

In Delavan specifically, the main problem areas I see repeatedly are: uninsulated or poorly sealed rim joists at the foundation level, vented crawl spaces pulling cold and moist air into the floor system, and attic bypasses around recessed lights and top plates that let heated air escape before it ever does its job. Fixing those three things is often enough to take a drafty, expensive-to-heat Delavan home and turn it into something that feels completely different in January.

Best Spray Foam Applications for Delavan IL Homes

Every home is different. But after doing this work across Tazewell and Peoria County for years, these are the applications that make the most consistent impact for Delavan homeowners:

  • Rim joist insulation. This is the highest-return spray foam project for most Central Illinois homes, and Delavan homes are no exception. The rim joist is the band of framing that runs around the perimeter of your foundation at floor level — it's almost always uninsulated or poorly insulated, and it's in direct contact with cold outdoor air all winter. Two inches of closed-cell spray foam seals every gap and brings that section up to R-12 or better. Most Delavan homeowners notice the difference in floor warmth after the first heating season.
  • Crawl space encapsulation. A lot of the older homes in Delavan have vented crawl spaces — a design approach that building science has thoroughly discredited. Vents allow cold, moist outside air into the crawl space all winter, which drives up heating costs and creates the conditions for mold and structural wood rot over time. We close the vents and apply closed-cell spray foam to the crawl space walls, converting it to a conditioned space that stays dry and at a moderate temperature year-round. The floor temperature improvement upstairs is noticeable within days.
  • Attic air sealing. Many Delavan homes have adequate blown insulation depth in the attic, but underneath all that loose fill, the ceiling plane has never been properly sealed. Every recessed light, every plumbing penetration, every top plate gap is a highway for heated air to escape. We move the existing insulation, seal every penetration with spray foam, and reinstall — or we supplement with additional blown insulation on top. This targeted air sealing often produces more energy savings than simply adding more insulation depth ever could.
  • Basement walls. For homes with poured concrete or block basements, spray foam on the interior walls stops the cold from radiating inward all winter and controls moisture vapor before it can condense on cold surfaces. A conditioned basement is also significantly more comfortable as living space and helps protect mechanicals — furnace, water heater, ductwork — from temperature extremes that shorten their service life.
  • Full open-cell attic spray. In homes with complex roof lines or vaulted ceilings where blown insulation isn't an option, a full open-cell spray foam application on the underside of the roof deck creates a conditioned attic. This keeps ductwork in a conditioned space, eliminates ice dam risk, and in older Delavan homes with cathedral ceilings, is often the only way to achieve code-level R-values in a tight space.

Open-Cell vs. Closed-Cell Spray Foam for Delavan IL

The most common question I get on estimates is which type of foam to use. Here's the plain-language answer for Central Illinois conditions:

Closed-cell spray foam runs R-6 to R-7 per inch, is rigid enough to add structural value to a wall or roof assembly, and acts as a Class II vapor retarder. That makes it the right call anywhere moisture management matters — rim joists, crawl space walls, basement walls, and cathedral ceiling assemblies where the rafter space is limited and every inch of R-value counts. It's more expensive per board foot than open-cell, but the density and vapor control are non-negotiable in those applications.

Open-cell spray foam runs about R-3.7 per inch, is vapor-permeable, and costs significantly less per square foot. It's the right product for full attic fills and interior wall cavities in conditioned spaces. The vapor permeability is actually a feature in attic applications — it lets the assembly dry to the interior if moisture ever gets in, rather than trapping it.

Most Delavan homes I work on end up with both types: closed-cell in the crawl space and rim joist, open-cell in the attic or walls. If you want to get into the technical details before we meet on-site, our open-cell vs. closed-cell foam guide walks through every scenario in detail.

What Happens During a Spray Foam Job in Delavan

For a focused application — rim joist, crawl space, or targeted attic air sealing — expect the crew to be on site for two to four hours. A full attic spray or basement wall project takes a full day. Everything is mixed and applied on-site from our rig; there's no pre-manufactured product that gets cut to fit. The foam expands on contact and cures within a few hours.

You'll need to stay out of the area being treated while we're spraying and for about 24 hours after application while the foam fully cures — that's standard with any spray foam chemistry, not anything specific to our product. We trim any overspray, clean up, and leave the space ready to use. If drywall or finish work needs to happen after the foam cures, we'll tell you exactly when it's clear to proceed.

We pull permits when code requires them. If you've had a previous contractor tell you that spray foam doesn't need permits, ask them to put that in writing — most significant insulation upgrades do require a building permit in Tazewell County, and skipping that step can create real headaches if you sell the property later.
